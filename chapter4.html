Chapter 4 - Investor Profile

This lesson covers everything in depth - net worth and cash flow analysis, personal factors, government and employer pensions, every registered plan, and risk tolerance vs capacity. Take your time and work through it like a textbook chapter.

👤 What is the investor profile?

The investor profile is a comprehensive picture of a client's financial situation, personal factors, needs, and risk tolerance. It is the foundation of every recommendation - without a complete profile, no recommendation can be considered suitable.

Key documents reviewed: Income tax return (NOA), RRSP and RRIF statements, non-registered investment statements, pension plan statements, bank statements, life insurance policies, mortgage and credit card statements.

💵 Net worth and cash flow statements

Net worth statement: lists assets at market value, subtracts liabilities. Net worth = Total assets − Total liabilities. A positive net worth shows progress; a negative net worth signals a problem to address.

Cash flow statement: lists net income (inflow) against expenses (outflow) over a period. A positive number means money is available for saving/investing; a negative number signals an unsustainable position requiring debt management or expense reduction.

Good debt vs bad debt: Good debt builds assets (e.g. a mortgage). Bad debt depletes resources (e.g. credit card debt from discretionary spending). Both must be repaid and both appear on the liabilities review - but they reflect very differently on overall financial health.

📋 Reviewing assets and liabilities in depth

Key documents include the income tax return/NOA (shows taxable income, deductions, RRSP room, TFSA room), RRSP/non-registered statements (show how money is invested, risk exposure, locked-in status), pension plan member statements, bank statements, and life insurance cash values.

Key liability documents include the mortgage statement (principal owed, rate, term), line of credit/HELOC statement, and credit card statements.

Reverse mortgage note: a reverse mortgage pays out home equity to the owner, but is a liability that must be repaid on sale or death - reducing what inheritors ultimately receive from the property.

🧠 Personal factors: values, health, and financial literacy

Personal values shape investment choices - some prioritize education savings, charitable giving, or socially responsible investing over pure retirement accumulation.

Health concerns include the risk of needing long-term care (which can deplete an estate), disability (managed via disability income insurance), and declining mental capacity with age - the agent must be alert to signs that a client's decision-making is becoming impaired.

Financial literacy = knowledge + skills + confidence to make responsible financial decisions. Never assume what a client knows. The standard 3-question test covers compounding, inflation, and diversification - an agent can use these to gauge understanding before making recommendations, and must avoid jargon (e.g. say "marginal tax rate," not "MTR").

⚖️ Legal considerations

Family law: spouses generally have a legal duty to equalize assets (including investments) upon separation/divorce - rules vary by jurisdiction (Québec treats common-law differently).

Will: review confirms bequests are backed by adequate resources (e.g. life insurance). A surviving spouse can generally inherit on a tax-deferred rollover basis.

Power of attorney (POA) for property: nominates an "attorney" to make decisions when the grantor cannot act for himself. Agents should DECLINE being named an attorney and must watch for signs of POA fraud/financial exploitation - a real and serious risk, especially with older or vulnerable clients.

🎯 The full list of investor needs

Needs supersede wants - they're the investor's real motivation for investing. Key categories: income, retirement income, tax efficiency, emergency fund, potential creditor protection, liquidity, estate planning, diversification, investment management, and suitability.

Creditor protection caution: it should be an incidental benefit of a soundly-chosen investment, never the SOLE reason for choosing it. If bankruptcy proceedings reveal an investment was chosen specifically to evade creditors, it can be seized despite normally offering protection.

💰 Income splitting and tax efficiency

Three main income-splitting strategies for couples: (1) spousal/common-law RRSP - higher earner contributes for the lower-earning spouse; (2) splitting up to 50% of eligible pension income (annuity, RPP, RRIF, DPSP annuity) for those 65+; (3) assigning CPP/QPP benefits between spouses.

OAS clawback bonus: reducing net income via income splitting can also reduce or eliminate the OAS recovery tax (clawback), which is 15% of net income above the annual threshold.

🏛️ Government retirement pensions

🏢 Employer pension plans (RPPs)

DBPP (Defined Benefit) - pension amount known in advance via formula (final average earnings, career average, or flat benefit); employer bears investment risk; "gold standard" but increasingly rare. Spouse gets at least 60% on death.

DCPP (Defined Contribution / Money Purchase Plan) - pension depends entirely on contributions + investment performance; employee bears investment risk and typically makes investment choices; must transfer out at retirement (cannot stay with employer).

PRPP (Pooled Registered Pension Plan, called VRSP in Québec) - similar to DCPP but employer contributions not mandatory; designed as a low-cost option for those without access to an RPP.

Vesting: the point employer contributions become owned by the employee. Before vesting, a departing employee may only keep their own contributions. All RPP savings (DBPP, DCPP, PRPP) are LOCKED-IN.

🔒 Locked-in accounts

Two phases: savings (LIRA / Locked-in RRSP) and income (LIF, LRIF, PRRIF for Saskatchewan/Manitoba, or RLIF for federally regulated plans). The savings-to-income conversion must happen by the end of the year the owner turns 71.

Withdrawal limits: LIF/LRIF/RLIF have both a minimum AND a maximum annual withdrawal. The PRRIF is the exception - it has no maximum, offering more flexibility. Funds can only be unlocked in limited cases: financial hardship, shortened life expectancy (≤2 years), or a very low balance.

💼 Employer savings plans: DPSP and GRRSP

DPSP (Deferred Profit Sharing Plan) - employer-only contributions sharing company profits; NOT locked-in; vests within max 2 years; creates a pension adjustment reducing RRSP room; significant shareholders and close family are not eligible.

GRRSP (Group RRSP) - identical to a personal RRSP but offered through an employer, often with lower fees; NOT locked-in; uses the employee's own RRSP contribution room (employer contributions are taxable to the employee).

📋 RRSP deep dive

Annual contribution limit = lesser of 18% of prior year's earned income, or the annual maximum dollar limit - reduced by any pension adjustment (PA). Unused room carries forward indefinitely. A $2,000 lifetime over-contribution cushion is allowed (not deductible); beyond that, a 1% monthly penalty applies.

Spousal RRSP: higher-earning spouse contributes to a plan for the lower-earning spouse, using the CONTRIBUTOR's room. If the receiving spouse withdraws within the year of contribution or the following 2 calendar years, the withdrawal (up to the contributed amount) is attributed back to the contributor's income - only the growth portion escapes attribution.

At maturity (end of year owner turns 71): must convert to a RRIF, term annuity to 90 (T-90), life annuity, or a combination - or cash out and pay tax immediately. A qualified beneficiary (spouse, or disabled dependent child/grandchild) continues the tax deferral; anyone else triggers full taxation to the estate.

📉 RRIF withdrawals

Mandatory minimum annual withdrawal, based on age (and rises steeply with age - from 4.00% at 65 to 20.00% at 95+). No withholding tax on the minimum; withholding tax applies only to amounts withdrawn ABOVE the minimum. Withdrawals can be based on a younger spouse's age to reduce the minimum percentage and stretch the RRIF longer.

All RRIF withdrawals are fully taxable as ordinary income regardless of how the underlying investments earned their return (capital gains, dividends, interest) - there's no tax advantage to holding equities specifically inside an RRSP/RRIF for this reason (though equities may still offer better growth potential).

Death of RRIF owner: a successor annuitant (must be a spouse/common-law partner) can continue receiving payments with no tax to the estate. A named beneficiary avoids probate but the RRIF value is still taxed on the deceased's final return (unless it's a qualified beneficiary continuing deferral). No beneficiary named = value falls into the estate, subject to probate.

🎁 TFSA essentials

Both growth AND withdrawals are completely tax-free - the opposite tax treatment of an RRSP. Contributions are NOT tax-deductible. Contribution room is NOT based on earned income (unlike an RRSP), so even someone with no income can contribute if they have room.

Critical re-contribution rule: a withdrawal can be re-contributed, but only starting the FOLLOWING calendar year (plus that year's new room) - contributing back in the same year as a withdrawal risks an over-contribution penalty (1%/month). TFSA income/withdrawals do NOT affect OAS or GIS eligibility.

🎓 RESP essentials

Subscriber = person who opens/contributes; Beneficiary = the (usually) child who receives payments. Lifetime contribution limit: $50,000 per beneficiary (applies across ALL RESPs combined for that beneficiary). Contributions are not deductible; growth is tax-deferred.

CESG: government grant matching 20% of contributions, max $500/year (basic), with additional top-ups available for lower/middle-income families. Unused grant room carries forward until the year the beneficiary turns 17. Withdrawals are called Educational Assistance Payments (EAPs), taxed to the student beneficiary - usually minimal tax given typically low student income.

♿ RDSP essentials

For beneficiaries eligible for the disability tax credit, under 60, Canadian resident. Lifetime private contribution limit: $200,000 (no annual cap). Canada Disability Savings Grant matches up to 300% of contributions (income-dependent); Canada Disability Savings Bond is paid even with no contributions, for low/modest-income beneficiaries. Grants/bonds paid until the year the beneficiary turns 49; unused entitlements carry forward up to 10 years.

10-year holdback rule: if a withdrawal is made, grants/bonds received in the PRECEDING 10 years may need to be repaid to the government. DAP = single payment (only if private contributions exceed government contributions). LDAP = recurring payments, must begin by age 60.

⚖️ Risk tolerance vs. risk capacity

Risk tolerance - the emotional/subjective willingness to accept losses. Investors consistently OVERESTIMATE their own tolerance, and may say one thing while doing another (panic-selling in a downturn despite claiming high tolerance).

Risk capacity - the objective, financial ability to absorb losses without harming lifestyle or goals. A high-net-worth investor can have LOW risk tolerance despite high capacity; a confident investor can have HIGH stated tolerance despite genuinely LOW capacity (e.g. limited savings near retirement).

Key principles: assess tolerance and capacity SEPARATELY - never let one influence the other. When they conflict, lean toward the more conservative assessment. Shorter time horizons generally mean lower appropriate risk tolerance, since there's less time to recover from losses. The connection between life-risk choices (e.g. extreme sports) and financial risk tolerance is NOT well established - don't assume one predicts the other.
