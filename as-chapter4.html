Chapter 4 — Insurance to Protect Savings

Health care expenses — prescription drugs, dental care, vision care, and emergency medical costs — can significantly drain cash flow and savings. Extended health insurance helps fund these expenses, protecting both emergency funds and retirement savings. This chapter covers individual and group extended health coverage, including medical care, dental care, travel insurance, and how to integrate both types of coverage.

🔍 Sources of extended health coverage

The two primary sources of extended health insurance are individual extended health insurance and group extended health coverage. Banks and other financial institutions may offer limited extended health coverage to credit customers, but this is too limited and not widespread enough to be addressed in detail.

Individual extended health insurance: purchased directly from a representative of the issuing insurance company. The plan is owned by, paid for by, and usually payable to the applicant. Terms can be fully customized. The insured can terminate the contract at any time. Used to "top up" provincial health care plans by those without group coverage, or to enhance existing group coverage. Subject to full medical underwriting prior to issue (except travel insurance, which is underwritten at time of claim).
Group extended health coverage: by far the most common source of extended health coverage beyond provincial plans — typically provided by an employer. Group plans cover individuals with a common factor (employment, occupation, association membership). Plans usually offer a package of benefits with some options such as family coverage.

🏥 Individual extended health coverage: medical care, dental care, deductibles

Types of individual extended health coverage: medical care; dental care; and travel insurance. These may be combined in one contract or acquired separately. One key advantage of individual coverage over group coverage: the insured can pick and choose what types and levels of coverage to include, selecting deductible and co-insurance levels and annual maximums to suit their needs and budget.
Medical care coverage: for those not yet covered by provincial plans (e.g., new arrivals during the waiting period), individual medical insurance provides protection for regular and emergency care. For most Canadians, it fills gaps between personal needs and provincial plan coverage (e.g., chiropractic services). Types of coverage available: extended health care (hospital or home care services not covered provincially); prescription drugs; AD&D; dental care; vision care; emergency travel medical services.
Dental care coverage — routine maintenance: regular check-ups, x-rays, cleaning, fillings, and extractions. Covered to an annual maximum (e.g., $1,000), subject to the basic co-insurance factor (typically 20–25% paid by the insured). Benefits based on the provincial dental association fee schedule — dentist charges above the schedule are not covered. For extensive dental care ($500+), the insurer typically requires a predetermination of coverage (advance estimate) before treatment begins.
Dental care — major restorative services: crowns and inlays are subject to a lower co-insurance factor, generally 50% (the insured pays 50% of the cost).
Deductibles: a dollar amount of otherwise qualifying expenses the insured must pay 100% before the plan picks up any portion. Applied annually, starting anew each year. Example: $100 annual dental deductible — José must pay the first $100 of dental expenses each year with no reimbursement. Deductibles are much rarer in individual contracts than in group plans.
Co-insurance factor: the percentage of a qualifying expense the plan will cover (by default, the insured pays the remainder). Example: Consuela's 80% co-insurance factor for dental — the plan pays 80% of each claim and she pays 20%. Deductibles and co-insurance serve two purposes: (1) shift some cost burden to the insured (reducing premiums); (2) discourage undiscerning use of services and unnecessary claims.

✈️ Travel insurance: coverage, pre-existing conditions, premiums

What travel insurance covers: an umbrella name for a wide variety of coverages including: emergency medical expenses; lost or damaged luggage; trip cancellation; return of an ill or injured traveller to their home province. The most comprehensive coverage is obtained by purchasing a customized contract directly from an insurer. Specific coverages: doctors' and nurses' fees; hospitalization costs; medical equipment; ambulance services; dental costs for accidental injury; return of traveller, deceased's remains, and even the insured's vehicle to province of residence; lost baggage; trip interruption or cancellation due to illness.
Credit card travel insurance: often more limited than insurer-provided coverage; offered automatically with card issuance or as an option. No medical evidence required at issuance — underwriting happens at time of claim. Of particular concern: pre-existing conditions (up to 6 months prior to departure) may void the claim. This leaves the insured with uncertainty about whether a claim will be honoured.
Pre-existing conditions and travel insurance: since coverage is issued with little or no upfront medical underwriting, underwriting occurs at claim time. A claim may be denied if the expense resulted from a pre-existing condition — generally defined as a condition arising within 90 to 180 days before departure where the insured: saw a doctor for specific symptoms; underwent tests; had treatment; had a prescription changed. Even failing to disclose a daily baby aspirin for stroke/heart attack prevention could be considered non-disclosure. Chronic conditions (e.g., diabetes diagnosed before the 90–180 day window) may also restrict coverage if the insured was not symptom-free under physician care for up to one year before departure. The existence of a pre-existing condition does not void ALL coverage — only claims specifically related to that condition are denied (Antonio's epilepsy example: his auto accident injuries would be covered, but epileptic seizure treatment would not).
Travel insurance benefits: often paid on a reimbursement basis. The insured must contact the insurer immediately upon hospital admission and prior to surgery — failure to do so may reduce or deny benefits. Standard exclusions apply (attempted suicide, self-inflicted injuries, war, normal pregnancy, drug/alcohol abuse) PLUS travel-specific exclusions: hazardous activities (hang gliding, parasailing, scuba diving); travelling to countries on a government watch list; medical treatment in non-emergency situations.
Travel insurance premium factors: (1) Amount of coverage and risks covered — more coverage = higher premium; trip cancellation adds to cost. (2) Duration — longer trip = higher premium, but NOT proportionally (basic processing fee applies to all policies). (3) Destination — travel within Canada: low premium; outside Canada (especially USA with higher hospitalization costs): much higher premium; Middle East and Latin America: high-risk premiums; some destinations so high-risk that insurers won't issue coverage. (4) Age and medical history — older travellers (especially over 65) pay significantly more (Trent, age 60: $719 for 21 days vs. Quince, age 40: $570 for the same plan).
Taxation of individual extended health insurance: premiums are NOT tax-deductible but may qualify for the federal medical expense tax credit. Benefits are received tax-free.

👥 Group extended health coverage: types of benefits

Prescription drugs: every group health plan includes a prescription drug plan. Only prescription drugs are covered — not over-the-counter drugs. Covered drugs are subdivided into brand-name and generic. Brand-name: made by the company that developed the drug, protected by patent (more expensive). Generic: made after patent expiry, same properties as brand-name but at lower cost. The insurer maintains a formulary (a list of covered drugs, types covered, and price paid). If a generic-only plan member insists on the brand-name drug, they are only reimbursed for the generic price — they pay the difference themselves.
Enhanced medical and hospital care: designed to "top up" provincial plan coverage. Covers professional and semi-professional medical services not insured provincially: chiropractors, massage therapists, naturopaths, optometrists. Also covers ambulance transportation and medical equipment (crutches, wheelchair, oxygen) to an annual maximum. Hospital coverage provides for a room upgrade (e.g., from ward to semi-private) — up to a maximum daily dollar amount and maximum days. Jonah example: provincial plan pays for ward bed; group plan pays the difference for semi-private; Jonah pays the difference between semi-private and private himself. Not usually subject to an annual deductible, but co-insurance may apply.
Dental care (group): the most significant and costly element of most group plans. Provincial health care covers emergency dental care from accidents only — leaving all other dental expenses to the individual/insurer. Four reasons dental costs are high in group plans: high dental care costs; family coverage included; access is discretionary (no doctor referral needed); premiums often fully covered by the employer. Costs are controlled through deductibles, co-insurance charges, and annual/lifetime maximums. Coverage includes: cleanings, check-ups, x-rays, fillings, extractions, restorative work. Orthodontic work may be covered with maximum limits and predetermination of costs. Purely cosmetic dental work (caps, whitening) is typically excluded.
Vision care: standard feature of most group plans. Covers prescription eyeglasses (lenses and frames), contact lenses, and optometrist fees (where not covered provincially). Maximum benefit: $100–$350 range every 24 months, for first pair or a change in prescription. Optometrist fees may or may not be covered depending on the plan.
AD&D (group): pays a benefit if the plan member dies or loses (or permanently loses use of) a limb, hearing, or eyesight as the result of an accidental occurrence (not illness). Accidental death: 100% of principal sum to named beneficiary. Loss of two limbs: 100%; one arm or leg: 75%; both eyes: 100%; one eye: 67%; both ears: 75%; one ear: 33%; speech: 50%. Death or loss must occur within 365 days of the accident and must be a direct result of the accident.

💳 Group plan benefits: deductibles, co-insurance, reimbursement, direct billing

Annual deductible mechanics: applies once per year (based on anniversary of the group contract initiation date, not the calendar year). Once the annual deductible is absorbed, additional expenses qualify for reimbursement. Family deductibles: there may be both an individual deductible and a family deductible. Individual deductible claims reduce the family deductible dollar-for-dollar. Once the family deductible is used up, individual deductibles no longer apply to any family member for the rest of the year — even if their individual deductibles have not been fully used. (John/Marsha/Colton example: individual $100, family $250 — once family deductible reaches $0, all claims qualify without any individual deductible.)
Co-insurance in group plans: applies after the deductible is eliminated, against every subsequent claim. Most commonly applies to dental benefits. Both deductible AND co-insurance can apply if few claims have been filed early in the year. Kashif example: $800 dental claim, $100 deductible, 80% co-insurance → first $100 not reimbursed (deductible), remaining $700 × 80% = $560 reimbursed. Kashif paid $240 total ($100 deductible + $140 co-insurance portion).
Reimbursement basis: plan member pays for the covered product/service upfront, files a claim with receipts to the plan administrator, and receives payment by cheque or direct deposit for the portion covered.
Direct billing: plan member is given a membership card (or plan is registered with pharmacist, dentist, etc.) — covered expenses are billed directly to the group plan. The member only pays the portion not covered by the plan (e.g., difference between brand-name and generic drug costs, or excess over the plan limit). Some plans use direct billing for prescription drugs and reimbursement for dental care.

💸 Taxation of group extended health plans

Standard rule (all provinces except Québec): employer-paid premiums for group health care coverage are tax-deductible for the employer AND are NOT a taxable benefit to the covered plan member. Benefits received under the group plan are tax-free to the plan member and family. In most provinces, it is normal for the employer to pay 100% of employee coverage premiums, though family coverage premiums may be paid by the employee.
Québec exception: employer-paid premiums for group health care coverage are deductible for the employer AND are treated as a taxable benefit to the covered plan member in the calendar year paid. Benefits received under the group plan remain tax-free to the plan member and family.
Non-employer group situations (association groups): where the group member pays the extended health premiums themselves, the premiums are NOT deductible but qualify as eligible expenses for the medical expense tax credit.

🔗 Integrating individual and group policies

Those who already have group coverage may also acquire an individual contract to "top up" their group coverage or fill in coverage gaps due to coverage limits, co-insurance limits, or simply because the coverage is not provided under the group plan.

Aarne's example: a 38-year-old BC resident with family group benefits that are inadequate. Group plan deficiencies: no AD&D; vision care limited to $100 every 2 years; orthodontic maximum $2,500 lifetime with 50% co-insurance; hospital coverage only semi-private. His individual policy supplements with: AD&D coverage; vision care $250/family member annually; orthodontic dental $2,500/year with NO co-insurance; private room hospital coverage. This illustrates how an individual policy can precisely target the gaps in a group plan.
Key comparison — individual vs. group plans: Individual: fully portable (coverage continues regardless of employment); full medical underwriting at application; pre-existing conditions excluded by rider; no retroactive underwriting at claim; highly flexible customization; relatively high premiums; no deductibles (typically); co-insurance usually applied; premiums qualify for medical expense tax credit; benefits tax-free. Group: coverage terminates if plan member leaves the group (NOT portable); no underwriting for basic coverage; pre-existing conditions ARE covered (no underwriting); fairly extensive but limited by master contract; lower premiums; deductibles common (especially dental); co-insurance common; employer-paid premiums deductible and not taxable to employee (except Québec); benefits tax-free.
