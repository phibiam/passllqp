Chapter 5 - Riders and Supplementary Benefits

This lesson covers everything in depth - what riders and supplementary benefits are, riders that increase the death benefit (PUA, term insurance, accidental death, guaranteed insurability), supplementary benefits payable during life (accelerated death benefits, accidental dismemberment, waiver of premium, parent/payor waiver), and how to evaluate cost, value, limitations, exclusions, and differences between companies when customizing coverage. Take your time and work through it like a textbook chapter.

📎 What riders and supplementary benefits are

Riders evidence changes made to a contract after it was issued - for example, an increase in death coverage or the addition of supplementary benefits. Supplementary benefits can be added at the time the contract is issued, or later via riders. The terms "riders" and "supplementary benefits" are often used interchangeably, and together they let a policyholder tailor his coverage to his unique needs.

Two general rules: most riders and supplementary benefits can apply to both term and permanent insurance policies. They typically require additional premiums, and - with the exception of the paid-up additions rider - they do NOT increase the policy's cash surrender value.

💀 Riders that increase the death benefit: overview

Four riders specifically increase the death benefit offered by the base policy: the paid-up additions (PUA) rider, term insurance riders, the accidental death (AD) rider, and the guaranteed insurability benefit (GIB) rider.

➕ Paid-up additions (PUA) rider

Allows a whole life or UL policyholder to pay additional lump-sum premiums to buy small amounts of paid-up permanent life insurance during the base policy's span. PUAs increase BOTH the death benefit and the cash surrender value (CSV) - similar in concept to the PUA dividend option on a participating whole life policy, but here the policyholder makes active lump-sum purchases rather than relying on dividends.

No new underwriting needed: the PUA rider is underwritten at the SAME TIME as the base policy, so no additional evidence of insurability is required when actually purchasing PUAs later.
Typical restrictions: minimum PUA purchasable at one time (reduces administrative cost), maximum PUA purchasable in any one year, cumulative maximum over the policy's duration, timing restrictions (e.g. only on policy anniversary), and a maximum age for the life insured at time of purchase. These restrictions (beyond the minimum) exist specifically to prevent a policyholder from waiting until his health fails before making a large coverage increase.
Worked example - Valerie: $500,000 whole life policy with a PUA rider permitting up to $10,000/year in additional coverage for 20 years, with no proof of insurability needed. She isn't required to purchase in any given year, but unused amounts don't carry forward - she can't buy $20,000 the following year just because she skipped a year.

📆 Term insurance riders: the basics

A term insurance rider adds additional death coverage, over and above the base policy, for a limited period (the rider's own term). It can cover the SAME life insured as the base policy, or a DIFFERENT person entirely - in which case that person's coverage is underwritten separately. The rider's coverage amount is independent of the base policy's amount, and can even exceed it.

Critical limitation: a term insurance rider's term can never exceed the term of the underlying base policy - once the base policy expires, so does the rider. If this is a concern, look for a rider with a conversion option, allowing it to become a stand-alone policy when the base policy expires, without new proof of insurability.

📋 Term riders added to a term policy

It's common for term insurance riders (such as family or child coverage riders) to be added directly onto a term insurance base policy. The main reasons for using a rider instead of a separate stand-alone policy are convenience and cost - managing one policy and paying one premium, while reducing administrative fees.

The annual policy fee: most life insurance policies include an annual policy fee (typically $50-$100) to help cover administration costs. This fee is usually a FIXED sum regardless of coverage amount, and usually isn't affected by adding one or more riders - meaning bundling via riders avoids paying multiple separate policy fees.

🏢 Term riders added to a permanent policy

A policyholder often has a mix of long-term and short-term insurance needs. Permanent insurance can cover the long-term need, while a term insurance rider covers the shorter-term need - this combined approach has a LOWER premium cost than buying a single permanent policy large enough to cover both needs.

Worked example - Zoltan: a business owner expecting a $500,000 tax liability at death (an open-ended, long-term need suited to permanent insurance) plus a $200,000 mortgage with 10 years remaining (a defined, short-term need suited to term insurance). Rather than buying a $700,000 whole life policy, he buys a $500,000 whole life policy with a 10-year $200,000 term insurance rider - more cost-effective while still fully addressing both needs.

👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Family coverage rider

A common addition for policyholders with a spouse and children - sold in UNITS that cover all eligible family members together (e.g. one unit might provide $50,000 for the spouse and $5,000 per child). The insurer normally caps the number of units purchasable, generally enough to cover or offset funeral/burial expenses.

Underwriting and age limits: the rider is underwritten based on the spouse's attained age when issued; spousal coverage usually doesn't extend past a certain age (e.g. 65).
How children are covered: the NUMBER of children covered doesn't affect the premium - every child (including adopted children) is automatically covered once they reach 15 days of age, with NO premium increase. Death during the first two weeks of life is NOT covered. Coverage typically continues until a specific age (e.g. 21 or 25).
Worked example - Valerie: adds a family coverage rider for $100,000 of term coverage on her stay-at-home husband Robert, plus $20,000 each on her twins Rebecca and Charlotte. When her new baby is born, he's automatically covered for $20,000 once he turns 15 days old, with no premium increase.

👶 Child coverage rider

For a policyholder who wants coverage for his children but has no spouse (or doesn't want spousal coverage), a child coverage rider can be added on its own. Like the family coverage rider, every child is automatically covered at 15 days of age with no premium increase, continuing to a specific age (e.g. 21 or 25) - but the maximum coverage amount is limited to whatever the rider specifies.

🔄 Converting child or family coverage riders

A child covered under a child or family coverage rider usually has the option to convert that coverage to INDIVIDUAL permanent life insurance - generally available any time while the rider is in effect, up until it expires. A child cannot enter into a life insurance contract himself until reaching the age of majority (usually 18), but conversion is otherwise available without that restriction blocking the underlying right.

No proof of insurability needed: the child doesn't have to prove insurability to convert. Some riders allow converting to up to FIVE TIMES the rider's coverage amount. The premium for the new individual policy is based on the child's attained age at the time of conversion.
Worked example - Rebecca: covered under Valerie's family rider until age 25, with the right to convert to a whole life policy up to 5x the term coverage ($100,000), without proof of insurability. Despite having a congenital heart defect that would normally make her difficult to insure, as long as she acts before the rider expires at 25, she can still secure $100,000 of permanent coverage.
Spousal coverage too: this same conversion option is usually available to a spouse insured under a family coverage rider, though the converted amount will likely be restricted to whatever amount the rider itself covered.

💥 Accidental death (AD) rider

Provides an EXTRA benefit, beyond the regular death benefit, if the life insured dies as a result of an accident - defined as death resulting from an unexpected violent or traumatic event. Coverage may be sold in units (e.g. multiples of $25,000, to a specified max) or as a multiple of the death benefit, most commonly 2x - which is why this rider is sometimes called "double indemnity."

Timing and exclusions: death must occur within a fixed period after the triggering event (e.g. one year) to qualify. The AD rider typically excludes deaths from suicide, self-inflicted injuries, war, or commission of a crime. Only ACCIDENTAL deaths are covered - illness-caused deaths are not, and distinguishing the two sometimes requires a detailed autopsy and circumstantial analysis.
Worked example - Janet: $250,000 whole life policy with a rider doubling the death benefit for accidental death. After dying from injuries sustained in a car crash, her beneficiary receives $500,000. Had the autopsy instead shown she died of a sudden coronary event (a natural cause unrelated to the crash), her beneficiary would likely have received only the original $250,000.
Age-related limits: AD coverage may be limited in ways the base policy isn't - e.g. applying only before a certain age (like 55), continuing only until a certain age (like 60), or reducing once the life insured reaches a certain age (like 65).
AD&D: AD benefits are often packaged with accidental dismemberment benefits - when both are combined, it's called an "accidental death and dismemberment (AD&D) rider."

🔓 Guaranteed insurability benefit (GIB) rider

Gives the policyholder the option to increase his EXISTING coverage in the future WITHOUT providing new proof of insurability. Premiums for the additional insurance are based on the life insured's attained age at the time of the increase, but assume his health remains the same as when the rider was issued - regardless of how his actual health has changed.

Ideal for: those who don't currently have a large insurance need (or can't currently afford more coverage) but expect to want more in the future - guaranteeing the right to increase coverage even if health later declines.
Worked example - Doug: 25, engaged, planning a family, tight cash flow. Buys a 10-year $300,000 term policy with a GIB rider allowing up to $20,000/year of additional coverage as his family and needs grow.
Typical restrictions: increases may only be permitted at certain times (e.g. within a month of each policy anniversary), each increase may be capped (specific dollar amount or percentage of original face amount), the total cumulative increase may be capped, the number of times the option can be exercised may be limited (e.g. 5 times over the contract), and the option may only be available until a certain age (e.g. 50).

🩺 Supplementary benefits: overview

Supplementary benefits are available during the life insured's LIFETIME and may affect the eventual death benefit paid. Four are covered: accelerated death benefits, the accidental dismemberment benefit, the waiver of premium for total disability benefit, and the parent/payor waiver benefit.

⏩ Accelerated death benefits (living benefits)

When added, the insurer pays out a PORTION of the death benefit BEFORE the life insured's actual death, if certain conditions are met - sometimes called "living benefits." There are two types: terminal illness (TI) and dread disease (DD) benefits.

⏳ Terminal illness (TI) benefit

A terminal illness is a disease/sickness expected to cause death in the foreseeable future. The TI benefit allows an advance on the death benefit if diagnosed with a terminal illness expected to result in death within a fixed period (e.g. 12 or 24 months), supported by a qualified physician's diagnosis/prognosis.

Cost structure: many insurers build TI benefit into the base policy at no extra premium; some extend it on compassionate grounds even without a contractual obligation. The benefit is usually capped - as a percentage of face amount (25%-75%) and/or a dollar limit (e.g. 50% of face amount up to a $50,000 max).
Who receives it and tax treatment: the TI benefit is payable to the POLICYHOLDER, regardless of who is named beneficiary - if the beneficiary designation is irrevocable, the policyholder needs that beneficiary's permission first. Since it's an advance on a death benefit (which is generally non-taxable), TI benefits are received tax-FREE.
Effect on the eventual death benefit: the TI benefit reduces the death benefit ultimately paid at death. Some insurers structure it as a loan at a fixed interest rate secured by the death benefit - in that case, the death benefit is reduced by the loan amount PLUS cumulative interest, not just the original advance.
Worked example - William: $500,000 term policy, wife Alyssa as revocable beneficiary, TI benefit capped at 25% of death benefit to a $100,000 max. Diagnosed with terminal cancer (6-12 months prognosis), he applies for and receives the full $100,000 TI benefit. At his death, Alyssa receives $400,000 ($500,000 original face amount minus the $100,000 already advanced).

🦠 Dread disease (DD) benefit (a.k.a. critical illness/CI benefit)

A dread disease is a life-threatening or debilitating health condition. The DD benefit pays out if diagnosed with one of the conditions specified by the contract - the life insured does NOT need to be in danger of dying; often a specified SURVIVAL period after diagnosis is required instead. Diagnosis must be supported by a qualified physician.

Covered conditions: most policies cover the "Big 4" (heart attack, stroke, coronary bypass surgery, life-threatening cancer), though many cover well over 26 conditions total - coverage varies meaningfully by insurer and contract, making careful policy review essential.
Payout and tax treatment: usually paid as a lump sum once the life insured survives a specified period after diagnosis (e.g. 30 days). Since it's an acceleration of the death benefit, it's NOT taxable.
Worked example - Scott: $500,000 whole life policy with a $100,000 DD benefit. After a stroke leaves him nearly fully paralyzed, he can apply for the $100,000 DD benefit once he survives at least 30 days post-stroke.
Naming confusion - read carefully: terminology varies inconsistently between insurers. If a policy uses "critical illness benefit/rider," check whether it REDUCES the death benefit (meaning it's actually an accelerated/DD benefit, likely no extra premium) or is paid IN ADDITION to the full death benefit (meaning it's a true CI rider requiring an additional premium) - these are structurally different products despite similar names.

🦾 Accidental dismemberment benefit

Usually paired with the AD benefit (together forming the AD&D rider) - pays a lump sum if the life insured loses a specific body part or function as a result of an accident. The exact losses and corresponding payout percentages are laid out in a Schedule of Loss.

Loss% of benefit payable
Both hands or both feet100%
Entire sight of one eye66⅔%
Entire sight of both eyes100%
One arm or one leg75%
One hand or one foot66⅔%
Thumb and index finger of either hand33⅓%
Quadriplegia / Paraplegia100%
Limits and exclusions: the maximum payable for any one accident/event is capped at 100% of the death benefit. The loss must result from an unexpected violent or traumatic event, within a fixed time of that event (e.g. one year). Self-inflicted injuries, war, and commission of a crime are generally excluded.

⏸️ Waiver of premium for total disability benefit

If the life insured becomes TOTALLY disabled, the insurer waives (stops charging) premiums on the policy AND any riders/supplementary benefits attached to it. "Total disability" is defined by the policy - commonly: unable to perform his regular job for the first 2 years of disability, then unable to perform ANY job he's reasonably qualified for (by education, training, experience) thereafter.

Coverage keeps working while waived: dividends (if participating) continue, cash values may keep growing, and all riders/supplementary benefits remain in force - the waiver doesn't pause the policy's other functions, just the premium obligation.

⏱️ The waiting period for waiver of premium

A waiting period (e.g. 3 months) is how long the life insured must remain totally disabled before premiums actually start being waived. During this waiting period, premiums must still be paid - UNLESS the policy provides retroactive waiving back to the start of the disability once the waiting period is satisfied.

🔁 Waiver of premium on renewable/convertible term policies

If the base policy is renewable term and comes up for renewal during a period of total disability, the waiver of premium benefit continues to apply even after that renewal. If the base policy is convertible term, at the end of the term it converts to whole life - and premiums continue to be waived for life, or for as long as the life insured remains disabled.

👨‍👧 Parent/payor waiver benefit

Used when the life insured is the POLICYHOLDER'S CHILD - operates much like the standard waiver of premium benefit, with three notable differences.

DifferenceDetail
TriggerPremiums waived upon the policyholder's DEATH; many insurers also offer waiver upon his total disability
UnderwritingSince the policyholder isn't the life insured, the policyholder must provide proof of insurability for HIMSELF, in addition to the child's own underwriting
Age limitSome policies waive premiums only until the child reaches a specified age (e.g. 18, 21, or 25)
Worked example - Anwar: primary earner with a $1,000,000 whole life policy on himself and a $300,000 term policy on his stay-at-home wife Anya (since he'd need to hire childcare if she died). He adds a waiver of premium for total disability rider to his OWN policy, and a payor waiver benefit to Anya's policy - ensuring both stay in force if he becomes totally disabled.

💵 Cost of coverage: built-in vs. additional premium

Sometimes a benefit (like the TI benefit or waiver of premium) is INCLUDED in the base policy with its cost built into the base premium - the policyholder has no choice, it simply comes with the policy. Otherwise, riders/supplementary benefits require additional premiums.

Rider vs. stand-alone product: some coverage (e.g. term insurance riders, critical illness) can also be purchased as a separate stand-alone product. Adding it as a rider is USUALLY cheaper (one policy fee, lower underwriting costs from shared medical information) - but the policyholder becomes tied to the base policy to keep that coverage. A policyholder genuinely interested in critical illness coverage specifically should still explore whether a stand-alone CI policy might better suit his needs - suitability should never be sacrificed purely for cost efficiency.

🎯 Value of coverage: does the rider actually match the need?

A rider or supplementary benefit only has real value if it provides the protection actually needed, at an affordable cost. Consider the AD rider specifically: does the survivors' need change based on HOW the life insured dies? Generally no - the tax liability and income needs are the same regardless of cause of death.

The compromise trap: if a needs analysis shows $500,000 is needed, recommending only $250,000 base coverage plus an AD rider (paying double only on accidental death) generally is NOT appropriate, since the manner of death can't be predicted and the full $500,000 need exists regardless. However, if the policyholder genuinely can't afford a full $500,000 policy, a $250,000 policy with an AD benefit might be a reasonable compromise given budget constraints - but it should be understood as a compromise, not a substitute for full coverage.

🔍 Limitations to check

Don't assume automatic qualification for any rider payment - always check the specific limitations: what counts as an "accidental" death and what time limits apply under an AD rider; when a child becomes eligible (and stops being eligible) under a family coverage rider; how much and how often coverage can increase under a GIB rider; the maximum addable under a PUA rider; and exactly which health conditions are covered under a CI/DD benefit.

🚫 Exclusions to check

Beyond limitations, riders and supplementary benefits also carry EXCLUSIONS that rule out payment entirely in certain circumstances. AD&D benefits commonly exclude self-inflicted injuries, commission of a crime, and war - and these same exclusions often apply to the disability income benefit, the waiver of premium for total disability, and the parent/payor waiver benefit as well.

🏢 Differences between insurance companies

The core DEATH BENEFIT itself operates consistently across insurers - if the life insured dies, the death benefit owed is paid (adjusted for paid-up additions/riders that increase it, or unpaid premiums/policy loans/TI advances that decrease it) - this is fairly black-and-white.

But riders and supplementary benefits vary significantly between companies, and even between different products from the SAME company. Real comparison examples: Elias chose Company A specifically because its waiver of premium waiting period was 3 months versus 6 months elsewhere. Jackson chose Company B over A because its DD benefit was 50% of face amount versus only 25%. Cindy specifically needed a DD benefit covering Alzheimer's disease - Company A covered it, Company B didn't - making A the only suitable choice for her family history. Comparing riders and supplementary benefits carefully, not just base coverage and price, genuinely matters.
