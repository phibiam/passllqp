Chapter 2 — Insurance to Protect Income

Disability insurance provides financial protection to working individuals who become injured or ill and unable to work. Benefits range from income replacement to paying monthly mortgage, loan, and credit card payments on behalf of the disabled claimant. This chapter covers sources of DI coverage, individual policy types and benefits, group disability insurance, and creditor disability insurance.

🔍 Sources of income protection

Anyone with earned income (employment or self-employment) who is medically insurable may obtain disability income replacement from: a personally owned policy acquired directly from an insurer; group insurance as an employee, union member, or association member; creditor insurance (including mortgage disability insurance); provincial Workers' Compensation Boards; CPP/QPP disability pensions; Employment Insurance (EI) disability benefits; and the SAAQ (Québec auto accident income replacement).

Individual DI: the applicant, insured, and life insured are typically the same person. The policyholder is also the premium payor and beneficiary. Personally owned premiums are NOT tax-deductible, but benefits are NOT taxable income.
Group DI: many plan members covered under one master policy. In employer/employee plans, the employer is the policyholder; the employee is the life insured and beneficiary. Pricing is based on the group's characteristics, not individual health.
Creditor disability insurance: provided through lenders to ensure loan payments continue if the borrower becomes disabled. In a traditional loan, the insurance pays the scheduled monthly payment; for credit cards, it pays the minimum monthly payment. Plans typically have at least a 90-day waiting period and a stringent "total disability only" definition.
Mortgage DI vs. individual DI: mortgage DI is typically group term, less expensive, limited underwriting, "total disability / any occupation" definition, beneficiary is the lender. Individual DI is more expensive, full underwriting, flexible definitions (own/regular/any occupation), and the beneficiary is the life insured who can use benefits for ANY purpose.

📋 Types of individual DI policies (by guarantee level)

Non-cancellable ("non-can"): highest guarantees. Policy cannot be cancelled, premiums cannot be increased, benefits cannot be reduced, and the insurer MUST renew annually until age 65. Only the policyholder can modify the policy. At age 65 some insurers offer conversion to a guaranteed renewable contract with a shortened benefit period (typically to age 72 or 75), with new premiums based on the insured's age at conversion.
Guaranteed renewable: insurer MUST renew until the maturity date (usually 65), but the insurer CAN increase premiums — however only for the entire class of policies, not for an individual. Changes must be communicated in writing at least 30 days before taking effect at the annual renewal date.
Cancellable: fewest guarantees. The insurer can change benefits, premiums, or even cancel the policy — again only for the entire class, not a single insured, with 30 days' written notice. Premiums are lowest of the three types. Most often issued to higher-risk occupational classes or those declined on other policy types.
Guaranteed issue: individually issued policies available to low-risk occupational classes (executives, professionals: lawyers, doctors, accountants) as an alternative to group LTD. Two forms: (1) Guaranteed standard risk — guarantees issue with no coverage restrictions or premium ratings, typically requiring 100% participation or 100% employer-paid premiums; (2) Guaranteed-to-issue — guarantees some coverage but allows full medical underwriting with possible ratings or exclusions. Both require minimum hours worked per week and applicants must be actively at work at time of application.
Non-traditional DI plans: designed for the growing self-employed/contract worker market (12.9% of Canadian workforce by 2024). Features include: accident-only coverage with first-day benefits; separation of accident and sickness coverage; disability defined as inability to perform specified functions (not job duties); income-based definitions (inability to earn a percentage of regular income); coverage for currently unemployed contract workers (inability to seek new contracts); and limited monthly benefits for those not in gainful employment (e.g., stay-at-home caregivers).

💰 Individual policy benefits: amount, waiting period, benefit period

Amount of benefit: to prevent "moral hazard" (claimant being better off on claim than working), insurers limit benefit to 60–66.66% of net pre-tax, pre-disability earned income. For a personally owned policy, this benefit is tax-free. Business expenses that cease during disability are deducted from income before the 60% calculation. Non-earned income (trust income, investment income) that continues during disability is treated as part of the benefit — reducing the DI coverage offered. Formula: benefit = 60% of total pre-disability income minus ongoing non-earned income.
Waiting (elimination) period: the time between onset of disability and first payment of benefits. Primary purpose: eliminate the administrative cost of processing very short-duration claims. Common waiting periods: 0, 30, 60, 90 days (90 days is most common), up to 12 months. Shorter waiting periods available only to lowest-risk occupational classes. Longer waiting period = lower premium. The waiting period does NOT affect the benefit period (when the benefit period is less than age 65).
Benefit period: the maximum duration for which benefits will be payable, starting after the waiting period expires. Common benefit periods: 2 years, 5 years, 10 years, to age 65 (some insurers offer to age 70 or 75 on accident-only basis). The longer the benefit period, the higher the premium. Short-term DI typically covers 10 to 26 weeks. Each disability is generally treated individually — the full benefit period can apply to each separate disability over the life of the policy.

🚫 Exclusions, limitations, and denial of benefits

Standard exclusions (apply to all policies): war (declared or not); terrorist acts; self-inflicted injuries; attempted suicide; participating in illegal activities; normal pregnancy and delivery.
Underwriting limitations for non-standard risks: reduce monthly benefit amount; reduce length of benefit period; increase length of waiting period.
Denial of benefits — five reasons: (1) Misstatement of material fact — policy may be voided within the 2-year contestability period if a false statement on the application was material to the underwriting decision. (2) Fraud — if the misrepresentation was intentional, the insurer can void the policy at ANY time, even after the contestability period; false claims also allow policy cancellation. (3) Absence of loss — benefits may be reduced or denied if income was lower than insured amount at time of disability. (4) Absence of proof — medical proof is required; the insurer can request doctors' reports and independent exams. (5) Delay in filing — claimant should contact insurer within 30 days of disability onset; typically must file claim within 90 days.

🔄 Recurring disability, presumptive disability, and survivor benefit

Recurring disability clause: prevents the insured from facing a second waiting period when the same disability returns within a short period. Most commonly: if a second disability from the same cause begins within 6 months of return to work, it is treated as one continuous disability — no new waiting period. The downside: the two disability periods are combined for purpose of the benefit period maximum, potentially reducing the remaining benefit entitlement for the second disability.
Presumptive disability: certain severe conditions are automatically deemed disabling — the waiting period is waived, full total disability benefits are paid for the full benefit period, even if the insured returns to work at full salary. Qualifying conditions: total and permanent loss of hearing; total and irreversible blindness; loss of (or loss of use of) both arms, both legs, or one arm and one leg; total and permanent loss of speech. The insured does NOT need to prove a loss of income — only that the qualifying condition exists.
Survivor benefit: paid to the insured's estate if the insured dies while on disability claim. Some policies require a qualification period (e.g., insured must have been disabled for at least 12 months before death). Paid as a tax-free lump sum, typically equal to 3× the monthly total disability benefit (e.g., $3,000/month benefit → $9,000 survivor benefit).

⚙️ Premium factors: gender, age, health, occupation, disability definition

Gender and age: women pay higher DI premiums than men (more and longer claims historically). Older applicants pay more than younger ones.
Health issues: past or present health is critical. Standard premiums assume standard risk. Health issues may result in uninsurability, premium ratings, or exclusion riders. Pre-existing condition (treatment within one year before application) typically results in a 1-year exclusion rider from policy issue. Family medical history (parents, grandparents, siblings) may also affect underwriting for hereditary conditions.
Definitions of disability (4 types):
• Own occupation — most liberal/easiest to qualify. Disabled if unable to perform ALL primary duties of regular occupation, even part of the time. Benefits paid even while working in another occupation (potential for income enrichment). Restricted to lowest-risk occupational classes (4A, 3A).
• Regular occupation — same as own occupation BUT if the insured voluntarily returns to work in any capacity, benefits are reduced or terminated. Available to almost all occupational classes.
• Any occupation — easiest to purchase but hardest to qualify under. Must be unable to perform duties of ANY occupation appropriate to skills/education. If the insured can perform similar work (even if not accepted), benefits stop. Available to all classes, especially high-risk.
• CPP/QPP total disability — most restrictive. Inability to perform any occupation for which suited by education and experience, on a TOTAL and PERMANENT basis, with likelihood of never recovering. Found most often in loan/mortgage insurance.
Occupational classes: 4A (professionals: doctors, lawyers, dentists, senior executives); 3A (administrative office workers, low-hazard clerical); 2A (supervisors in low-risk environments, salespersons); A (skilled manual workers, courier truck drivers); B (manual labourers in hazardous environments). Higher-risk class = higher premium, fewer available benefits (e.g., 2A class cannot get own-occupation coverage or non-cancellable contract). Occupational clause: insurer can increase premiums/adjust benefits if insured changes to a higher-risk occupation. Downward reclassification is also possible with medical underwriting after demonstrating job stability.
Waiver of premium: NOT a rider for DI — it is a built-in contract provision at no extra charge. Waives premium payment obligation during the disability claim period. Premium is still paid during the first 90 days (often refunded). Waiver continues for the duration of the claim or up to age 65, whichever comes first. May apply to both total and partial disability claims depending on policy terms.

🔧 Riders: FPO, COLA, AD&D, partial/residual disability, ROP, exclusion riders, hospitalization

Future Purchase Option (FPO) / GIB / GIO: guarantees the right to buy additional coverage in the future without new medical underwriting, regardless of health changes. Options available at each policy anniversary (or every 2 years in older policies) until age 50 or 55. Per-option limit: typically 20% of base coverage or $1,500/month, whichever is less. Overall limit: 2–5× original coverage. FPO can only be exercised up to the 60% income limit — if existing coverage already equals 60% of current income, the option cannot be exercised regardless of the guaranteed right. New coverage is priced at the insured's attained age at exercise.
COLA (Cost-of-Living Adjustment): provides annual increases to monthly benefits starting in the second year of a claim, to offset inflation. Two methods: Simple (flat rate, e.g., 2% or 4% of initial benefit); Compound (indexed to CPI, compounded annually). There may be a maximum annual indexing rate with carry-forward provisions for unused indexing room. Alternative: automatic coverage enhancement/automatic benefit increase — increases benefits by 2–3% annually before a claim, with corresponding premium increases, reviewed every 5 years.
AD&D (Accidental Death and Dismemberment): pays additional benefits for death or specified severe injuries from an accident. Death/loss must occur within 365 days of the accident and be directly attributable to it. A principal sum is paid for death; lesser percentages for loss of sight, hearing, or limbs. No medical underwriting required at time of issue. Standard exclusions: self-inflicted wounds, attempted suicide, dangerous sports, flying (except as passenger on commercial airliner).
Partial disability benefit: paid from the outset of disability (no prior total disability required) when the insured cannot perform some substantial duties or can only work part-time. Pays 50% of total disability benefit for 2–3 years, or the residual benefit, whichever is greater. Requires physician care.
Residual disability benefit: paid when the insured can return to work part-time after a period of total disability. Must have first met total disability criteria (typically 6 months including waiting period). To qualify: at least 20% income reduction. Formula: (pre-disability income – current income) ÷ pre-disability income × full monthly benefit. Thresholds: if earning ≥80% of pre-disability income, no benefits; if earning ≤20%, full benefits; between 20–80%, pro-rata benefit.
Return of Premium (ROP): addresses concern that premiums may never generate a benefit. Typically refunds ~50% of premiums (tax-free) if the policy expires with total claims less than total premiums paid. Some policies offer 70% refund with no claims. If total benefits paid exceed total premiums, no return. Can also be structured as refunds at regular intervals (e.g., every 8 years). ROP rider adds 40–60% to the base premium cost.
Ratings and exclusion riders: three options for substandard risks: (1) Decline — policy not issued (last resort); (2) Premium rating — additional premium charged for higher risk (e.g., controlled diabetes, hypertension); may be removed if condition improves with medical evidence; (3) Exclusion rider — policy issued at standard premium, but no benefits for disability arising from the specific identified condition or activity (e.g., pre-existing back injury excluded).
Hospitalization benefit: two types: (1) First day hospitalization rider — bridges gap between first day of hospitalization and start of disability benefits (often 90 days later). No waiting period; requires 24–72 hour minimum hospital stay. Benefit = daily pro-rata of the monthly disability benefit. (2) Hospital indemnity rider — daily benefit of $50–$250 to cover specific hospital-related expenses (childcare, parking). First-day benefit with minimum 24-hour stay; maximum typically 3–4 months.

💸 Taxation of individual DI benefits and conversion

Personal ownership (standard rule): premiums are NOT tax-deductible; benefits are NOT taxable income. This also applies to key person DI policies owned personally, treatment/rehabilitation paid by insurer, and ROP/waiver of premium benefits.
Corporate ownership — key employee as beneficiary: premiums are likely tax-deductible by the employer and treated as a taxable benefit in the employee's hands; benefits received by the employee will be taxable income. Either premiums paid by employer OR benefits received by employee are taxable (but not both).
Corporate ownership — protecting corporate interests (key person, buy-sell): same as personal ownership: premiums not deductible, benefits not taxable to the corporation.
Corporate ownership — shareholder as life insured: CRA likely treats premiums as a shareholder benefit: non-deductible by the employer and taxable to the shareholder.
Conversion: the right to convert a group DI policy to individual DI coverage without medical underwriting when group coverage ends (e.g., business dissolved, employment terminated). Conversion must be made within 30 days of termination. No financial re-underwriting at time of conversion; that assessment occurs at claim time. Also: non-cancellable policies may convert to guaranteed renewable policies at age 65 with a reduced benefit period.

👥 Group disability insurance: STD, LTD, amounts, and taxation

Group providers: employer/employee groups (most prevalent) and association groups (members of the same profession, industry, union, or alumni). Association group members typically pay 100% of premiums themselves via direct bank debit.
Short-term disability (STD): designed for disabilities of short duration (usually under 1 year). Benefits: 70–75% of pre-disability salary (higher percentage because benefits are TAXABLE). Definition: "own occupation" in group DI has the same effect as "regular occupation" in individual DI — if the employee can work in a related capacity, he must choose between that job and claiming benefits. Waiting period: typically 7 days for illness; first day for accidents. Maximum benefit period: almost always 12 months.
Long-term disability (LTD): takes over when STD expires. Benefits: 50–60% of pre-disability income (lower because benefits are generally TAX-FREE when employee pays 100% of premiums). Benefit periods: 2 years, 5 years, 10 years, or to age 65. Definition shifts: own occupation for first 12 months of LTD → any occupation thereafter.
Disability during leave of absence: no universal rule — depends on the master contract. Options range from no coverage during leave, to coverage only if premiums continue being paid, to no benefits during unpaid leave (no income to replace). The employee often becomes responsible for premiums during leave even in employer-pay-all plans.
Benefit amounts — three limits: (1) Non-evidence maximum — basic coverage without medical underwriting for all plan members; (2) Overall maximum with medical evidence — higher coverage available to individual members who qualify financially and medically, subject to the group plan's stated maximum (e.g., $5,000–$15,000/month for larger groups); (3) All-source maximum (group offset amendment) — all disability income from all sources (CPP/QPP, EI, Workers' Compensation, group plan) cannot exceed 85% of pre-disability income. Personally owned DI policies are usually exempt from this offset. Group plan benefits are reduced to bring total to 85%.
Qualification period vs. waiting period: qualification period = time from date of employment before new employee can join the group plan (often tied to the probationary period, 30–90 days). Waiting period = time between onset of disability and first benefit payment. These are different concepts.

📊 Taxation of group DI benefits and integrating group with individual

Group DI taxation summary: Individual policy (policyholder pays) — premiums NOT deductible, benefits tax-free. Group (employer pays only) — premiums deductible by employer, benefits TAXABLE to employee. Group (employee pays only) — premiums NOT deductible, benefits TAX-FREE. Group (employer + employee share) — employer portion deductible/taxable; benefits partially tax-free (equal to employee's cumulative premium payments) and partially taxable (benefits exceeding employee's accumulated premiums).
Creditor disability insurance: benefits are tied to the monthly loan payment (principal + interest). Definition is always "total disability." No partial or residual disability coverage. Premiums: non-deductible unless the loan was for income-producing purposes (business/investment loan). Benefits: tax-free (paid to the creditor on behalf of the debtor).
Integrating group and individual coverage — benefit amount: purchase individual DI with same waiting and benefit periods as the group plan to fill the gap between the group's 60–66.66% benefit and a desired higher level (e.g., 75%). Claims are filed against the group policy first (first payor); individual policy is the second payor. Total coverage must not exceed the 85% all-source maximum in either contract.
Integrating group and individual coverage — benefit period: purchase individual DI with the same benefit level as the group plan but with a benefit period that begins when the group plan ends, extending the total coverage duration (e.g., group covers 24 months → individual policy picks up for an additional 3 years → total 5-year coverage). The waiting period on the individual policy equals the group plan's combined waiting + benefit periods.
