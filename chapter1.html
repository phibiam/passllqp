Chapter 1 — Investment & Savings

Before jumping into the quiz, read this short lesson. It covers the key concepts you'll be tested on. This takes about 5 minutes.

💡 What is investing?

Investing means using your savings to buy something that will grow in value over time. The goal is to earn a return — the profit made on your investment.

Returns can be positive (a profit), neutral (you get back what you put in), or negative (you lose money). The return is usually expressed as a percentage — e.g. a 10% return on $1,000 means you earned $100.

Key principle: Higher potential returns always come with higher risk. Lower-risk investments (like GICs) earn less. Higher-risk investments (like equities) can earn more — or lose more.

📈 Compound interest — the most powerful concept in investing

Simple interest pays you on your original deposit only. Compound interest pays you on your deposit plus all the interest you've already earned. Over time, this creates dramatically faster growth.

Example: $1,000 at 6% compound interest annually.
Year 1: $1,000 × 1.06 = $1,060
Year 2: $1,060 × 1.06 = $1,123.60
Year 3: $1,123.60 × 1.06 = $1,191.02
With simple interest it would only be $1,180. The extra $11.02 is compounding — and it grows every year.

🗂️ The main asset classes

Investments are grouped into categories called asset classes. Each has different risk and return characteristics.

🎯 Diversification, liquidity, and time horizon

Diversification means spreading investments across different asset classes so a loss in one doesn't wipe out everything. Don't put all your eggs in one basket.

Liquidity means how quickly you can turn an investment into cash. A savings account is highly liquid. Real estate is illiquid — it can take months to sell.

Time horizon is how long until you need the money. Longer horizons allow more risk because you have time to recover from losses. Short horizons require safer investments.

🏦 Types of investments at a glance

GICs — Guaranteed Investment Certificates. Guaranteed principal + interest. Low risk, low return. Subject to early withdrawal penalties.

Bonds — Loans to governments or corporations. Pay fixed interest (coupon). When interest rates rise, bond prices fall (and vice versa).

Stocks — Ownership in a company. No guarantee. Returns via capital gains and dividends. Highest risk, highest potential return.

Mutual funds / ETFs — Pooled investments. Instant diversification. Mutual funds priced once daily; ETFs trade like stocks all day. ETFs have lower MERs.

Segregated funds — Insurance contracts with maturity + death benefit guarantees. Higher MERs than mutual funds but offer downside protection.

Remember: Only life insurance licensed advisors (LLQP) can sell segregated funds and annuities. Cryptocurrency is NOT a licensed product under LLQP.
