Chapter 1 — Investment & Savings

This lesson covers everything you'll be tested on in this chapter — from the fundamentals of investing through segregated funds, annuities, and every major investment type. Take your time; this is meant to read like a textbook chapter, not a quick summary.

💡 What is investing?

Investing begins once a person has savings, or the ability to set money aside from income. The money set aside is called principal or capital. Investing is the process of using that capital to buy investment products with the goal of having it repaid plus a profit — called the return.

Some old stand-by saving methods still work best: pay yourself first (treat savings like a fixed expense, e.g. via pre-authorized contributions), save 10% of take-home pay, save windfalls like tax refunds or bonuses, use dedicated "savings buckets" for specific goals, and budget for it.

Returns can be positive (a profit), neutral (you get back exactly what you put in plus fees), or negative (you don't get all your money back — this is a core risk of investing).

Key principle: Higher potential returns always come with higher risk. Lower-risk investments (like GICs) earn less. Higher-risk investments (like equities) can earn more — or lose more. This is investment risk/return theory: those willing to risk their money are rewarded with potentially better returns.

📈 Compound interest — the most powerful concept in investing

Interest may be paid daily, monthly, quarterly, or annually. When interest is reinvested rather than withdrawn, the next interest payment is calculated on principal plus all interest already earned. This is compound interest — you earn interest on interest.

Example: $1,000 at 6% compound interest annually.
Year 1: $1,000 × 1.06 = $1,060
Year 2: $1,060 × 1.06 = $1,123.60
Year 3: $1,123.60 × 1.06 = $1,191.02
With simple interest it would only be $1,180. That extra $11.02 is compounding — and it accelerates every year.

For compounding to work to its full potential, all returns must be reinvested — withdrawals diminish or eliminate the effect. This is why advisors urge clients to start saving as early as possible: time is money's best friend. Compounding has a dark side too: unpaid debt balances compound against the borrower in exactly the same way.

📊 Nominal vs. real returns

A nominal return is the stated or advertised rate — e.g. a savings account advertised at 2%. The real return is the nominal return minus the rate of inflation, and tells you how much purchasing power you actually gained or lost.

Example: A GIC pays a 3% nominal return. If inflation is 1.8%, the real return is 1.2% (3% − 1.8%) — a true gain. If inflation is 5%, the real return is −2% (3% − 5%) — the investor is losing purchasing power even though the account statement shows a gain.

🧮 Present value and future value

Future value (FV) answers: how much will today's sum be worth later? Present value (PV) works backwards: how much do I need to invest today to reach a future goal? Both rely only on interest growth — not other factors like capital gains.

Future value formula: FV = PV × (1 + i)ⁿ
Present value formula: PV = FV ÷ (1 + i)ⁿ
where i = interest rate per period and n = number of periods.

Future value example: Sam has $5,000 today at 3% annual interest. In 5 years: $5,000 × (1.03)⁵ = $5,796.37. At a higher rate of 4.5%, it grows to $6,230.91 — higher rates always favour the investor in an FV calculation.

Present value example: Julie wants $5,000 in 5 years and can earn 3% annually. She needs to invest $5,000 ÷ (1.03)⁵ = $4,313.04 today. At a higher 4.5% rate, she'd only need $4,012.26 — higher rates mean less needs to be set aside now to reach the same future goal.

🗂️ The main asset classes

Asset classes are categories of investments sharing similar characteristics and regulations. The three main classes are stocks (equities), bonds (fixed income/debt), and cash (money market instruments). Real estate and commodities are often added as additional classes. Each class has its own risk/return profile, and asset classes are the building blocks of diversification.

Asset classRisk levelReturn typeExamples

🎯 Diversification, liquidity, and time horizon

Diversification spreads risk across asset classes and within them (different industries, countries, maturities) so a loss in one area doesn't sink the whole portfolio — based on not putting all your eggs in one basket. Pooled investments like segregated funds, mutual funds, and ETFs give investors instant diversification that is very hard to replicate by buying individual stocks or bonds alone — most investors simply lack the capital to do it themselves.

Liquidity is the ability to sell an investment quickly at or near its current market value. A savings account is highly liquid; real estate is illiquid because a sale can take significant time. Liquidity matters most for emergency funds, where an investor must be able to access cash quickly at full value.

Time horizon is the future date when invested money will be needed. Short-term goals are 3 years or less, medium-term is 3–10 years, and long-term is 10+ years. The longer the horizon, the more risk an investor can typically afford to take, since there's more time to recover from losses.

🎯 Investment objectives and financial goals

Investment objectives are the reasons people invest, and they should always be classified by time horizon: short-term (≤3 years, e.g. a holiday), medium-term (3–10 years, e.g. a car or home down payment), or long-term (10+ years, e.g. retirement).

A financial goal is the dollar figure tied to an objective — e.g. "$800,000 saved by age 60" is the goal behind the objective of "retire at 60." Goals motivate investors to start, continue, and monitor their saving and investing.

Group plan objectives differ from individual ones: an employer offers a group plan to attract and retain employees, while individual investors focus on their own specific needs.

🛡️ Why investors choose guaranteed investments

A guaranteed investment promises to repay some or all invested capital, sometimes with a guaranteed return as well. Guaranteed investments carry less risk and appeal to investors who are not risk-takers, generally accepting lower returns in exchange for certainty.

They are especially valuable for investors who cannot recover lost income through employment — typically those near or in retirement — and for anyone with a fixed future obligation, such as funding a child's tuition by a set date.

📋 Registered vs. non-registered plans

A plan (RRSP, TFSA, etc.) is just an empty "shell" — it provides tax treatment, but the actual investments (GICs, segregated funds, mutual fund units, etc.) are what's placed inside it and determine performance.

Registered plans are registered with the CRA in the owner's name for tax benefits. Non-registered plans offer more deposit/withdrawal flexibility but all returns must still be reported to the CRA.

💼 Segregated funds — the LLQP's core product

A segregated fund contract (also called an IVIC — Individual Variable Insurance Contract) is a special type of deferred annuity: at maturity, the insurer uses the contract's value to pay an annuity unless the client instructs otherwise. Only licensed life insurance agents can sell them and advise on them.

Clients don't legally own the segregated fund units — by law the insurer owns the funds and underlying assets — but clients' deposits are notionally invested and they're entitled to the contract's benefits. Insurers must keep segregated fund assets separate from their general company assets (hence "segregated").

Two core guarantees on every segregated fund contract: a maturity guarantee (typically 75% of deposits) and a death benefit guarantee (typically 75% of deposits). Some contracts also offer a guaranteed minimum withdrawal benefit (GMWB).

Maturity guarantee example: Lee invests $1,000 with a 75% maturity guarantee — guaranteed minimum of $750. If market value at maturity is $620, Lee receives the guarantee, $750. If market value is $1,440, Lee receives the higher market value, $1,440.

Death benefit guarantee example: Jane invests $1,000 with a 75% death benefit guarantee ($750). If she dies when market value is $700, her beneficiary receives the guarantee, $750. If market value is $910, the beneficiary receives the higher market value, $910.

Because of these guarantees, the maximum an investor can lose by holding to maturity is the gap between what was invested and the guaranteed percentage (e.g. invest $10,000 with a 75% guarantee → maximum loss is $2,500). Before maturity, however, the guarantee does not apply and an investor can lose up to the full amount invested.

Naming a beneficiary means contract proceeds bypass the estate entirely — no probate, no probate fees, and typically protection from the deceased owner's creditors. This is one of the most-tested advantages on the exam.

🛡️ Assuris — investor protection for insurance products

Assuris is a non-profit that protects policyholders if a member life insurance company becomes insolvent. It covers segregated fund contract guarantees and all annuity contracts from member insurers. Crucially, Assuris does not protect against market losses — it only protects the guaranteed amounts in the event the insurer itself fails.

Because insurers must keep segregated fund assets legally separate from general company assets, those funds remain available to pay out contract holders even if the insurer goes bankrupt — and the guarantees on top of that are further backed by Assuris, up to certain limits.

💵 Annuities — converting capital into income

An annuity converts a lump sum into a stream of payments, combining return of principal and interest. The annuitant (measuring life) is the person whose lifespan determines how long payments continue; the payee receives the payments. These can be the same person or different people.

Annuity payment size is driven by the prevailing interest rate at purchase, the size of the deposit, and the annuitant's age and gender. Fixed annuities lock in the payment for the contract's duration; variable annuities link payments to investment performance (with a guaranteed minimum); indexed annuities increase payments with inflation.

TypeDuration
Term certainFixed number of years regardless of survival
Life annuityFor life of the annuitant; ends at death (unless a guarantee period was added)
Joint and last survivorContinues until both spouses have died

Annuities can be immediate (payments start right away) or deferred (payments start at a future date). Banks can only sell term certain annuities; life annuities and joint annuities require an LLQP licence.

⚖️ Annuity advantages, disadvantages, and risks

Advantages: steady guaranteed income, no investment decisions or monitoring required, no risk of outliving income (life annuity), variable/indexed options to keep pace with markets or inflation, Assuris protection.

Disadvantages: lack of flexibility once locked in, interest rate risk (stuck at the original rate even if rates rise), loss of liquidity, potential loss of principal if the annuitant dies early without a guarantee period, and a typically high minimum investment (often $50,000+).

Prescribed vs. non-registered accrual annuity taxation: A prescribed annuity blends capital and interest evenly across every payment — the same taxable amount each year. An accrual annuity front-loads interest, creating higher tax in early years.

📈 Stocks (equities)

Buying stock means buying partial ownership of a company. Common shares carry voting rights but dividends (if any) are paid only after preferred shareholders. Preferred shares get dividend priority but typically no voting rights.

Returns come from capital gains (selling above purchase price) and dividends (not guaranteed — many companies pay none). Blue-chip stocks (large, established companies) are relatively lower risk; penny stocks (under $5, illiquid, speculative) are far riskier. A stock can lose its entire value.

Investor protection for stocks comes from the Canadian Investor Protection Fund (CIPF), which protects against dealer insolvency only — not against a decline in stock price. CIPF limits are generally $1 million for general accounts, $1 million for registered retirement accounts, and $1 million for RESPs, each combined separately.

📜 Bonds (fixed income)

A bond is debt: the investor lends money to a government or corporation (the issuer) in exchange for regular interest (the coupon rate, usually paid semi-annually) and repayment of face value at the maturity date.

Bond termMaturity range
Short-term3 years or less
Medium-term3–10 years
Long-term10+ years
The core bond relationship: when interest rates rise, bond prices fall — and vice versa. A bond trading above face value is "at a premium"; below face value is "at a discount." This inverse relationship is one of the most heavily tested LLQP concepts.

Bond credit quality is rated by agencies — AAA is investment-grade (low default risk, lower return); BB or lower is "junk" or high-yield (higher default risk, higher return, to compensate investors for the added risk).

🏦 GICs, savings accounts, and mutual funds/ETFs

GICs guarantee return of principal plus a stated interest rate at maturity. Types include fixed-interest, cashable/redeemable (lower rate for early access), escalating, variable-interest, market-linked, foreign currency, and insurance GICs (accumulation annuities — sold only by insurers, offering probate bypass and potential creditor protection).

Savings accounts offer high liquidity and CDIC protection up to $100,000 per insured category, but interest is taxed at the highest rate and often fails to keep pace with inflation.

Mutual funds and ETFs both pool investor money for instant diversification. Mutual funds are priced once daily; ETFs trade like stocks throughout the day and typically carry lower MERs. Mutual fund fees include the MER, trading expense ratio (TER), and sometimes a front-end or back-end sales load (note: deferred sales charges, or DSCs, have been banned on new mutual fund purchases since June 1, 2022).

Fund risk scale: standard deviation under 6 = low risk; 6–11 = low-to-medium; 11–16 = medium; 16–20 = medium-to-high; 20+ = high. This standardized scale applies to both mutual funds and ETFs.

🏠 Real estate, group plans, and cryptocurrency

Real estate (investment property, not a principal residence) offers rental income and capital gains potential but carries significant liquidity risk, ongoing costs (tax, insurance, maintenance), and landlord obligations. No investor protection exists for real estate losses.

Group plans include defined benefit pension plans (DBPP — guarantees a specific retirement pension, but only if fully funded), defined contribution plans (DCPP — no guaranteed payout), group RRSPs, DPSPs, group TFSAs, and PRPPs. Group plan savings are typically locked in until retirement except for proven financial hardship.

Cryptocurrency is extremely volatile, is not protected by any investor protection scheme, and is not a licensed product under LLQP — agents cannot sell or advise on it under their life insurance licence.

💰 Tax treatment of investment returns

Income typeTax treatmentExamples
Interest incomeFully taxable at marginal rate — least tax-efficientGICs, savings accounts, bonds
Canadian dividendsLower rate due to dividend tax creditCanadian stocks, dividend funds
Capital gainsOnly 50% included in income — most tax-efficientStocks sold at profit, real estate

Capital losses can offset capital gains: applied first in the year incurred, then carried back up to 3 years or forward indefinitely against future capital gains.

RRSP: Contributions are tax-deductible. Growth is tax-deferred. Withdrawals are fully taxable as ordinary income, regardless of how the return was originally earned (capital gains and dividends lose their preferential treatment).
TFSA: Contributions are NOT deductible. But all growth and withdrawals are completely tax-free.
