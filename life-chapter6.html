Chapter 6 - Group Life Insurance

This lesson covers everything in depth - how group life insurance works (the master contract, group membership, premiums, taxation), group term coverage and schedules of benefits, dependant life coverage, survivor income benefits, AD&D, conversion privileges (including Québec-specific rules), replacement contracts, waiver of premium for disabled members, group creditor insurance, and how group life compares to individual life insurance. Take your time and work through it like a textbook chapter.

👥 What group life insurance is

Group life insurance is coverage offered by a group POLICYHOLDER to a group of people sharing some form of common association with that sponsor - most commonly an employment relationship, but it can also arise through unions, alumni associations, occupational or business associations, or retail associations. Group plans often package life insurance together with medical, dental, and disability coverages.

Two parties, one relationship: there are two parties to a group insurance contract - the insurer and the group policyholder. The policyholder REPRESENTS the members of the group and sets the terms of coverage. It may react to members' requests (as with a union), but usually makes the structural decisions itself.
Worked example - Helen: a self-employed engineer with no employer to provide group coverage. She's still eligible for group life insurance through her university's alumni association, her occupational association (as a professional engineer), her Chamber of Commerce membership, and her retail association memberships - illustrating that "group" doesn't require an employment relationship.

📜 The master contract

A master contract exists between the group policyholder and the insurer - there's NO direct contract between the lives insured (the plan members) and the insurance company. This means plan members have no control over the contract beyond naming a beneficiary and potentially buying optional coverage if the plan permits.

What the member actually gets: since the plan member isn't the policyholder, he doesn't receive a copy of the master contract itself. Instead, his benefits and rights are described in an insurance certificate (also called a "benefit booklet") provided upon joining. In Québec, a plan member must contact the POLICYHOLDER (not the insurer) to obtain a copy of the master contract; elsewhere, he can consult the insurer directly.
Worked example - Derek: a health condition would normally prevent him from buying individual life insurance. His employer's group plan offers $100,000 of coverage for which he's eligible DESPITE his medical condition - illustrating one of group insurance's core advantages.

✅ Group membership and eligibility

A group plan member is a person insured under the group plan's master contract, who must meet the eligibility criteria set by the policyholder. If the plan is sponsored by a membership association, the person generally must join the association first; membership in the GROUP PLAN itself is usually optional, requiring the member to apply, and continued plan membership generally requires maintaining association membership.

Employer-sponsored plans - the probationary period: new employees typically become eligible after satisfying a probationary period (often around 3 months), also called the waiting period. Some employers AUTOMATICALLY enroll employees in mandatory coverage once the probationary period ends; others make enrollment optional, particularly when employee contributions are required.
The enrolment period: when enrollment/coverage is optional, the employee usually has a limited enrolment period (occurring after the probationary period) during which he can join WITHOUT providing evidence of insurability.
Actively-at-work requirement: for employer-sponsored plans, the employee must be actively at work on the day his coverage starts - true even for optional enrollment. If away due to illness or vacation, coverage doesn't begin until he returns to work.

🏷️ Membership classes

A group contract may cover various membership classes (employees, spouses, dependent children) and can distinguish between different employee types (management vs. non-management, part-time vs. full-time). Coverage is set the SAME WAY for everyone within a given class (base coverage), may be enhanced by optional additional coverage, and often varies BY class within the same group.

Worked example - DEF Company: a kitchen cupboard manufacturer provides one times salary death benefit for all full-time factory floor employees, and two times salary for all salaried managerial staff - two different classes, two different schedules, within the same master contract.

💰 Group premiums: how they're set

Group life insurance premiums are based on the makeup of the GROUP AS A WHOLE, not the specifics of each individual - e.g. a flat rate of $0.20 per $1,000 of coverage applied equally to every member, regardless of that person's individual age, gender, or smoking status. This is a major benefit for those whose health would otherwise prevent them from getting an affordable individual policy.

Annual recalculation: the insurer usually recalculates the premium every year to reflect changes in the group's demographics over time - e.g. if the group's average age increases, premiums per member will likely rise to reflect greater overall risk.
Who pays: most group plans covering employees require the employer to pay AT LEAST 50% of premiums, with employees paying the balance (e.g. a 50/50 split) - though some employers pay the full amount. If contributory (employees pay a portion), the employer deducts employee premiums via payroll deduction and remits the FULL amount to the insurer.

🧾 Tax treatment of group life premiums

If the employer pays some or all of the premiums, it can deduct those premiums as a BUSINESS EXPENSE. For the employee, employer-paid premiums are considered a TAXABLE BENEFIT - added to the employee's income, who pays tax on the value of those employer-paid premiums. Premiums paid BY the employee are not deductible to either the employer or the employee.

Worked example - Aimee: her annual group life premiums are $500; her employer pays 60% ($300) and she pays the remaining $200 via payroll deduction. The $300 employer-paid portion is a taxable benefit added to her income; her own $200 isn't deductible to either party.
Death benefits remain tax-free: regardless of who paid the premiums, ALL death benefits paid out under a group life insurance plan are non-taxable to the employee, his estate, or the beneficiary - this is unaffected by the taxable-benefit treatment of the premiums themselves.

🧮 Sales tax on group premiums

Like individual life premiums, group life premiums are subject to provincial insurance premium tax (built into the quoted price). UNLIKE individual life premiums, group life premiums are ALSO subject to provincial retail sales tax in some provinces: 8% in Ontario, 9% in Québec, and 7% in Manitoba. GST/HST is additionally charged on any administrative fees associated with the plan.

📈 Group term insurance coverage

Unlike individual life insurance (where the policyholder has considerable freedom over coverage amount), group coverage is RESTRICTED by the terms the policyholder sets. All members of a plan receive a prescribed base coverage amount or percentage automatically, and may or may not be able to buy additional optional coverage depending on the plan. Most group life insurance takes the form of YEARLY RENEWABLE TERM insurance.

📋 Schedule of benefits: the four formats

The base level of coverage is usually prescribed by a schedule of benefits forming part of the master contract - and if the plan has different membership classes, a DIFFERENT schedule applies to each class. Four common formats: earnings multiple, flat rate, length of service, and combination.

Earnings multiple (most common): coverage equal to a multiple of the member's BASE salary (excluding bonuses/overtime) - e.g. one times salary, two times salary. Coverage under this format may be capped at a plan-specified maximum.
Flat rate: every group member of a given class receives the SAME dollar amount of coverage - often used by unionized groups covering hourly-wage employees.
Length of service: some older plans base coverage on tenure, intended to reward long-serving employees - rarely used today.
Combination: incorporates several factors (e.g. earnings AND position). It's common to have different schedules across classes - e.g. an "executive" class at two times salary, while "all other employees" get a flat $50,000.
Worked example - Sandy, Rob, Maria: Sandy (hourly assembly-line worker) gets flat rate coverage of $50,000. Rob (manager, $92,000 salary plus ~$10,000 bonus) gets one times salary = $92,000 (bonus excluded). Maria (executive, $190,000 salary, up to 30% bonus potential) gets twice salary = $380,000 (bonus excluded either way).

🔝 Coverage maximums and reductions for older/retired members

Most group plans cap the amount of coverage for any single member, particularly relevant for earnings-multiple schedules covering highly paid employees. Since the risk (and cost) of death generally increases with age, plans often specify REDUCED coverage for older or retired members to keep group costs reasonable - as a fixed percentage of pre-retirement coverage, a fixed dollar amount, or a gradual decline each year until a specified minimum is reached.

Worked example - Carl: base benefit of $100,000. At retirement (age 60), coverage drops to 50% ($50,000). At 65, it drops to 25% ($25,000). At 70, coverage ceases completely.

➕ Optional additional coverage

Beyond base coverage, some plans let members purchase additional coverage to customize protection to their unique needs - but the member must first be enrolled in the base plan before buying additional coverage.

Evidence of insurability and adverse selection: because choosing the coverage amount creates potential for adverse selection ("anti-selection" - where someone who IS or PERCEIVES himself to be a greater risk is more likely to buy and claim on insurance), the member usually must provide evidence of insurability when applying for additional coverage. EXCEPTION: some plans waive this requirement if the member applies within a specified period (e.g. 60 days of eligibility) - preventing members from waiting until they show signs of illness before buying up.
Who pays: while the employer usually pays at least 50% of premiums for BASE coverage, the member typically pays the FULL amount of premiums for any optional coverage.

📦 Optional term coverage: units and example

The majority of optional/additional coverage takes the form of term life insurance, usually sold in multiples of fixed units (e.g. $25,000 per unit). Members can buy as many additional units as desired up to a maximum, but CANNOT buy partial units. Total coverage (base plus additional) is usually subject to an overall maximum.

Worked example - Mario: 55, just started a new job, automatically enrolled in $75,000 of base group coverage. He takes out a $200,000 mortgage and wants matching coverage, but individual insurance would be very expensive (or unavailable) due to health issues. His plan allows up to 10 units of $25,000 each, with NO evidence of insurability required if applied for within 60 days of joining. He buys 8 units ($200,000) to fully cover the mortgage, affordably and without underwriting.
Spousal extension and permanent coverage: some plans extend this optional term coverage to the member's spouse with the same units/maximums (distinct from dependant coverage, covered separately below). In rare cases, optional coverage may include permanent insurance (e.g. whole life) - though permanent coverage is more commonly available upon policy CONVERSION instead.

👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Dependant life coverage

Most group plans let members buy optional life insurance on their DEPENDANTS, as long as they do so within a short window of joining (e.g. 60 days) - with no proof of insurability required for the dependants. If a member's marital status changes, some plans allow adding dependant coverage without proof of insurability within a specified window following that change.

Adverse selection applies here too: because dependant coverage is optional, a member is more likely to buy it if the dependant is a POOR insurance risk - so premiums for dependant coverage are often higher than for an equivalent independent single-life policy.
Who counts as a dependant: typically the member's married/civil union spouse or common-law partner (opposite-sex or same-sex), and the member's children (biological, adopted, step-children) who depend on him financially, generally between 14 days old and an upper age limit (often 18, 19, or 21) - extendable while attending school full-time (usually to age 23-25), and potentially indefinite if the child is disabled and unable to work.
Worked example - Chuck and Paul: both covered for $200,000 under the same employer's plan. Chuck (married, no children) and Paul (wife plus two children, a third expected) BOTH opt into dependant coverage - despite the difference in dependant count, they pay the SAME premium, since many plans cover all dependants regardless of number. Paul's newborn is automatically covered once reaching 14 days of age. (Some plans instead require separate application/payment per dependant.)
Typical death benefit amounts: dependant coverage benefits are LOWER than coverage on the member's own life or optional spousal coverage - typically $5,000 to $20,000 on the spouse's life, with 50% of that amount on each dependent child.
Premiums: usually the same for each plan member regardless of his age or number of dependants (though some plans require a separate premium per dependant). Since coverage amounts are low, annual premiums are small (e.g. ~$2/month to cover all children for $10,000).

💵 Survivor income benefits

Some group plans provide a MONTHLY survivor income benefit in addition to the death benefit, normally as an OPTIONAL purchase by the plan member. It's typically payable to the member's surviving married/civil union spouse or common-law partner until that person reaches age 65, remarries, or dies.

Children too: survivor income benefits may also be payable to surviving children until a certain age (e.g. 21), potentially extended for full-time students. Some plans let the member choose survivor income benefits payable ONLY to surviving children.
Benefit amount: normally expressed as a percentage of the member's monthly salary just prior to death, and may be subject to a monthly, annual, or cumulative cap.
Worked example - University ABC: plan pays 25% of monthly basic earnings to the surviving spouse, plus 5% of monthly basic earnings to each surviving child. Some plans pay HIGHER benefits to surviving children if the spouse predeceased the member (i.e. the children are orphaned).

🚑 Accidental death and dismemberment (AD&D)

Group plans often include AD&D benefits. The ACCIDENTAL DEATH portion pays an EXTRA benefit (over and above the base death benefit) if the member dies as a result of an accident - usually equal to the basic death benefit. The ACCIDENTAL DISMEMBERMENT portion pays a lump sum if the member loses a specific body part or function as a result of an accident, with the percentage varying by the extent of loss per a Schedule of Loss.

Basic vs. voluntary AD&D: "basic AD&D" is automatically provided, usually tied to the death benefit amount. "Voluntary AD&D" is optional, usually purchased in multiples of a specific unit (e.g. 5 × $5,000 = $25,000), up to a maximum number of units.
Worked example - Charlotte: $120,000 base death benefit (one times salary), plus 4 units of voluntary AD&D at $25,000/unit. If she dies accidentally: $120,000 + (4 × $25,000) = $220,000. If she dies from illness: only $120,000 (the AD&D portion doesn't apply).
No evidence of insurability needed: because AD&D benefits only pay out as a result of an accident (not based on overall health/mortality risk), evidence of insurability is NOT required to obtain AD&D coverage.

👪 AD&D coverage for dependants

Some plans also allow buying AD&D coverage for the member's spouse and/or children, usually as a fixed PERCENTAGE of the member's own AD&D benefit.

Worked example - University DEF Alumni: $25,000 of member AD&D for $1.00/month, or $1.38/month for "member and family." Under family coverage: spouse-only gets 50% of the member's benefit; spouse-and-children gets spouse 40% plus 5% per dependent child; children-only (no spouse) gets 15% per dependent child.

🚫 AD&D exclusions and overall limits

AD&D benefits are only paid for losses resulting from an accidental (unexpected, violent, or traumatic) event. Common exclusions: self-inflicted injuries, war, active service in the armed forces, commission of a crime by the insured, driving while impaired, and piloting a non-commercial aircraft.

Overall unit limits: if voluntary AD&D is available in fixed units, the plan typically places an overall limit on units a member can buy - e.g. units of $10,000 up to a maximum of 10 units ($100,000 total).

🔁 Conversion privileges: the general right

A group plan member generally has the right to convert some or all of his group coverage to INDIVIDUAL coverage with the SAME insurer, WITHOUT proof of insurability, if: he leaves the plan due to retirement or changing employers, he's no longer a member of the sponsoring organization, or the plan itself is terminated.

Worked example - Derek: his employer terminates the group plan due to financial hardship. While he may convert his coverage to an individual policy, it will be considerably MORE expensive than the group rate, and he'll need to pay the premiums himself going forward.

⚖️ Conversion in Québec: leaving the plan

The Québec Insurers Act includes specific rules protecting members who leave a group plan, or when the master contract expires/is replaced. If a member leaves the group prior to age 65, he must be given the option of converting some or all of his coverage into the SAME amount of individual insurance - this also applies to any spousal or dependant coverage that existed under the group plan.

Dollar limits on conversion: the convertible amount on the participant's OWN life must be at least $10,000, and cannot exceed the LESSER of his total group coverage or $400,000. For dependants, the convertible amount must be at least $5,000 per spouse/dependant, without exceeding the coverage held on their life on the conversion date.
Timing: the member must be allowed to exercise conversion rights for at least 31 DAYS after leaving the group, with NO evidence of insurability required for himself, his spouse, or dependants. Group coverage remains in force during that 31-day window, or until converted.
Conversion options offered: the member must be given a choice between (1) coverage comparable to the group contract in amount and term, OR (2) one-year individual term insurance comparable to the group coverage, itself convertible at year-end into temporary or permanent protection.

📑 Conversion in Québec: master contract terminates

If the master contract expires and is NOT replaced, or is replaced with a contract providing LESSER coverage, any member insured under the expired contract for at least FIVE YEARS must be given the option to convert all or part of his coverage to individual life insurance within 31 days of the master contract's expiry.

Dollar limit here: the convertible amount must be at least $10,000 OR 25% of the member's life insurance amount, whichever is GREATER.

🍁 Conversion in the rest of Canada (CLHIA guidelines)

Outside Québec, conversion privileges aren't governed by provincial insurance law, but the industry follows CLHIA (Canadian Life and Health Insurance Association) guidelines, which provide similar (though not identical) protection.

The CLHIA standard: on or before age 65, a member should be able to convert group coverage on his OWN life to an individual policy without proof of insurability, up to the LESSER of: his life insurance benefit, the portion of the benefit no longer covered by any group contract, or a maximum of $200,000 for all coverage held under the group contract. At minimum, he should be able to choose yearly-renewable term or term-to-age-65, and must apply within 31 days of his group coverage terminating.
A key gap vs. Québec: unlike Québec's explicit statutory rules, CLHIA guidelines are SILENT on conversion privileges for a member's spouse or other dependants. In practice, most plans extend the same conversion privileges to optional SPOUSAL coverage, but DEPENDANT coverage is often NOT convertible.

💲 Premiums upon conversion

A member converting his group coverage should compare the converted premium to the premium for a brand-new individual policy. Adverse selection tends to apply heavily to converted policies - a person who is otherwise uninsurable or at higher mortality risk is MORE likely to convert (since no insurability proof is needed), and the insurer doesn't get the chance to underwrite normally. This increased risk to the insurer results in HIGHER premiums on converted coverage compared to a typical new individual policy for a healthy applicant.

🔄 Replacement contracts

Employers regularly look for ways to reduce costs or enhance benefits, sometimes changing group insurance providers and replacing existing contracts with new ones. CLHIA guidelines are designed to ensure a member doesn't lose coverage SOLELY because the sponsor changed insurers, or because he wasn't actively at work at the time of the change.

Benefit amounts on replacement: as long as the member is eligible under the terms of the REPLACEMENT contract, he should be covered for the same amount under that new contract, subject to its own maximums.

🤕 Disabled members and waiver of premium

Group life policies typically include a waiver of premium provision: if the member becomes disabled, the insurer waives premiums for a specific period while STILL providing coverage. CLHIA guidelines REQUIRE that this waiver and continued coverage persist even if the employer terminates the group contract with the insurer.

🏦 Group creditor insurance: overview

Financial institutions often encourage borrowers to obtain life insurance covering their debts - most commonly mortgages, but applicable to any consumer debt - ensuring repayment if the borrower dies unexpectedly. While borrowers can obtain insurance wherever they choose, most lenders offer GROUP CREDITOR INSURANCE for convenience: a subset of group life insurance where the FINANCIAL INSTITUTION is the policyholder and the BORROWERS are the plan members.

Governance: in Québec, group creditor insurance is governed by (among other things) the Insurers Act and CLHIA guidelines. CLHIA guidelines elsewhere similarly govern disclosure requirements.

📝 CLHIA disclosure requirements for creditor insurance

CLHIA guidelines require lenders to disclose specific information to borrowers at the time of applying for creditor insurance, including: the type of coverage provided (life, disability, critical illness); who is eligible to apply; that the coverage is VOLUNTARY and NOT required for loan approval; that the borrower has at least 20 DAYS after receiving the Certificate of Insurance to cancel for a full refund; that the borrower may cancel at ANY time; all terms/conditions that might limit/restrict/exclude coverage (e.g. pre-existing conditions, misrepresentation consequences); the premium amount or calculation method; that coverage is subject to insurer acceptance; further steps the borrower must take; the insurer's obligation to notify the borrower if coverage is declined; the terms upon which coverage starts if accepted; the insurer's name and contact instructions; and the circumstances under which the insurance ends.

💀 Group creditor insurance: death benefit and beneficiary

The death benefit under group creditor insurance is generally limited to the AMOUNT OF DEBT OUTSTANDING. As the debt is repaid, the death benefit decreases accordingly - although the PREMIUM usually does NOT decrease in step with the reduced debt.

The beneficiary: the beneficiary is the LENDING FINANCIAL INSTITUTION, which uses the proceeds to extinguish the debt. Because the benefit is generally limited to the outstanding debt, there's NO residual benefit paid to the borrower's own estate.

🧾 Group creditor insurance: premiums

Premiums for group creditor insurance are typically based on the borrower's age (often grouped within ranges of 5 to 10 years), smoking status, the policy, and the type of loan.

Mortgage insurance specifically: the premium is based on the mortgage outstanding AT THE TIME OF ENROLLMENT - it's essentially decreasing term insurance, where the death benefit declines as the mortgage balance declines over time, but the PREMIUM stays the same throughout.
Line of credit insurance: premiums tend to be based on the outstanding loan balance at the end of each month, or averaged over the preceding year - a more dynamic calculation than the fixed mortgage approach.

➕ Additional group creditor coverage: disability, critical illness, unemployment

Some group creditor insurance providers also offer protection if the borrower becomes disabled, suffers a critical illness, or becomes unemployed - either bundled into the base creditor life package, or offered separately.

Disability: pays the LENDER a monthly benefit if the borrower becomes disabled and unable to work - the lesser of the monthly loan payment or a specified maximum. Benefits may continue as long as disabled, or for a limited period; a cumulative maximum may also apply.
Critical illness: pays off the OUTSTANDING DEBT if the borrower is diagnosed with a covered illness - paid REGARDLESS of whether the borrower can still work.
Unemployment: pays the lender a monthly benefit if the borrower loses his job through NO FAULT of his own - normally limited to a specific dollar amount or duration.

⚖️ Group life vs. individual life: key differences

Group and individual life insurance differ across several dimensions. Control: the employer/plan sponsor owns and controls a group policy, while the POLICYHOLDER owns and controls an individual policy. Evidence of insurability: generally NOT required during a group plan's enrolment period, but always required for individual coverage.

Premiums and health status: group premiums are based on the GROUP's makeup, while individual premiums are based on the individual's own age, health, and coverage amount. As a result, people in poor health can get GROUP coverage at affordable rates, while they'd be denied or charged higher premiums individually.
Guaranteed premiums and coverage duration: group premiums are typically only guaranteed ONE YEAR at a time, while individual policyholders can choose coverage with rates guaranteed for the full duration. Group coverage rarely continues past age 65, with base/optional amounts dictated and limited by the plan; individual term coverage can extend to age 75 or 80, or permanent coverage can last until death, and can be fully customized to individual needs.

⚖️ Advantages and disadvantages of group life insurance

Group life insurance carries distinct trade-offs compared to individual coverage.

Advantages: no evidence of insurability required (covering those in poor health, with pre-existing conditions, or who smoke, at affordable rates); some/all premiums may be employer-paid; convenient enrollment and payroll-deducted premiums; coverage may convert to individual coverage without proof of insurability if the plan terminates or the member leaves; valuable as an employee benefit for attracting and retaining talent.
Disadvantages: people in very good health pay the SAME premiums as everyone else in the group (no reward for good health); people with poorer health/greater medical needs tend to file more/larger claims, which can drive group premiums UP over time; the employer/sponsor controls the plan and can make changes WITHOUT consulting members; the coverage amount may not match what the member actually needs; coverage generally ceases when the policy terminates or the member leaves the group (unless converted, and converted premiums are NOT guaranteed and may be costly); employer-paid premiums are a taxable benefit to the employee.
