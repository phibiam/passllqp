Chapter 10 - Assessing the Client's Needs and Situation

This chapter covers the framework a life agent uses to assess a client's insurance needs before making recommendations. It walks through family dynamics (current spouse, ex-spouse obligations, minor children, other dependants), employment situations (employee, business owner, retirement), the current financial picture (assets, debts, tax liabilities, expenses, cash flow), existing insurance (individual, business, group, government benefits), and client priorities (family lifestyle, final expenses, plans for the future). The Derek and Becky case study is used throughout to illustrate each concept.

👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 The assessment framework: who are we protecting?

When doing a life insurance needs analysis, the financial needs of the BENEFICIARY (or beneficiaries) must be the basis for the policy - not the life insured's own needs. The death of the life insured triggers payment of the death benefit to the beneficiary. Multiple beneficiaries can be named, each in an amount to fulfill their individual needs.

Derek and Becky case study overview: Derek (47) and Becky (38) have been married two years. Derek has a son Jordan (8) from his ex-wife Susan (44), and they share joint custody. Becky has a daughter Anya (4) whom Derek formally adopted. Together they have a newborn Robbie (2 months old). Derek is concerned about securing his family's financial future if he dies.

💑 Assessing family dynamics: current spouse

If the life insured is the policyholder, his spouse is often the first and most important beneficiary. The key question is: how financially dependent is the surviving spouse on the life insured's income?

Dependent vs. self-sufficient: in single-income households, the surviving spouse may have been entirely supported by the life insured's income. If she has never worked and has no training, her earning prospects may be limited. If she is the caregiver for children, returning to work also creates childcare costs. In dual-income households, the surviving spouse may be partially or fully self-sufficient after the life insured's death.
Case study - Derek and Becky: Derek's take-home pay is $8,400/month. Becky stays home caring for Jordan, Anya, and Robbie, and plans to return to work as an HR manager once Robbie starts school full-time (about 6 years from now), expecting $4,500/month take-home pay. Derek wants Becky to NOT have to return to work until Robbie is 18, if at all possible. If Derek died today, Becky's income would be insufficient to maintain the family's current standard of living.

⚖️ Support obligations to ex-spouse

If the life insured has an ex-spouse, he may have a legal or moral obligation to provide financial support to that spouse. The ex-spouse should be named a beneficiary in an amount to satisfy that obligation.

Court-ordered insurance: court-ordered spousal support may stipulate that the obligation continues even upon death. If the court order is silent, the ex-spouse may have a claim against the deceased spouse's estate. Sometimes the court order specifically requires the supporting spouse to obtain life insurance naming the ex-spouse as beneficiary.
Case study - Derek and Susan: their mediated spousal support agreement requires Derek to pay Susan $500/month until she reaches age 65 or remarries. While not court-ordered, Derek wants to ensure this support continues even if he dies.

👶 Minor children

If the life insured has minor children financially dependent on him, he will want them to be well cared for. This usually means maintaining the same standard of living until adulthood, and potentially through post-secondary education. Minor children can be named beneficiaries, but any insurance proceeds will be paid to the person responsible for them (per applicable provincial legislation), who holds the funds until the children reach the age of majority.

Current care arrangements - the stay-at-home parent: the death of a stay-at-home parent can have a huge financial impact, particularly with young children. If Becky died, Derek would have to pay for childcare for Jordan, Anya, and Robbie so he could continue to work - plus the family would lose the income Becky plans to earn when she returns to work in six years.
Child support to ex-spouse: if the life insured has children from a prior relationship with his ex-spouse having full or partial custody, he will typically have a legal obligation to provide child support. The ex-spouse often needs to be named a beneficiary to satisfy this obligation.
Court-ordered child support insurance: court orders for child support often require the supporting spouse to obtain life insurance to ensure support continues even if he dies. If the court order is silent, the surviving spouse may still have a claim against the estate on behalf of the children.
Case study - Derek and Jordan: Derek agreed to pay Susan $1,000/month in child support for Jordan until Jordan turns 19. While not specifically addressed in the agreement, Derek wants to ensure Jordan continues to receive this support even if Derek dies.

🧑‍🦽 Other dependants: disabled family members and aging parents

The life insured may be providing financial support to other family members who would suffer financially if he dies. Those providing that support should be named as beneficiaries.

Disabled family members: if the life insured's family includes someone who is disabled and needs lifelong care, this presents a considerable financial burden and significant impact on estate planning. Case study - Peter and Janine's son Chase (8) has a severe developmental disability, will never live independently, but has a normal life expectancy. Peter and Janine need to plan for potentially 50+ years of support for Chase.
Aging parents: as Canada's population ages, more adult children care for elderly parents - both physically and financially. Any assumed financial responsibility for parental care should be factored into the insurance needs analysis.

💼 Assessing the employment situation: employees

Loss of income is often the biggest financial risk associated with death. The agent must gain a clear understanding of this income stream regardless of whether the life insured is an employee, business owner, or retired.

Current income: a life insurance needs analysis is normally based on TAKE-HOME PAY (income after all deductions: income tax, CPP, EI, union dues), from the time of analysis until normal retirement age. This represents the actual spending power the surviving family will lose if the primary income earner dies. The surviving spouse's income is also important as a source of funds.
Future income potential: if the objective is to maintain the same standard of living, the analysis should factor in expected future income increases (and inflation). Case study: Derek expects a promotion to COO within two years, increasing take-home pay to $9,500/month with potential for larger bonuses - suggesting he may want flexibility to increase coverage in the future.
Job stability: if the policyholder's job is at risk, he might benefit from a product allowing decreased premiums for a period without reducing coverage. Case study: Becky is worried her time away from the workforce and outdated technology skills may make re-employment difficult, making Derek's income replacement even more critical.
Group benefits: the loss of employment may mean loss of group medical and dental benefits. If group benefits include life insurance, the expected death benefit should be factored in as existing coverage (see "Assess existing insurance" below).

🏢 Business owners: sole proprietorships and corporations

If a policyholder, life insured, or beneficiary owns a business, the agent needs a clear understanding of the business structure, because it impacts both the amount and type of insurance required.

Sole proprietorship: the sole proprietor reports net business income on his personal tax return. If a sole proprietor dies, the business typically ceases to exist. The deemed disposition rules apply to the ASSETS of the sole proprietorship (not the business itself). Case study - Peter: consulting income of $120,000, expenses $15,000, net $105,000 reported personally. If Peter dies, the family loses this income. His minimal business assets (home office furniture, computers) have depreciated, so no capital gain is expected on their deemed disposition.
Corporation: a sole corporate owner may receive employment income (a business expense) plus taxable dividends from after-tax profits. Upon the owner's death, shares are deemed disposed of at FMV, potentially triggering a large capital gain. If the company is a qualified small business corporation (QSBC), some or all of the gain may be sheltered by the LCGE ($1.25 million in 2024, indexed annually). Unlike a sole proprietorship, a corporation continues beyond the owner's death - shares can be inherited and dividends continue to be paid to the heir. Case study - Kimberly: salary $180,000 + dividends ~$50,000/year from Intersavvy Inc. (CCPC). Even after using the full LCGE, she expects an unsheltered taxable capital gain of ~$500,000 on her shares upon death.

🤝 Business owners: partnerships and buy-sell agreements

If the life insured owns a partnership interest, he reports his share of partnership income on his personal tax return. Upon death, he is deemed to have disposed of his partnership interest at FMV, potentially triggering a large capital gain. Unlike CCPCs, partnership interests do NOT qualify for the LCGE.

Case study - Mark: owns 50% of an apartment partnership with his children Aaron (25%) and Penny (25%). His share of profits last year was $140,000. His will leaves his interest to Aaron and Penny. Anticipated taxable capital gain of $400,000 upon death - no LCGE available. Mark is worried this will erode the rest of his estate he plans to leave to his wife Michelle.
Existing buy-sell agreement: if a buy-sell agreement is in place, the owner cannot freely leave his business interest by will - the agreement specifies who buys the interest and at what price. If life insurance funds the buy-sell, it determines coverage amounts needed. Case study - Kimberly's buy-sell with Janet: if Janet dies, Kimberly buys Janet's shares for $2 million (and vice versa). Kimberly must hold a $2 million life insurance policy on Janet's life naming herself as beneficiary.
Business income stability: fluctuating business income can affect the ability to pay premiums, making flexible-premium products important. Income replacement for a business owner should be based on AFTER-TAX take-home pay. Business dividends also represent income to replace. Expected future income increases should be factored in if the goal is to maintain the same standard of living.

🏖️ Retirement income considerations

Retirement doesn't end the need for life insurance - but it may end the need to replace employment income. The agent should determine when the life insured expects to retire and what sources of retirement income exist.

Pension plans: if the life insured is a member of a defined benefit pension plan (DBPP), the agent should determine the survivor benefits. Pension legislation generally requires a 60% to 66% survivor's pension to the surviving spouse (varies by province). If a defined contribution pension plan (DCPP) is used to buy a life annuity, survivor benefits may or may not be payable depending on the annuity type purchased.
CPP/OAS benefits: the surviving spouse may be eligible for CPP or OAS survivor benefits upon death. If the spouse also has her own employer pension or CPP/OAS benefits, these reduce her dependency needs.
Case study - Derek: plans to retire at age 65 and wants enough life insurance to replace his income for the next 18 years if he dies prematurely.

🏦 Assessing the financial situation: assets

The life insurance needs analysis must consider all property that could: be used to address estate objectives; have a corresponding liability (e.g., a mortgage); or result in a tax liability from the deemed disposition upon death. A key distinction must be made between assets the family WOULD sell to meet estate needs (e.g., investment assets) and those they would PREFER to keep intact (e.g., family cottage or heirlooms). The adjusted cost basis (ACB) and designated beneficiary should be recorded to determine if a taxable capital gain could arise upon death.

Liquid assets: easy to sell or convert to cash without loss in value. Include: chequing/savings account balances, TFSA cash balances, money market funds, term deposits with maturity under one year, cashable GICs. Uses: replace deceased's income until estate settled; pay final expenses (funeral costs); pay death-related taxes; pay off debts due upon death. Without sufficient liquid assets, the executor may be forced to sell estate assets at fire-sale prices. Case study - Derek and Becky: $25,000 each in TFSAs (money market funds and cashable GICs), plus $20,000 in a joint chequing account.
Fixed assets: tangible property that could be sold to generate cash - real estate (homes, income property, vacation property), jewelry, antiques, vehicles, other significant property. Case study: joint home ($550,000 FMV, $425,000 ACB); Derek's new car ($45,000); Becky's van ($10,000); Derek's inherited cottage ($600,000 FMV, $250,000 ACB) - intended for Jordan, Anya, and Robbie; Derek's vintage cars ($72,000 FMV, $60,000 ACB, which Becky would likely sell on his death).
Investment assets: shares in corporations, bonds, term deposits (1+ year maturity), mutual funds/ETFs/seg funds, rental real estate, business interests. The ACB and FMV of investment assets must be recorded, because the deemed disposition upon death could create a significant tax liability if a rollover doesn't apply. A distinction must also be made between registered (RRSP, RRIF, LIRA) and unregistered assets: registered funds can roll over to a spouse (or sometimes a child), but if no rollover applies, the full amount must be included in the deceased's income on his final tax return. Case study: Derek has a $280,000 non-registered portfolio (ACB $162,000, generating $7,200/year after-tax income); personal RRSP $89,000; spousal RRSP for Becky $36,000; Becky's own RRSP $24,000.
Pension entitlements: if the life insured or spouse is a member of an employer-sponsored pension plan, the analysis should factor in current entitlement and survivor benefit provisions both before and after retirement. Case study: Derek's DCPP is worth $180,000 (contributions, employer match, investment income). Becky is the designated beneficiary. If Derek dies before retirement, funds roll over to a LIRA for Becky, accessible when she reaches age 55.

💳 Assessing the financial situation: debts

Debts must be considered because payments limit available cash flow for estate objectives, and creditors may demand payment upon the borrower's death. If insufficient cash is available, estate assets may have to be liquidated to satisfy creditors.

Mortgage: if the life insured is the sole owner and mortgagee, the mortgage must be discharged before the property can pass to the intended beneficiary. If spouses are co-owners, the lender may or may not extend the mortgage to the surviving spouse alone. Case study: Derek and Becky have a $318,120 mortgage ($1,800/month, 18-year amortization). Given Becky's lack of income, Derek wants the mortgage paid off if he dies.
Credit cards and lines of credit: if both spouses share a credit card and one dies, the lender may adjust the spending limit to reflect the surviving spouse's income. Credit card debt should ideally be paid off quickly due to high interest rates. A joint home equity LOC may remain open after one spouse's death if the survivor still owns the secured property; otherwise the balance must first be repaid. Case study: Derek and Becky pay credit cards in full each month - no balance outstanding.
Other loans: car or motor home loans generally become payable upon the borrower's death. Demand loans (no predetermined repayment schedule, only interest payments required, repayable by borrower at any time without penalty) may also exist. Case study: Derek has a $41,000 car loan ($620/month for 72 more months). He wants this paid off if he dies.

📊 Tax liability upon death and current expenses

The insurance needs analysis should recognize potential tax liabilities from: deemed dispositions of capital assets, and deregistration of registered plans. Any non-registered property transferred to Becky can roll over. Registered plans (TFSA, RRSP, pension) can also roll over if Becky is named beneficiary.

Case study - Derek's cottage tax concern: the biggest concern is the cottage he wants to pass on to the children. Transferring to children (directly or through a trust) triggers a capital gain: FMV $600,000 minus ACB $250,000 = $350,000 capital gain. Tax calculation: $250,000 × 50% inclusion = $125,000 taxable; $100,000 × 66.7% inclusion = $66,700 taxable. Total tax liability on the cottage alone = $191,700. Selling the vintage cars would also trigger a taxable capital gain.
Current expenses: the analysis should consider how current expenses would change if the life insured dies, covering shelter, transportation, childcare, food/clothing, savings commitments, and discretionary expenses. Case study total monthly expenses = $8,560, including: spousal support $500, child support $1,000, mortgage $1,800, car loan $620, property/home expenses, utilities, insurance, food, clothing, RESP contributions $450, and lifestyle expenses.
Available cash flow: cash flow = net income minus expenses/savings. Positive cash flow means money is available for additional insurance premiums. Negative cash flow means the family must increase income or decrease spending to buy insurance. Case study: Derek's $8,400 take-home pay vs. $8,560 in expenses = -$160/month shortfall. Derek plans to use $600/month in after-tax investment income from his non-registered portfolio to cover the shortfall and pay any insurance premiums.

📋 Assessing existing insurance: individual policies

The agent must identify and assess all existing insurance. For individual policies, key information to gather includes: type of policy (term, T-100, participating whole life, non-participating whole life, UL); face amount and death benefit structure (level, increasing, decreasing; dividends used for PUAs?; UL account value or cumulative premiums?); CSV and ACB (for UL and whole life, including any surrender charges); policyholder; life/lives insured (joint first-to-die or last-to-die?); term of coverage; renewability and convertibility (until what age?); beneficiaries (revocable or irrevocable? split? minor child provisions? testamentary trust?); riders and supplementary benefits (GIB, PUA, dependant/family coverage?); premiums (renewal rates for term; min/max deposits for UL; limited pay?; YRT or LCOI?); limitations or exclusions; and any charges against the death benefit (policy loan, collateral, living benefits, partial surrenders).

Case study - Derek's existing policy: 10-year term, $250,000 level death benefit, bought when Jordan was born (Derek was 39). Originally named Susan as beneficiary, changed to "estate" after divorce. Includes a double indemnity rider for accidental death. Renewable and convertible to age 70. No other riders. Standard health rate premium = $250/year. Due to renew in two years. Renewal rates: $1,125/year at 49, $2,625/year at 59, $6,875/year at 69.

🏭 Assessing existing insurance: business insurance

If the life insured is a business owner or key person, his life may already be insured for business purposes - meaning the BUSINESS (not the family) is the named beneficiary, leaving the family with potentially unmet personal coverage needs. Key questions about business insurance include: is it connected to a buy-sell agreement (who are the parties, what are the coverage amounts, who owns the policies - the business in a redemption plan, or individual owners in a criss-cross arrangement)?; what type of policy (term for loan collateral or cost-minimization in criss-cross; permanent for buy-sell with cash surrender value benefit, or indefinite coverage)?; who owns the policy and pays the premiums?

Split-dollar arrangements: if the life insured is a key employee in a split-dollar arrangement, determine who receives the original face amount and who owns the cash value. In the most common key-person structure, the business owns the face amount and the employee owns the cash value - the business's premium share is tied to comparable term insurance costs, and the employee pays the balance.

👥 Assessing existing insurance: group insurance

The agent should evaluate any group life insurance in place - policies where the life insured is the group member AND any policies where the life insured is covered as a dependant under a spouse's group plan. Key questions: face amount (how much coverage, is it tied to salary or fixed, can it be increased, is there an AD&D benefit?); policyholder and conditions of membership (how stable is the employer, what are association membership requirements, what happens if membership ends?); end date and convertibility.

CLHIA conversion guidelines: suggest (not legally required) that for at least 31 days after a plan member leaves the group - on or before age 65 - the member should be able to convert up to $200,000 of group coverage to individual insurance without proof of insurability, choosing at minimum between yearly renewable term or term-to-age-65. In Québec, the law requires conversion rights for participants aged 65+ within 31 days, with coverage between $10,000 and $400,000 without proof of insurability.
Group insurance vulnerabilities: limited coverage amounts; potentially high future premiums if the group plan later terminates; plan member has no control over the policy; employer/sponsor can terminate or switch providers without member approval; must maintain group membership to keep group rates; conversion premiums are not guaranteed. Case study: Derek's group life = one times annual salary, convertible to the same amount of individual term coverage on leaving the plan.

🇨🇦 Government benefits: CPP, QPP, OAS, and Workers' Compensation

Government benefits upon death are not usually sufficient to address all insurance needs, but should be noted in the analysis.

CPP survivor benefits (2025 maximums): Death benefit = $2,500 lump sum (paid to estate). Survivor's pension = $770.88/month (under 65), $859.80/month (65+). If 65+ also receiving CPP retirement pension, combined maximum = $1,449.53/month. Children's benefit = $301.77/month per child (under 18, or 18-25 in full-time school). Benefits must be applied for - not paid automatically. Case study: Derek has made maximum CPP contributions for ~20 years and meets eligibility requirements.
QPP differences: death benefit maximum = $2,500. Survivor pensions for those under 45 vary by disability and dependent children status. See Table 10.3 for specific amounts. Children's benefit ("orphan's pension" in Québec) = $301.70/month.
OAS - Allowance for the Survivor: provides allowance to a low-income surviving spouse of an OAS pensioner, when the surviving spouse is between ages 60 and 64. Maximum allowance is reduced based on other income, and eliminated completely if annual income exceeds $29,976 (2025). All OAS benefits and thresholds are indexed quarterly.
Workers' Compensation (WC): protects employees from financial hardship from work-related injuries and occupational diseases. WC fatality and dependency benefits (paid to surviving spouse/children) include: funeral costs, an immediate lump sum, and monthly pensions. CRITICAL LIMITATION: WC benefits are ONLY payable if death is caused by a WORK-RELATED accident or illness. Death from a non-work accident or unrelated illness does not trigger WC benefits. Therefore, WC benefits cannot be counted on to satisfy life insurance needs.

🎯 Client priorities: family lifestyle and final expenses

After assessing family dynamics, employment, finances, and existing coverage, the agent must understand the client's qualitative priorities. The right amount of money into the right hands is key. Key questions about family lifestyle goals: how long will children need care and what type?; will the surviving spouse go back to work immediately or stay home?; will the surviving spouse need additional training or education?; does the life insured want the family to continue in the same residence?; does he want the home to be mortgage-free?; does he have a cottage or heirloom property to pass on without forced sale?; does he have a family business to pass on?

Case study - Derek's priorities: wants Becky to stay home until Robbie is in school full-time (6 years); wants to ensure enough income to supplement Becky's eventual return-to-work salary to maintain their standard of living until her retirement at age 60; wants all debts paid off; wants to fulfill spousal and child support obligations to Susan and Jordan without involving Becky; wants to ensure the cottage can pass to the children by having funds available to pay the resulting income tax.
Final expenses: funeral arrangements are personal. The agent should ask: is there an existing funeral contract, and if so is there an outstanding balance?; if not, what level of funeral is desired (simple or elaborate, burial or cremation)? Case study: Derek wants a modest funeral and believes $20,000 will be adequate.

🔮 Client priorities: plans for the future

Many clients want to plan beyond maintaining the family's current lifestyle, including: post-secondary education (costs continue rising faster than inflation); weddings for children; home downpayment assistance; other family legacies (lump sum to help children establish themselves financially); and philanthropy (charitable giving at death - either because they couldn't afford it during life, or as a tax-planning strategy via life insurance).

Case study - Derek's future plans: wants to help children afford a quality post-secondary education - believes an additional $10,000 per child (beyond RESP contributions) will be sufficient. No plans to provide capital for a home or business. Also wants to make a $100,000 charitable donation to his local hospice if he dies, because the hospice recently provided excellent end-of-life care to his father.
