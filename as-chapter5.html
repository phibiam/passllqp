Chapter 5 — Insurance to Protect Businesses

Many disability and A&S insurance plans protect individuals — but there is also a whole field of disability insurance designed to protect businesses themselves when an owner or key employee becomes disabled. This chapter covers business ownership structures, disability risks faced by business owners, and the specific insurance products designed to address each risk: BOE insurance, business loan protection, disability buyout coverage, key person insurance, and health spending plans.

🏢 Forms of business ownership

Sole proprietorship: the only owner of an unincorporated business. By far the most common business structure in Canada. Easy and inexpensive to set up (may require provincial registration). Primary downside: the business and personal financial affairs are inseparable — net income is reported on the proprietor's personal tax return (no separate lower corporate tax rate) and personal assets are exposed to business creditors. Sole proprietors receive all benefits of the business personally.
Partnership: two or more individuals carrying on a business together for profit, sharing net profits or losses. More complex than proprietorships. Net income is computed at the partnership level and reported to partners pro rata per the partnership agreement — taxed at each partner's personal tax rate. Primary disadvantage: all partners may be held solidarily (jointly and severally) liable for the debts and liabilities of the partnership, even for another partner's negligence.
Corporation: a separate legal and tax entity, separate from its owners (shareholders). Ownership is in shares (e.g., 5% of shares = 5% of net value). Control through share voting rights. Liabilities of the corporation do NOT become the liabilities of shareholders. Taxed on net income at corporate rates; may distribute after-tax income as dividends (taxed at lower rates in shareholders' hands). Willy example: $200,000 net income as sole proprietor pays personal tax on all of it; as a private corporation taking $150,000 personally and leaving $50,000 in the business — the $50,000 is taxed at approximately 12–15% corporate rate vs. 45% personal rate.
Private (closely held) corporation: shares not publicly traded; held by a limited number of shareholders who are typically owner/operators. Established for tax advantages (lower corporate tax rate) and/or protection from business creditors.
Public corporation: shares traded on a public stock exchange; shareholders are investors (not owner/operators) — not exposed to corporation's liabilities.

⚠️ Risks to the business owner and disability statistics

Three main disability risks for business owners: (1) Inability to work due to disability; (2) Inability to sell the business (triggered by disability); (3) Loss of a key employee to disability. All three can be mitigated through specific disability insurance products.
Disability statistics: for a person aged 45 (typical business owner age), the odds of developing a disability are approximately 27.7%. In 2022, 27.0% of Canadians aged 15+ (8.0 million people) had at least one disability — an increase of 4.7 percentage points from 2017.
Business as primary asset: the business is typically the owner's most significant asset (greater than home, RRSP, investment portfolio, pension). It is the source of current income, primary retirement asset, and in worst cases, the emergency fund. It can only fulfil these roles if it can be sold at the owner's discretion, within a reasonable time frame, and for a fair price.
Disability-triggered forced sale obstacles: identifying a buyer; convincing the buyer to commit; negotiating a fair price from a position of weakness; ensuring the buyer has sufficient resources for timely execution. Every day of delay reduces the likelihood of achieving a fair price. Employees fear job security; co-owners may resent sharing profits with a non-productive disabled co-owner or working with the disabled owner's family members.

📋 BOE insurance: purpose, who qualifies, what's covered

Purpose of BOE insurance: pays specified ongoing business expenses while the business owner is disabled and unable to generate revenue. Personal disability income insurance replaces the owner's personal income but provides no cash flow for business expenses (Tamirru example: $5,400/month personal DI covers personal living but does nothing to maintain his $4,000/month studio rent and assistant's salary). BOE insurance keeps the business and business assets viable so the owner has a business to return to upon recovery.
Who qualifies for BOE: sole proprietorships, partnerships, or closely held corporations with 5 employees or fewer. The thinking: the owner's services are so vital that disability could cripple the business. If a co-owner or employee can replace the disabled owner's services, the business may qualify for a lesser amount or be disqualified entirely.
Definition of disability for BOE: "regular occupation" — if the business owner cannot perform their regular duties with the business, the definition is met. "Any occupation" would be inappropriate since the business needs the owner specifically, not just any worker.
Qualifying BOE expenses: rent of business premises; property taxes on owned business premises; business income taxes; utilities (heat, water, natural gas, electricity); vehicle leases; salaries of most employees; phone, internet, and communications charges; loan interest; lawyers' and accountants' fees.
NON-qualifying BOE expenses: capital portion of loan payments; new capital expenditures; salary of the disabled owner; salary of someone hired to temporarily replace the disabled owner; salaries of employees who can independently continue to generate revenue; salaries of relatives brought into the business AFTER the onset of disability.

⚙️ BOE insurance: waiting period, benefits, carryover, tax treatment

Waiting period: typically short — sometimes as little as 15 days, seldom longer than 90 days. This reflects the urgency of a business's financial obligations when the owner is suddenly disabled.
Benefit maximums: the monthly benefit amount (e.g., $6,000/month) is a MAXIMUM reimbursement benefit, not a fixed benefit. The full amount is only payable if at least that much in qualifying expenses is claimed. Benefits remain in force until the earliest of: business owner recovers and returns to work; the total benefit maximum has been claimed; or the business has been sold or terminated. Todd example: $6,000/month for 24 months = $144,000 total maximum. If only $4,200 is claimed in month 1, only $4,200 is paid. If the full $144,000 is not claimed within 24 months, the benefit period may extend beyond 24 months until the entire $144,000 is claimed.
Carryover of benefits/expenses: flexible contracts permit carry-forward of both unused monthly benefit room AND unclaimed expenses. Type 1 (expense carry-forward): qualifying expenses exceeding the monthly maximum in one month can be carried forward and claimed in future months when expenses are lower. Marie example: $6,900 in month 1, $6,000 claimed, $900 carried forward; $6,600 in month 2, $6,000 claimed + $600 → $1,500 accumulated carryforward; if only $3,000 in month 3, then $3,000 + $1,500 carryforward = $4,500 paid. Type 2 (benefit room carry-forward): if expenses in a month are less than the maximum, the unused benefit room can be carried forward to future months for reimbursement of higher future expenses.
BOE provisions and exclusions: may include: waiver of premium; return of premium; future purchase option (FPO); presumptive disability clause; partial disability clause; residual disability benefits. Standard disability exclusions apply (war, self-inflicted injuries, normal pregnancy, criminal activities).
BOE tax treatment: premiums are TAX-DEDUCTIBLE as a business expense. Benefits received are TAXABLE income to the business. However, the covered expenses are themselves tax-deductible business overhead — so the tax on BOE benefits and the deductions for expenses generally offset each other. Net tax effect: neutral. Lucien example: $4,200 BOE benefit received (taxable income) OFFSET by $4,200 of deductible expenses covered = no net tax payable.

🏦 Business loan protection disability insurance

Purpose: pays business loan payments during a period of disability — either periodic (monthly) or lump-sum. Separate from BOE insurance (BOE also covers loan interest, but claims cannot be made under BOTH types for the same loan — the insured must choose which contract to claim under). Both policies serve different needs so holding both does not create over-insurance.
Who needs it: sole proprietors, business partners, and sole owners of private incorporated businesses where the business has qualifying loans and revenue depends on the owner's continued active participation.
Eligible loan criteria (all three must be met): (1) The loan must be essential to the successful operation of the business. (2) Interest on the loan must be tax-deductible (i.e., the loan was undertaken for the purpose of earning income from business or property). (3) The loan was issued by a financial institution (bank, trust company, credit union, or loan company). Examples: mortgages on business premises; equipment loans; lines of credit and account overdrafts.
Who qualifies: the business must have at least 3 years of profitable operation, meet a minimum net worth standard, and operate in higher occupational categories. The business owner (or the business itself) is the policy owner, pays premiums, and receives benefits. The life insured is the business owner; definition of disability = "regular occupation."
Benefits: periodic (monthly) up to $10,000, typically for up to 24 months; OR lump-sum up to $250,000 (not exceeding 75% of loan balance), but only after a lengthy elimination period (usually one year). Total benefits cannot exceed the loan balance. Optional benefits: waiver of premium is most common. Standard disability exclusions apply.
Business loan protection tax treatment: premiums are NOT tax-deductible (protecting loan principal, which is a capital asset). Benefits received (discharge of loan principal and interest) are TAX-FREE. Hideki example: $200,000 equipment loan (front-end loader + truck), 60-day waiting period, 60-month benefit period — protects his loan repayment ability and prevents the bank from seizing his equipment.

🤝 Buy/sell agreements and disability buyout insurance

Purpose of buy/sell agreements: predetermine the purchase and sale of business assets in the event of disability (also death, critical illness, or retirement). Provides certainty for all parties: the disabled owner knows the business will be sold at a fair price; co-owners know they won't be forced to work with family members of the disabled owner or share profits with a non-productive owner; employees have job security. Lawrence/Daryl/Darrin example: without a buy/sell agreement, disagreements on price and financing forced an instalment purchase years in the making, while Darrin's wife refused to sell equipment — all preventable with proper planning.
Elements of a buy/sell agreement: triggering circumstances (death, disability, retirement, bankruptcy); who must sell and who must buy; when the buyout takes place (e.g., 90 days after disability declaration or 180 days after); the price or pricing method (fixed price, price formula, or third-party valuator); how the buyout will be funded (personal assets, loan, instalment payments, or disability insurance); non-competition, confidentiality, and non-solicitation clauses; dispute resolution (mediation/arbitration); restrictions on third-party sales or rights of first refusal. Disability defined as "regular occupation." The buyout typically does not take effect until the owner has been disabled for 12+ months, to allow time for recovery.
Funding options for buy/sell: (1) Personal assets — few buyers have sufficient liquidity; (2) Loan — no guarantee of approval, must be repaid with interest, drawn from reduced business revenues; (3) Instalment payments — leaves seller uncertain about payment continuity while new owner struggles to maintain schedule; (4) Disability insurance — preferred option: funded in advance with premium payments, guarantees funds will be available when needed, can be customized to match buy/sell agreement terms (amount, definition, waiting period).
Two types of buy/sell agreements: Cross-purchase (criss-cross): each party owns, pays for, and is beneficiary of a policy on the OTHER party's life. Each receives proceeds to buy out the disabled partner's interest. Entity purchase (share redemption): the BUSINESS ENTITY itself owns, pays for, and is beneficiary of the policy. Insurance proceeds are used by the business to buy out and cancel the business interest of the disabled partner/shareholder. Harold/Kendrick example: cross-purchase = Harold owns policy on Kendrick and vice versa; entity purchase = the corporation owns policies on both.
Taxation of buy/sell: Cross-purchase: the sale is a disposition for income tax purposes — the seller reports a capital gain or loss. The purchaser's adjusted cost base equals the purchase price paid. Entity purchase (partnership): treated as a disposition of capital property — seller reports capital gain or loss. Entity purchase (corporation): portion of price equal to paid-up capital (PUC) is not taxable; any amount above PUC is a deemed taxable dividend. Sale to third party or another shareholder (not a redemption): any resulting capital gain may qualify for the Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption (LCGE) if shares qualify.
Disability buyout insurance features: definition of disability = "regular occupation" (sometimes "own occupation and not working"). Waiting period = 12 months (sometimes 18–24 months) — significantly longer than other DI policies to give the disabled owner time to recover. Benefit period = often one day (lump-sum payout at end of waiting period). Some policies: 50% lump-sum + 50% in instalments over up to 60 months. Once the waiting period is met, the buyout is triggered — even if the owner subsequently recovers. Coverage amounts: up to $1,000,000–$2,000,000. Conditionally renewable and non-cancellable. Reducing benefit schedule after age 60 (reducing 20% of original face amount per year, recognizing pending retirement). FPO available to keep pace with increasing business value. Termination: if the insured is no longer an owner or the business is no longer operating — but conversion to personal coverage may be available (without medical evidence, subject to financial qualification). Tax treatment: premiums NOT deductible; not reported as taxable benefit to the insured owner; benefits TAX-FREE to the policyholder.

👤 Key person disability insurance

Who is a key person: an employee whose skills and contributions are so unique and important to business success that they are very difficult to replace in the short term. Could be a key executive, technical expert, systems designer, sales trainer, or top revenue generator. Matti example: his sales manager Ruttu is responsible for $200,000 in annual net income — losing Ruttu to disability would substantially reduce the business's income.
Purpose of key person DI: provides the employer with cash flow to offset potential losses from the key person's absence: decline in productivity from the key person's absence; decline in other employees' productivity due to loss of support/guidance; decline in sales or networking contacts; cost of recruiting and training a replacement. Ownership restriction: maximum limit on ownership (10%–50% depending on insurer) before an individual is disqualified from being considered a key person.
Key person policy terms: definition of disability = "regular occupation." Conditionally renewable, provided the insured continues to be employed full-time with the same employer. Riders: waiver of premium; recurrent disability clause; replacement expense benefit rider (additional payout to cover hiring a replacement). Benefits: restricted to no more than 100% of the insured's annual salary, paid monthly, to a maximum of approximately $15,000/month. Waiting period: 30–90 days (short, because the business impact is immediate). Benefit period: usually no more than 12 months (if the key person is off longer, the employer must find a replacement). Tax treatment: premiums NOT deductible; NOT reported as taxable benefit to the employee; benefits TAX-FREE to the employer/business.
Mona example (Signs and Lines): Mona generates 45% of the company's $300,000/year revenue. Key person policy: 60-day waiting period, 12-month benefit period, $12,500/month — sufficient to cover overhead and other employees' salaries while Mona is disabled, keeping the business operating.

🏗️ Health spending plans: ELHTs, PHSPs, grouped plans

Employee Life and Health Trusts (ELHTs): created in 2010, replacing Health and Welfare Trusts (HWTs) which were required to convert to ELHTs by January 1, 2022. Used primarily by medium-to-large employers due to complexity and administration costs. Trust must reside in Canada. Employer contributions: tax-deductible AND not a taxable benefit to the employee. Contributions are not refundable to the employer and must fund group health/accident insurance premiums, private A&S plans, or group term life insurance. Designated employee benefits (eligible medical expenses such as health care, drugs, dental): tax-free to the employee. Non-designated employee benefits: may be taxable.
Personal Health Spending Plans (PHSPs): also called private health services plans. Designed for sole proprietors, partners, and their families. Contributions (to a prescribed maximum) may be used to directly pay qualifying health care services: prescription drugs, vision, dental, and other medical services qualifying for the medical expense tax credit. Annual limits: $1,500/year per business owner/adult family member (sole proprietors); $750/year for family members under 18. An administrator handles contributions and disbursements for a fee. Contributions must be used within 2 years or forfeited. Tax: 100% deductible by employer, not taxable income to employees (except in Québec for provincial purposes). Benefits not taxable. Most PHSPs include travel insurance and an exceptional expense rider (e.g., semi-private hospital room).
Grouped DI/CI plans: an employer acquires, funds, and holds individual DI or CI policies for 2 or more employees (one employee is not a group). Benefits are paid directly to the disabled employee. Must be offered to a defined class of employees — cannot be selected randomly or exclusively for shareholders. Different grouped plans may be set up for different employee classes. Shareholders may be members if they are also employees. Tax: premiums ARE deductible by the employer in the year paid; employees do NOT report premiums as income; BUT benefits received by employees ARE taxable income. Hans example: $800 premiums paid by XYZ Corporation (deductible); Hans receives $3,600 in benefits (taxable income to Hans).
Integrating business and personal coverage: key challenge for insurance advisors — ensure no gaps and no needless duplication. Sources of DI coverage: CPP/QPP; EI; Workers' Compensation; group/grouped employer plans; individual A&S policies; DI attached to life insurance; BOE; business loan protection; buyout coverage; key person coverage. Carly example: co-shareholder in 30-employee corporation. Company holds key person coverage (viability), buyout policies (12-month waiting period), and corporation-paid DI paying 50% of net income for 9 months (90-day waiting period). Carly also needs a personal DI policy: 30-day waiting period, 12-month benefit period, $10,000/month for first 60 days + $5,000/month for remaining 10 months — because corporation plan benefits are taxable and the after-tax income would be insufficient to sustain her personal lifestyle during the year before buyout is triggered. All-source maximum (85%) applies when government plans + private plans are combined.
