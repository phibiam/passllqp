Chapter 6 — Client Profile

Before presenting any A&S insurance recommendation, an agent must first analyze the client's complete situation. A thorough client profile covers three areas: personal situation, financial situation (income and expenses), and insurance situation. This chapter provides the framework for gathering and assessing all the information needed to make appropriate recommendations for disability, critical illness, long-term care, and extended health insurance.

👤 Personal situation: age, gender, and avocation

Age: affects the risk of illness or disability, the number of years benefits may be needed, the medical care expenses that may need to be covered, and therefore the cost of insurance protection.
Gender: women generally have a longer life expectancy than men of the same age (relevant to LTC, where care must be provided for a longer period) and a higher likelihood of incurring a disability than men of the same age.
Avocation (sports, hobbies, activities): sedentary lifestyle (watching TV, reading, chess) may invite health problems through lack of exercise. Moderate recreational activity (walking, cycling, golf, non-contact hockey) promotes fitness and reduces illness risk. Extreme sports (rugby, skydiving, rock climbing) create much greater injury and permanent disability risk — these clients may be uninsurable or subject to exclusions for disability or LTC coverage.

💍 Marital status and dependents

Single client: fewer financial obligations to others; cannot count on a spouse's income or personal assistance during disability. Simple case: Henri earns $60,000/year and needs $3,000/month DI (60% of income) if disabled.
Married or common-law client: the spouse could provide a second income source that partially offsets the need for coverage. Jacques and Marie each earn $60,000/year; combined need = $6,000/month but each still earns $5,000/month if the other is disabled — leaving only a $1,000/month shortfall for each to insure against. Note: the spouse may also have to reduce work to care for a disabled partner, so a higher level of coverage is still prudent.
Previously married/separated/divorced: verify current support obligations to former spouse or children; obtain copies of any relevant agreements or court orders.
Dependents: spouse (who may need special care or long-term care); children (fully dependent until end of secondary school, potentially longer for post-secondary or special needs); other family members (aging parents, grandparents, siblings requiring care). All dependents add to the minimum coverage amount required.

💼 Occupation: duties, work history, and future plans

Occupation is the most critical factor for DI: determines the type and definition of coverage available (own occupation definition only for low-risk professionals like doctors, lawyers, senior management), minimum waiting period, duration of benefits, premiums, and whether the client is insurable at all. Note: occupation is less important for determining CI and LTC coverage amounts.
Duties: the physical environment (office vs. construction site), amount of physical exertion, stress level, hours per week, and vacation time all affect occupational risk classification.
Work history: past jobs in hazardous environments (mining, chemicals) may have caused postponed ailments. Frequent job changes (every couple of years) may reduce the weight given to current occupation details in underwriting.
Future work plans: planned occupation changes may affect income (higher or lower DI coverage needed), occupational risk classification, or insurability. A planned retirement date also affects how long DI benefits need to be payable — ideally from now until the retirement date.

🏥 Health: personal history, family history, and retirement goals

Personal health history: current health and past health problems are major factors for all A&S insurance underwriting. Past disability claims, previous illnesses, and current medications must all be fully disclosed with complete detail (nature of injury/illness, dates, duration, treatment, lingering effects). Incomplete answers on the non-medical questionnaire risk delays or coverage denial. Health problems could lead to DI claims (inability to work), CI claims (heart attack, stroke triggered by diabetes, high blood pressure, smoking), or LTC claims (debilitating conditions requiring home care or nursing home).
Family health history: parents and siblings' health histories are of almost equal interest to underwriters. Certain conditions tend to run in families — adult-onset Type II diabetes, breast cancer risk, genetic conditions. Family longevity is a positive factor for life insurance underwriting (longer life = lower premiums) but a NEGATIVE factor for CI or LTC insurance (longer life = greater likelihood and size of claim). Family risk factors could lead to coverage restrictions or declines for CI and LTC.
Retirement goals: planned retirement date determines DI benefit period (coverage should extend to retirement). Retirement lifestyle affects CI and LTC needs: active retirement with extensive travel increases the importance of CI coverage (heart attack or stroke could curtail mobility or require special equipment like wheelchairs); living abroad (e.g., winters in Florida) means potentially much higher health costs not covered by provincial plans — travel insurance, health insurance, or LTC policies may be needed as supplemental coverage.

💰 Client income: earned income and unearned income

Earned income is central to DI planning: DI replaces earned income lost to illness or injury. For employees: income is based on pre-tax income; need history of employment (stability, length, trends), breakdown of salary vs. bonus vs. commissions (different predictability; group plans only cover income from that employer). Bonuses: how consistent? Commissions: obtain a 5-year trend to assess stability. For self-employed: based on pre-tax, after-expense (net) income. Also assess whether overhead (rent, employees) requires a separate BOE policy — home office consultants with variable overhead may not need BOE; those with fixed overhead do.
Unearned (passive) income: investment income (interest, dividends, capital gains); income from registered plans is locked in and normally not counted in DI planning; non-registered investment income that is paid out to the client should be factored in (both pre-disability and post-disability); if normally reinvested, a decision must be made about converting to cash flow during disability. Market downturns could deplete investment values — allowance must be made for this.
Spousal income: a passive income source for a married/common-law client — continues even if the client is disabled. However, the spouse may reduce work hours or leave work to act as caregiver, reducing this income source when it is most needed. Must be assessed in the context of overall family needs, not just the client's needs.
Other income sources: support payments (spousal or child support from a former partner — taxable or tax-free; how long payable; can the payor maintain payments if they become disabled or die?); pension income (government and private — when can it start? how much?); royalties and trust income (continue regardless of disability — must be recorded and factored in); disability income already in place (existing private, group, CPP/QPP, Workers Compensation, EI — amounts, duration, qualifying definitions).
In-kind income: care provided free by a live-in family member (spouse, child) eliminates expenses that would otherwise be paid to a third party — a form of indirect income. The offset: the caregiver's lost employment income. Community programs (Meals on Wheels, in-home care, rehabilitation) are additional free resources. Emergency funds and liquid assets can be converted to cash flow during disability — ideally 3 to 6 months of expenses in an emergency fund.

📊 Client expenses: living expenses, savings, and debt

Two approaches to calculating DI needs: Income approach — 60% of pre-tax income (since DI benefits are generally tax-free). Expense approach — based on actual monthly expenses needed. Sofia's example: $60,000 income, $2,000/month family expenses → income approach = $3,000/month; expense approach = $2,000/month. Both are valid and the choice depends on the client's circumstances and affordability. CI benefits are lump-sum based on estimated costs of dealing with a critical illness (not tied to income). LTC benefits are based on maximum reimbursement for covered services (not tied to income).
Living expenses: accommodation (mortgage or rent — the largest monthly expense; review terms, renewability, balance outstanding), food, utilities, transportation, etc. Bank statements (over 1 year) reveal spending patterns and amounts. A budget distinguishes "need to" expenses (must be provided for in a disability plan) from "nice to" expenses (can be cut if necessary). Simon's example: $1,200 housing + $800 groceries + $300 utilities + $400 transportation = $2,700/month minimum.
Savings and investments (dual role): as assets, they can supplement lost income; as liabilities, contributions constitute ongoing cash drains. Review: RRSP/TFSA statements (balance, liquidity, maturity dates, surrender fees; RRSP withdrawals are taxable; TFSA withdrawals are tax-free); RDSP (for qualifying disabled beneficiaries — lifetime limit $200,000; government matching grants and bonds available; investment income accumulates tax-free but grants, bonds, and income are taxable on payout); non-registered investments; pension plans (commuted value, portability, earliest commencement date, bridge to CPP/QPP); cash value life insurance (dividends, CSV loans, premium holidays, waiver of premium — four potential sources of emergency cash flow).
Debt: personal line of credit/HELOC (outstanding balance, interest rate — secured lines have much lower rates than credit cards; unused portion can serve as emergency funds or be used to discharge high-rate debt); credit card statements (90% of Canadians hold at least one; review three most recent statements for spending patterns, average payment, balance, current credit limit); tax liabilities (personal income tax arrears, corporate income tax, GST/HST obligations for businesses with gross revenues over $30,000/year, property tax); other liabilities (support agreements, pending lawsuits, likely need to support aging parents, Home Buyers' Plan repayment obligation).

🧾 Financial situation: net worth and cash flow

Net worth = assets minus liabilities. Key questions: Should assets be valued at fair market value or after-tax? Can non-registered assets easily be converted to cash? For how long could assets be converted to cash before retirement goals are jeopardized? Example: cottage valued at $500,000 may have a large taxable capital gain on sale — net worth without this tax consideration overestimates the client's financial position. A $300,000 RRSP represents much less after income tax, especially if withdrawn all at once.
Assets review: list at current fair market value, noting whether registered or non-registered, ACB for non-registered assets, liquidity. Current assets (can be converted to cash within 12 months) are most relevant for emergency planning. The family home should generally be excluded from consideration as emergency funds except in dire circumstances. The net worth statement for John and Jane H. example shows: total assets $1,103,400 (house, cottage, vehicles, RRSP, TFSA, life insurance CSV, cash) minus total liabilities $240,200 (mortgages, line of credit, credit cards, car loans, RRSP loan) = net worth $863,000.
Cash flow statement: lists all sources of net income (after-tax) and all expenses including debt payments. A surplus shows resources to fund insurance premiums or offset lost income; expenses identify minimum required coverage; even a deficit shows where expenses could be cut. Key questions: (1) In the event of disability, does the client have sufficient resources to cover income loss for at least one year? (2) In the event of a medical emergency (CI, LTC), does the client have sufficient resources to cover expenses for at least one year?

🛡️ Insurance situation: existing coverage and government programs

Existing A&S coverage — by source: individual personally owned; individual corporately owned; group insurance; individual lender-owned (associated with mortgage or credit cards — usually very limited). By benefit type: DI, BOE, disability buyout, extended health, CI, LTC. Group coverage: employer plans terminate when the plan member leaves the group (no conversion right generally); association group coverage does not have this restriction.
Characteristics to document for each policy: risk covered; conditions covered; exclusions; type, amount, duration of benefits; ancillary benefits (waiver of premium, death benefit); tax status of premiums and benefits; disability/CI definition; termination date; renewability/convertibility. Only by comparing all these factors across all sources can the agent develop a clear picture.
Cost of coverage: if the client has limited resources, triage may be necessary — prioritize which coverages are most critical. Association group coverage may provide similar benefits to individual policies at lower cost. Credit card coverage may provide limited benefits at a comparatively high price.
Employment Insurance (EI) sickness benefits: to qualify: must have worked at least 600 hours in the previous 52 weeks and suffered at least a 40% reduction in income. In 2024: pays 55% of average weekly insurable earnings up to $695/week maximum (taxable). One-week waiting period. Maximum 26 weeks. EI is a second payor to most other disability benefits (CPP, QPP, Workers Compensation, group insurance) except personally owned policies — dollar-for-dollar reduction. EI contributions: shared by employer and employee; deductible for employer; tax credit for employee portion. Self-employed: must pay both employer and employee portions. Québec residents have a lower EI contribution rate due to the QPIP.
CPP/QPP disability benefits: payable to plan members under 65 with "severe and prolonged" disability. Severe = unable to work in ANY capacity (not just previous job). Prolonged = long-term, usually permanent, or likely to result in death. Tim and Tom example: Tim can still work (just at a different job) = NOT qualified; Tom cannot work at all = QUALIFIED. Benefits begin the 4th month after disability commences. Continue until recovery, age 65, or death. At 65, automatically converts to retirement benefit. Contributions shared 50/50 employer/employee; 100% self-employed. Benefits are taxable income.
Workers Compensation: provincially operated plans for injuries or illness incurred ON THE JOB or workplace-related illness (asbestos poisoning, chemical exposure). Benefits average 80-90% of pre-disability net income. Contributions 100% paid by the employer as a payroll tax — deductible for the employer; not a taxable benefit to the employee. Benefits are TAX-FREE to the recipient.
Four coverage scenarios at the end of the client profile process: (1) Coverage is complete, adequate, and appropriate — leave in place. (2) Unnecessary surplus — modify to eliminate unproductive expense. (3) Adequate but overlapping — note all-source maximum provisions; excess may be scaled back. (4) Gaps in coverage (most common) — gaps include: uninsured risks, insufficient benefits, waiting periods too long, benefit periods too short, overly restrictive definitions. The agent's key role: identify and prioritize gaps and formulate a plan to address them.
