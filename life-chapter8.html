Chapter 8 - Business Life Insurance

This lesson covers everything in depth - the four potential impacts of death on a business (loss of skills, creditor demands, family interference, equality for family members), capital gains tax on shareholder death, the three business types (sole proprietorship, partnership, corporation) including the LCGE for CCPCs, key person insurance and split-dollar arrangements, and buy-sell agreements (cross-purchase and share redemption), funded by criss-cross insurance or business-owned insurance, including the capital dividend account (CDA). Take your time - the buy-sell funding structures and CDA mechanics are exam favourites.

💼 Why businesses use life insurance

Canada is a nation of small business owners, and life insurance serves several distinct business objectives: protecting the business from the death of a key employee or shareholder, funding a buy-sell agreement, and providing additional benefits for highly valued employees. Death is not just a personal tragedy - it can have a significant impact on a business, particularly small businesses or start-ups.

Four potential impacts of death on a business: loss of skills, creditor demands, family interference, and the need to treat family members equally. The agent must understand the structure of his client's business in order to advise him appropriately from an insurance perspective.

🧠 Impact 1: Loss of skills - the key person

Some companies have one person or a handful of people who are indispensable and not easily replaced - these are "key persons" or "key employees." Examples: a highly skilled craftsman, an entrepreneurial genius, an influential vice-president, a charismatic salesperson, or an engineer with unique expertise. If a key person dies, the business loses skills vital to continued viability - resulting in lower production, decreased quality, higher expenses, reduced profit margins, reduced consumer confidence, and lower revenues.

Worked example - Monica: a talented architect whose firm estimates 30% of business comes from clients specifically seeking her talents. Her death would have a significant impact on the company's revenue - illustrating why identifying and insuring key persons is a core business insurance need.

🏦 Impact 2: Creditor demands

Many business loans are demand loans - they have no fixed term or set repayment duration as long as interest is paid. The borrower can repay in full at any time without penalty, but the lender can also RECALL the loan (demanding repayment in full) as long as any notice requirements in the loan agreement are met. If a business has significant debt and a key person dies, creditors can become nervous and recall demand loans or refuse to extend additional credit.

Worked example - Cooper: senior sales director responsible for 50% of the firm's new business. After his death, the bank heard about it and saw declining sales figures, then recalled the firm's $500,000 demand loan for their IT infrastructure - funds they were counting on Cooper to generate sales to repay.

👨‍👩‍👧 Impact 3: Family member interference

When a business has multiple owners and one dies, problems arise if the deceased left his business interest to a surviving spouse or children who may not be capable of or compatible with the surviving owners. Even where the heir is willing to sell, he could demand an unreasonable price.

Worked example - Jason, Ivan, Conner, and Mason: the three joint owners of Restful Solutions (a funeral home). Jason died without a will, leaving his ownership interest to his 24-year-old son Mason, who had a history of petty theft and drug use. Mason showed up at the business, demanded to take over his father's role, and when refused demanded $1,200,000 for his share - illustrating exactly why buy-sell agreements (discussed below) are critical in multi-owner businesses.

⚖️ Impact 4: Equality for family members

Succession planning addresses how a business will continue when current owners die, retire, or leave. "Equally" means "fairly" - not "exactly the same." Where one child has the interest and aptitude to take over a family business while another doesn't, creative estate planning is needed to treat both children fairly.

Worked example - Christina, Harry, and Nancy: Christina owns Hibiscus (a garden centre worth ~$1.5 million). Harry (who has worked there since age 10) should inherit the business; Nancy (a doctor with no interest in gardening) should receive fair treatment. Christina's other assets are only ~$400,000, so she can't simply leave the business to Harry and $400,000 to Nancy and call it equal. Life insurance on Christina's life (payable to Nancy's benefit) can bridge this gap.

📊 Capital gains tax on the shareholder's death

When a Canadian taxpayer dies, he is deemed to have disposed of all of his property for its fair market value (FMV) immediately prior to death - unless a spousal rollover applies. For shares in an active business, this can result in a taxable capital gain if shares pass to anyone other than the surviving spouse. If the estate can't pay this tax, the executor may be forced to sell shares to pay it, which may be difficult or impossible for closely-held private company shares.

Worked example - Lori: owns 20% of a business corporation with an ACB of $100,000 and FMV of $600,000. Taxable capital gain = ($600,000 minus $100,000) x 50% = $250,000. At a 50% marginal rate: income tax liability = $125,000. If the estate can't pay this, and her daughter Olivia can't either, shares must be sold to fund the tax bill.

👤 Business type 1: Sole proprietorship

A sole proprietorship is an unincorporated business owned by a single person. From a tax and legal perspective, there is NO legal separation between the business and the owner - they are the same entity. The sole proprietor reports the business's net income on his personal return and is personally responsible for all business debts. The business is not a separate entity that can be bought, sold, or bequeathed - it ends upon the owner's death. However, the assets of the business (inventory, buildings, etc.) form part of the sole proprietor's estate and are subject to the deemed disposition rules upon death.

🤝 Business type 2: Partnerships

A partnership is a business owned by two or more people with the common purpose of earning a profit. Partners may be active (working in the business) or passive/silent (providing capital and sharing earnings without active involvement). Each partner's ownership share is his "share in the partnership" and may differ among partners.

Tax and liability: each partner reports his portion of net partnership income on his personal tax return. Each partner is JOINTLY AND SEVERALLY liable for all debts or obligations of the business - UNLESS it is a limited partnership. A limited partner shares in profits but is NOT personally responsible for the business's debts; however, a limited partnership must always have at least one GENERAL partner who IS personally liable.
Unlike a sole proprietorship: a partnership share CAN be bought, sold, or bequeathed upon death. It HAS an adjusted cost base (ACB), and a capital gain or loss can be triggered upon sale or the deemed disposition at death. The resulting tax liability can be significant and needs careful advance planning.

🏢 Business type 3: Corporations

A corporation is a SEPARATE legal entity, distinct from its owners (shareholders). Shareholders are NOT personally liable for the business's debts - unless they personally guaranteed them. Directors can be held personally responsible for the company's income taxes and source deductions (income tax withholdings, CPP, EI). The corporation's net income is taxed IN the corporation, not on shareholders' personal returns. After-tax profits increase the value of shares and/or are paid out as dividends. Shares have an ACB, and sale or deemed disposition at death can trigger capital gains or losses.

Public vs. private corporations: a public corporation is listed on a public stock exchange. A private corporation's shares are not publicly traded. A subset of private corporations are Canadian-Controlled Private Corporations (CCPCs) - incorporated in Canada, resident in Canada, not controlled by non-resident persons or public corporations, and not listed on a stock exchange.
Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption (LCGE) for CCPCs: upon actual or deemed disposition, shares owned by CCPC shareholders may be eligible for the LCGE. The LCGE for a CCPC is $1.25 million for 2024 (indexed annually for inflation), and can be used to eliminate or offset capital gains on qualified CCPC shares, family farm shares, or family fishing business shares.
Worked example - Gene and Kiliman: Gene's CCPC (Kiliman) had FMV $2,400,000 and ACB $2,000,000 at death. Capital gain = $400,000. Gene uses $250,000 of his LCGE, leaving a taxable portion of ($400,000 minus $250,000) x 50% = $75,000. Tax at 45% marginal rate = $75,000 x 45% = $33,750. Without the LCGE: $400,000 x 50% x 45% = $90,000 in tax - a significant difference.

🔑 Key person life insurance: the basics

If a business owner or employee is essential to the success of the business, the business may want to buy KEY PERSON life insurance on that individual and name ITSELF as the beneficiary. The death benefit received is tax-free and can be used to: recruit and train a replacement, make up for lost revenue, or cover overhead expenses until the business gets back on its feet.

🔀 Split-dollar arrangements

A split-dollar life insurance arrangement is a method of sharing the attributes and costs of a permanent life insurance policy between two or more parties. It is typically used when one party needs life insurance protection and the other needs a tax-deferred investment vehicle. A common use is a key employee and the corporation jointly owning a policy - the arrangement is a separate agreement from the policy itself.

Two common structures: (1) Employee owns the death benefit (face amount), corporation owns and controls the cash value / account value portion. (2) Corporation owns the death benefit up to the face amount, employee owns the cash value and any death benefit in excess of the face amount. The second is the most common arrangement for key person insurance - the corporation gets the face amount to offset the key person's death, while the employee gets access to a tax-deferred investment vehicle.
Premium sharing: the corporation's share of premiums is tied to the reasonable cost of comparable term insurance coverage for the death benefit it owns; the employee pays the balance. The Income Tax Act has no specific split-dollar rules, but the CRA's administrative view is that the premium allocation should reflect the fair market value of each party's respective interests - approximately the cost of YRT or NCPI for a UL policy.
On retirement or termination: the corporation no longer needs key person coverage. Transferring the ownership interest in the death benefit to the departing employee may result in a taxable benefit. If the policy is surrendered, the employee reports a policy gain (CSV minus his ACB).

💳 Key person insurance as a requirement for borrowing

If a business's success depends on a key person, a financial institution lending to that business may REQUIRE the assignment of a life insurance policy on that key person. If the key person dies, the lender receives whatever portion of the death benefit is needed to extinguish the debt.

Premium deductibility: if the business acquires key person insurance because the lender requires it, the business can deduct the premiums (or the NCPI, in the case of permanent insurance) from its business income. If the death benefit exceeds the outstanding loan, the deduction for premiums is prorated accordingly.

📝 Buy-sell agreements: the basics

A buy-sell agreement is used in businesses with more than one owner (general partnerships, private corporations) to control what happens if one owner dies. It typically specifies: WHO has the option or obligation to buy the deceased owner's interest, the PRICE to be paid (fixed dollar amount, formula, or method), and HOW the purchase will be FUNDED. Agreements can also cover buyouts for retirement, disability, or departure - but this chapter focuses only on death.

Why buy-sell agreements are important: they provide a GUARANTEED BUYER (solving the liquidity problem of small business shares), a GUARANTEED VALUE (ensuring fair compensation for the deceased's estate), and a MANDATORY SALE (preventing an unwanted outsider - like Mason - from becoming a joint owner). The most important provision is HOW the transaction will be funded, since there's no point mandating a sale at a specific price if the buyer lacks funds.

🔄 Cross-purchase agreements

In a cross-purchase buy-sell agreement, the surviving owners agree to buy the deceased owner's interest. For a corporation, total shares outstanding remain the same - each surviving shareholder now owns a LARGER number of shares (and a greater percentage ownership).

Worked example - Bob, Calvin, Dylan: three shareholders each owning 100 shares at $10,000/share. Their buy-sell says survivors buy the deceased's shares. If Bob dies: Calvin and Dylan each buy 50 shares from his estate at $10,000/share. Bob's estate receives $1,000,000. Calvin and Dylan now own 150 shares each (50% ownership each instead of 33.33%). Total shares outstanding: still 300.

🔃 Share redemption plans

A share redemption plan is another type of buy-sell arrangement where the CORPORATION redeems the shares of the deceased shareholder. This REDUCES the total number of shares outstanding, which automatically increases the surviving shareholders' percentage ownership (their actual share count doesn't change).

Worked example - Emily, Fatima, Gloria (Forest Air): three shareholders each owning 100 shares at $10,000/share. If Emily dies: Forest Air pays $1,000,000 to her estate and CANCELS those 100 shares. Total shares drop to 200. Fatima and Gloria still own 100 each - but now that's 50% each instead of 33.33%. Key distinction: in a share redemption, the COMPANY (not surviving shareholders) is the buyer.

✖️ Criss-cross insurance

Criss-cross insurance is often used to fund CROSS-PURCHASE buy-sell agreements. Each party to the buy-sell agreement buys life insurance on the OTHER parties, in an amount sufficient to cover their purchase obligation upon that party's death. Premiums are NOT deductible (the insurance is not being used to secure a loan for business or investment purposes), but the death benefit is TAX-FREE.

Worked example - Bob, Calvin, Dylan: each must be able to buy 50 shares at $10,000 each = $500,000 from each other. So Bob buys $500,000 coverage on Calvin AND $500,000 coverage on Dylan. Similarly for Calvin and Dylan - 6 policies total (each of 3 owners buying 2 policies on the other 2).
The premium fairness problem: Bob (32, healthy) pays much more for coverage on Dylan (54, overweight, smoker, hypertensive) than Dylan pays for coverage on Bob. The death benefit is the same for both - illustrating a key disadvantage of criss-cross insurance when there are significant age and health differences between owners.
Tax on the deceased's shares: the deceased owner is deemed to have disposed of his business interest for FMV just prior to death, triggering a capital gain on his final return - unless sheltered by the LCGE. The surviving associate uses the tax-free death benefit received to fund the share purchase from the estate.
Full criss-cross process - Jack and Alfred (Jackal Inc.): (1) each pays premiums on the other; (2) Jack dies, his 100 shares go to his estate; (3) insurer pays Alfred the tax-free death benefit; (4) Alfred pays Jack's estate for 100 shares; (5) shares transfer to Alfred, who now owns 100% of Jackal Inc.

🏦 Business-owned insurance: advantages

An alternative to criss-cross insurance is having the BUSINESS buy life insurance on the business owners. Key advantages over individually-owned policies:

Advantage 1 - Equalizes premium costs: if the cost of insuring each associate varies significantly due to age and health, business-owned insurance ensures costs are shared equally among owners.
Advantage 2 - Proof of premium payment: associates can verify via the company's financial statements that premiums are actually being paid. If policies are individually owned, it's harder to get this proof.
Advantage 3 - After-tax cost advantage: since premiums are generally NOT tax-deductible and must be paid with after-tax dollars, it is cheaper for a corporation to buy the insurance if the corporate tax rate is LOWER than the associates' personal tax rates.
Advantage 4 - Efficiency with multiple owners: with more than two associates, business-owned insurance is generally more efficient and cost-effective than having each owner buy individual policies on all the others.
Advantage 5 - Joint first-to-die option: if there are only two shareholders and the agreement will be funded by business-owned insurance, a joint first-to-die policy may be appropriate.

📒 The Capital Dividend Account (CDA)

A private corporation uses its Capital Dividend Account (CDA) to record various amounts received on a TAX-FREE basis - including the death benefit received from a life insurance policy. It can then pass those tax-free amounts on to shareholders as a TAX-FREE capital dividend. The CDA is a NOTIONAL account - it doesn't actually hold funds; it just tracks amounts for tax purposes. When the corporation pays out a capital dividend, the CDA balance is reduced by the same amount.

Amount credited to the CDA: for a TERM policy, the ACB is zero, so the FULL death benefit is credited to the CDA. For whole life or UL policies, only the portion of the death benefit that EXCEEDS the policy's ACB is credited to the CDA - the portion equal to the ACB is taxable to the corporation. Note: G3 policies grow ACB faster in early years than G2, meaning more of the death benefit is taxable to the corporation and less goes to the CDA - which is LESS favourable for corporate-owned policies.
For simplicity, this chapter assumes term insurance with ACB = 0, so the full death benefit goes to the CDA.

🔄 Business-owned insurance funding a cross-purchase agreement

When corporate-owned insurance funds a cross-purchase buy-sell agreement, the corporation is named as the beneficiary. When a shareholder dies, the surviving owner(s) purchase the shares from the estate - often using a promissory note. The insurer pays the death benefit to the corporation, which is credited to its CDA. The surviving shareholder(s) then direct the corporation to pay them a tax-free capital dividend, which they use to pay off the promissory note.

Full corporate cross-purchase process - Jack and Alfred (Jackal Inc.): (1) Jackal Inc. pays premiums on both Jack and Alfred; (2) Jack dies, his 100 shares go to his estate; (3) insurer pays tax-free death benefit to Jackal Inc., credited to its CDA; (4) Alfred pays Jack's estate with a promissory note; (5) Jack's estate transfers 100 shares to Alfred (Alfred now owns 100% = all 200 shares); (6) Alfred instructs Jackal Inc. to pay him a tax-free capital dividend; (7) Alfred uses those funds to pay off the promissory note.

📤 Business-owned insurance funding a share redemption plan

In a share redemption plan, the COMPANY and all shareholders are parties to the buy-sell agreement. The company buys life insurance on all of the shareholders, naming itself as the beneficiary. When an owner dies, the company uses the death benefit to redeem the shares from the deceased shareholder's estate - cancelling those shares and reducing total shares outstanding.

Full share redemption process - Jack and Alfred (Jackal Inc.): (1) Jackal Inc. pays premiums on both Jack and Alfred; (2) Jack dies, his shares go to his estate; (3) insurer pays tax-free death benefit to Jackal Inc., credited to its CDA; (4) Jackal Inc. uses the funds to redeem Jack's shares from his estate, cancelling them; (5) total shares outstanding drop to 100; (6) Alfred still owns 100 shares, which now represents 100% of the company. No promissory note or capital dividend is needed in this structure - the company pays the estate directly for the shares.
