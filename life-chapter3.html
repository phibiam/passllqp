Chapter 3 - Whole Life and Term-100 Insurance

This lesson covers everything in depth - the concept of permanent insurance, whole life mechanics (premiums, reserves, mortality costs), premium and death benefit options, participating vs. non-participating policies, dividend payment options, non-forfeiture benefits (CSV, policy loans, APL, reduced paid-up, extended term), limited payment whole life, premium offset policies, and Term-100 insurance. Take your time and work through it like a textbook chapter.

♾️ The concept of permanent insurance

Permanent life insurance provides coverage over the ENTIRE lifetime of the life insured. It does not expire (as long as required premiums are paid) and does not need to be renewed - unlike term insurance, which ends at age 75-80 and cannot protect against the risk of death beyond that point.

Key contrast: term premiums typically increase with age, while whole life or T-100 premiums usually remain UNCHANGED over the life insured's lifetime. The trade-off for this premium stability is higher premiums in the early years compared to an equivalent amount of term coverage.

🏦 The policy reserve

In addition to providing coverage, whole life premiums build up a RESERVE. This reserve helps fund the higher cost of insurance later in life, since premiums collected early exceed the actual cost of insuring a younger life insured.

3️⃣ The three types of permanent insurance

TypeKey feature

Universal life (UL)Lifetime coverage, flexible premium above a minimum, tax-sheltered savings component
This chapter covers whole life and T-100 in detail; universal life is covered in the following chapter.

📜 Whole life insurance overview

Whole life insurance typically guarantees the premiums, the death benefit, and a minimum cash surrender value (CSV). It is sometimes called "straight life" or "ordinary life" insurance. Unlike term, a whole life policy does not expire and does not require renewal.

⚖️ How the policy reserve absorbs cost over time

In the EARLY years, whole life premiums exceed the actual cost of insuring the life insured - this "overpayment" creates the policy reserve, which the insurer invests. In the LATER years, premiums fall short of the actual mortality costs and expenses - the policy reserve subsidizes this deficit, keeping the premium level throughout.

📊 How whole life premiums are set

Premiums are based on long-term assumptions about mortality, expenses, and investment returns, projected over a contract that could last 50, 60, or even 70 years. Because of this long time horizon, insurers use very CONSERVATIVE assumptions - resulting in a higher premium to ensure enough is collected (with interest) to be more than adequate for the eventual death benefit.

Mortality costs - term vs. whole life: term mortality costs are lower since coverage is limited in duration, the lives insured tend to be younger, and there's no CSV to fund. Whole life mortality costs are higher because the insurer KNOWS it will very likely have to pay the death benefit eventually (covering the life insured until death, absent surrender) - so it spreads the cumulative annual mortality cost over the policy's anticipated duration.

💼 What whole life premiums fund (expenses)

Similar to term, but more extensive: selling costs, underwriting, investment management fees, income taxes, claims investigation, business overhead, death benefits, dividends to shareholders, processing change requests, and policy loan/surrender/termination requests. The insurer must project ALL of these well into the future.

📈 Investment returns and capital reserves

The insurer invests the policy reserve (bonds, interest on cash reserves, dependable stocks) to achieve growth, and must assume a rate of return over the contract's duration when setting premiums. Insurers must also legally maintain minimum capital surplus reserves (the Life Insurance Capital Adequacy Test, or LICAT) to guarantee death benefit payments and other obligations.

📅 The modal factor

Premiums are normally quoted annually and paid in advance. Paying annually lets the insurer invest the funds up to a year sooner than monthly payments would allow. For any payment frequency other than annual, the insurer applies a MODAL FACTOR reflecting its lost investment income.

FrequencySample modal factor
Semi-annually0.51 to 0.53
Quarterly0.26 to 0.30
Monthly0.087 to 0.09
Worked example: Victor's whole life policy has a $956 annual premium. Paying monthly at a 0.088 modal factor: $956 × 0.088 = $84.13/month. Over the year: $84.13 × 12 = $1,009.56 total - 6% HIGHER than the quoted annual premium. The "annualized premium" for any non-annual frequency will always be higher than the quoted annual rate.

💳 Premium payment options

OptionWhat it means
Ongoing premiumsFixed premium paid for the duration of the contract (until death or surrender) - sometimes called a "lifetime-pay policy"
Single premiumOne lump sum covers the entire lifetime - by definition, also a "paid-up" policy since no further premiums are needed
Limited paymentPremiums for a specific period (e.g. 10/20 years) or to a specific age (e.g. 65 or 100), after which the policy is paid-up
Worked example: Helena, 40, non-smoker, gets a $500,000 whole life policy at $11,065/year, guaranteed never to increase - a classic ongoing/lifetime-pay structure.

🎯 Guaranteed vs. adjustable whole life

A guaranteed whole life policy locks in the death benefit and premiums - they will not change regardless of the insurer's actual mortality, expense, or investment experience. The insurer absorbs the assumption risk; the policyholder pays for that certainty via the premium.

An adjustable whole life policy guarantees the death benefit and premiums only for an initial period (e.g. 5 years), after which the insurer reviews actual experience against projections and may increase, decrease, or maintain both. This exposes the policyholder to real uncertainty absent in a guaranteed policy.

Two real examples: Ranjit's adjustable policy premium DECREASED from $6,500 to $6,415 after a 10-year review showed policyholders living longer than expected (fewer death benefits paid). Helmuth's premium INCREASED from $11,600 to $12,100 after a 5-year review showed lower-than-expected investment returns due to falling interest rates.

💰 Non-participating vs. participating policies

Whole life policies are classified as non-participating (non-par) - no potential to earn dividends - or participating (par) - the policyholder may receive a portion of company surplus as dividends.

Why surpluses occur: insurers are typically very conservative in their mortality, expense, and investment assumptions. Surpluses arise if actual mortality is lower than projected, investment returns are higher than expected, or expenses are lower than expected. The insurer retains some surplus to boost reserves against future shortfalls (regulators also mandate minimum capital reserves regardless of surplus/shortfall).

🛡️ Non-participating policies: who bears shortfall risk

If a non-par insurer isn't conservative enough and realizes a revenue shortfall, the COMPANY ALONE bears that burden - the policy and policyholder are unaffected, protected by the capital reserve. A non-par policyholder can only share in surplus by becoming a company SHAREHOLDER and receiving stock dividends - not through his policy.

🎁 Participating policies and policy dividends

When par policies generate excess revenue, some may be used to maintain reserves; any remainder may be distributed as POLICY DIVIDENDS. Despite the name, policy dividends are NOT the same as stock dividends paid to shareholders - they are fundamentally a return of a small portion of the premium, not guaranteed, paid annually on the policy anniversary, and scaled to the policy's face amount.

Risk allocation: participating policyholders do NOT assume shortfall risk - they won't be asked for additional premiums and their death benefit won't be reduced if a shortfall occurs. This is why participating policy premiums are typically HIGHER than comparable non-participating premiums - par policyholders get the upside potential (dividends) without the downside risk.
Identifying par products: most include "participating" in the product name; contracts clearly specify how/when dividends are paid and disclaim that dividends are not guaranteed, paid only at the Board's discretion.

📋 The five dividend payment options

OptionHow it works
CashPaid directly to the policyholder (cheque/direct deposit), usually annually - can be spent, invested, or even used to buy the insurer's own shares
Premium reductionApplied to reduce the coming year's premium; if the dividend grows to exceed the premium (common after years in force), the excess is paid out or allocated elsewhere - also called "premium offset"
AccumulationDeposited into a separate side account, invested for growth; investment income is taxable; withdrawable anytime; unwithdrawn funds pass to the beneficiary at death
Paid-up additions (PUA)Used as a single premium to buy additional PAID-UP whole life coverage - no future premiums needed for that increment. Used by over 90% of whole life policies sold
Term insuranceUsed as a single premium to buy one-year term insurance on top of existing coverage
PUA highlight: PUAs require NO new medical evidence of insurability - a powerful way to increase coverage even if health has since deteriorated. Each PUA has its own death benefit and CSV, can be surrendered separately from the main policy, and its death benefit is paid tax-free.

📉 Impact of dividend choice on death benefit and CSV

OptionEffect
PUAMay increase BOTH the CSV and the death benefit

Dividend illustrations: agents often show a chart of projected CSV/death benefit growth based on the insurer's current dividend scale. Since mortality, expense, and investment factors can't be guaranteed, the scale can and does change - agents must ensure clients understand illustrations are NOT guarantees, just projections under current assumptions.

🔓 Non-forfeiture benefits: the core advantage over term

When a term policy is terminated or expires, the policyholder has nothing of value. Whole life policies, by contrast, typically offer NON-FORFEITURE BENEFITS - value the policyholder keeps even if he stops paying premiums. These benefits increase in value the longer the policy is in force (as CSV builds) and are negligible in the early years.

💵 Cash surrender value (CSV)

The CSV is the amount the insurer pays the policyholder if he TERMINATES (surrenders) the policy - coverage ends in exchange for immediate cash. A portion of the CSV is taxable upon receipt. In early years, premiums barely cover actual costs, so CSV is small or nonexistent; later, premiums exceed costs and the surplus builds the reserve that CSV draws from.

Surrender charges: insurers levy these against cash value to discourage early surrender and recoup issuance costs (underwriting, admin, commissions). They typically decrease over time and eventually disappear. For whole life, surrender charges usually aren't separately specified - instead, the guaranteed CSV illustration simply shows $0 for the first 3-10 years before gradually increasing.

🏧 Policy loans

A policyholder can typically borrow up to 90% of the policy's CSV from the insurer. No fixed payback schedule, but interest accrues at current rates. If the policy is later terminated, the CSV payout is reduced by the outstanding loan plus accrued interest; if the life insured dies with a loan outstanding, the death benefit is similarly reduced.

Worked example - Yolanda: $500,000 whole life policy, $120,000 CSV, takes an $80,000 loan at 5% for a cottage down payment. Two years later (no payments made), CSV has grown to $136,000, and she terminates the policy. She receives $136,000 − $80,000 − $8,200 accrued interest = $47,800. Had she died instead: $500,000 − $80,000 − $8,200 = $411,800 to her beneficiary.

🔄 Automatic premium loans (APL)

Most whole life policies offer this option once CSV is large enough to cover a missed premium. If the policyholder misses a scheduled payment, the insurer AUTOMATICALLY loans against the CSV to cover it (with interest charged) - preventing the policy from lapsing. This can apply repeatedly across multiple missed premiums, up to a maximum (usually 90-100% of CSV). If this limit is reached and premiums remain unpaid after the 30-day grace period, the policy terminates and any residual CSV is paid out.

Worked example - Ella: $500,000 policy, $226,000 CSV, $11,500 annual premium. Forced into early retirement, she uses APL to skip premiums for 5 years until her CPP/OAS begin. This reduces her death benefit by the unpaid premiums plus interest, but keeps the policy alive - and she can repay the loan later to restore the full death benefit.

📝 Reduced paid-up insurance

Lets the policyholder STOP paying premiums entirely while keeping SOME coverage in place for life - the CSV is used as a single premium to fund a smaller, fully paid-up whole life policy. The amount of paid-up coverage depends on the CSV size and the life insured's attained age. No medical evidence of insurability required, since there's no added risk to the insurer.

Continuing Ella's example: if she feels she'll never afford the $11,500 premium again, she could use her $226,000 CSV as a single premium for a smaller paid-up policy - less than the original $500,000, with the exact amount depending on her age.

⏳ Extended term insurance

Also lets the policyholder stop paying premiums entirely, but instead converts the CSV into TERM insurance for the SAME original death benefit amount - the trade-off is a limited TERM of coverage (rather than a reduced amount of permanent coverage), with the term length depending on the CSV size and attained age at conversion.

Continuing Ella's example: if she wants to keep the FULL $500,000 in place as long as possible rather than accept reduced coverage, she could use her $226,000 CSV to buy $500,000 of term insurance - the exact term length depending on her age.

🔢 Limited payment whole life

Provides lifelong coverage while only requiring premiums for a specified period (e.g. 10/20/25 years) or to a specific age (e.g. 65). Once the payment period ends, the policy becomes paid-up - coverage continues for life with no further premiums owed.

Worked example: Hamish buys a "25-pay life" policy at 45 - he pays premiums for 25 years (until age 70), but coverage continues right up until his death, whenever that occurs.

Limited payment premiums are HIGHER than ongoing/lifetime-pay premiums for the same coverage, since the insurer must collect the same total financial result over a shorter collection period. Benefits include: a known, fixed end date for payments (often timed to retirement), elimination of longevity risk (the risk of living - and having to keep paying premiums - longer than anticipated), and a time-value-of-money advantage to the insurer that can reduce the policyholder's long-term cost.

💸 Premium offset policies

When premiums owed are paid using dividends instead of out-of-pocket payments, they are said to be "offset." Two dividend options can lead to this: premium reduction (dividends applied directly against the premium) and PUA (dividend-purchased paid-up additions whose own CSV, combined with ongoing dividends, eventually contributes to covering premiums).

Historical caution - "vanishing premium" policies: in the 1970s-80s, insurers marketed par policies with aggressive dividend illustrations based on the high interest rates of that era, often showing premiums "vanishing" in as little as 10 years. When interest rates dropped sharply in the late 1980s-90s, dividend scales fell too - even a 1% drop significantly reduces dividends - and many policyholders who expected premiums to vanish in 8-10 years ended up paying for 20-30 years instead. Agents must clearly disclose that illustrations are based on the CURRENT (non-guaranteed) dividend scale, and that even small scale changes can dramatically affect how long premiums are actually required.

🧭 When whole life insurance is the right fit

As a general rule, whole life is more suitable than term for risks of UNKNOWN or LONG duration. Key considerations: how long coverage is needed, affordability, financial situation and income stability, willingness to pay premiums for life, need for increasing coverage, and investment objectives.

Use caseExample
Taxes upon deathHenry, 50, expects his cottage to triple in value before he dies around 90 - term coverage to 90+ isn't available, but whole life provides the protection needed for this open-ended timeline, with flexibility to terminate and use the CSV if his plans change
Future insurabilityEric, 45, already has health issues (overweight, high blood pressure, Type 2 diabetes) making coverage expensive now - he secures protection for his son Oliver by naming his younger, healthier spouse as contingent owner/life insured
Increasing coverageTony, 33, wants coverage to grow with his rising salary without new health-based pricing risk later (given his family's history of health issues starting around age 40) - a participating whole life policy with PUA option grows coverage via dividends, with no new insurability proof required

💯 Term-100 (T-100) life insurance

T-100 ("Term-to-100") combines elements of term and permanent insurance: level premiums like whole life, but typically WITHOUT a CSV or non-forfeiture benefits. Coverage virtually lasts for life since few people live past 100. Some policies pay the death benefit when the life insured reaches 100 (if still alive); others simply stop requiring premiums at 100 while coverage (and eventual death benefit payment) continues until actual death.

💲 T-100 premiums and the lapse risk

Premiums cease at age 100 regardless of whether the death benefit has been paid. Because T-100 typically has NO CSV, premiums are usually lower than equivalent whole life coverage (fewer features to fund) - but higher than term coverage stopping at 75-80, since T-100 protects for life.

Critical lapse risk: without a CSV, there's no automatic premium loan (APL) safety net. If a payment is accidentally missed and not corrected within the 30-day grace period, the policy LAPSES - becoming worthless even after decades of faithful premium payments. Some T-100 contracts allow reactivating a lapsed policy within a specified window (usually 2 years) if missed premiums are paid.
Level cost of insurance (LCOI): most T-100 policies guarantee level premiums for the duration (to age 100), with no CSV or non-forfeiture benefits - this premium structure is called LCOI. As with all permanent insurance, the younger the life insured at issue, the lower the lifetime premium.

🔟 Limited payment T-100

T-100 can also be issued on a limited-pay basis (premiums for a set number of years or to a specific age, then paid-up) - though not widely available in Canada today, though existing policies of this type do still exist. Limited payment T-100 commands a HIGHER premium than straight T-100 (same logic as limited payment whole life - collecting the same total result over a shorter period), and unlike standard T-100, these policies generally DO build a CSV and may even offer APL protection against missed payments.

🧭 When Term-100 is the right fit

T-100 may be appropriate when: the policyholder needs a FIXED amount of coverage for a need that won't increase before death (regardless of when that occurs); he's prepared to pay premiums until age 100; he's certain he'll never want to surrender the policy; he doesn't want a participating policy's added features/cost; and he has no need for universal life's investment flexibility.

Worked example - Trevor: after his wife's death, he's determined to leave $500,000 to the Hospice Centre that supported his family. He buys a $500,000 T-100 policy now while premiums are affordable, guaranteeing the gift even if he lives to 110 and depletes his other resources. Since he has no intention of ever cancelling the policy, he doesn't need (or want to pay for) a CSV-building product.
