Chapter 8 — Group Plan Specifics

Almost 68% of Canadian workplaces offer employees some form of non-wage benefits, primarily through group insurance plans. This chapter covers how group A&S insurance is structured, priced, funded, and administered — and the agent's role in the group plan process.

👥 Understanding the group

Unlike individual insurance, group members are automatically covered upon joining the plan provided they are actively at work. Since individual health data is absent, actuaries rely on general statistics and past claims experience. Key factors the insurer must understand:

Number of members: small groups (25 lives or less) cannot rely on the law of large numbers — claims experience may not resemble actuarial norms, resulting in higher premiums. Small groups are often pooled with similar groups for pricing. Large groups spread risk more widely and allow more accurate premium setting.
Age and gender: a primarily female membership is likely to file more health and disability claims than a primarily male group. Groups with predominantly older members are more likely to file drug and long-term care claims. Age/gender mix influences both the types of benefits offered and their pricing.
Turnover rate: the percentage of group members likely to leave and be replaced annually. Low turnover rates (5–10% per annum) offer consistency — past claims experience is more credible and attractive to insurers. High turnover rates diminish the value of past claims experience, resulting in higher premiums and longer waiting periods for benefits like disability and long-term care.
Nature of the business: construction businesses expose members to greater physical risks than office-based firms. However, some white-collar occupations involve longer hours and more stress. Architects, for example, traditionally have very low disability claims rates due to a low physical risk environment.
Comparative businesses: for smaller groups or first-time applicants without reliable claims history, insurers look to their own or the industry's claims experience with groups in the same or a comparable line of business to estimate future claims and set rates.

📋 Employee data sheet

When a prospective group plan member completes an enrolment card and the employer completes the employee census report, the insurer receives information used to determine claims projections and plan costing:

Date of birth: determines eligibility for certain benefits (e.g., LTD typically terminates at age 65) and helps determine the overall age mix for manual rating of disability and LTC benefits.
Gender: the gender mix is a significant factor in pricing disability income replacement benefits.
Occupation/occupational class: the plan may have different benefit packages for different classifications (e.g., executives vs. factory workers). Critical for determining which benefits a plan member qualifies for.
List of dependents: required if the group plan offers coverage for the plan member's spouse and children.
Social Insurance Number: required for reporting purposes if premiums or benefits are taxable to the group member.
Salary/earnings: used to determine disability income replacement benefit levels. For larger groups (more than 25 members), different employee classes may qualify for different benefit levels.

⚖️ Legal framework for personal data protection

PIPEDA (federal): applies to federally regulated businesses. Requires informed consent for any collection of personal data, used solely for the purposes for which it was collected.
Quebec Law 25: significantly enhances obligations — requires designating a person in charge of personal information protection; clearly informing employees of data use; implementing appropriate security measures; obtaining express consent for communicating to third persons (e.g., insurers); and conducting a privacy impact assessment for projects involving sensitive information.
Consequence: employers and agents must ensure all forms, the collection process, and communications meet these requirements — failing which they are liable to administrative penalties or civil proceedings.

🛡️ Products and services

Group products include: disability income replacement insurance (STD and LTD); extended health benefits (prescription drugs, vision care, dental, AD&D); critical illness (some plans only); and long-term care (less common). Group plans may also offer Employee Assistance Plans (EAPs) — a benefit not available from individual contracts.
Employee Assistance Plans (EAPs): provide counselling services outside traditional health care benefits. Designed to help attract and retain employees and to assist employees in overcoming personal or work-related stresses. Services are typically provided through off-site, third-party providers to keep use strictly confidential. Services include psychological counselling, addiction counselling, marriage counselling, and legal counselling. Use is solely at the discretion of the group plan member. Subject to annual deductibles, co-insurance, a maximum number of hours per annum, and a maximum allowable hourly rate.
EAP taxation: premiums paid by an employer for EAP benefits are tax-deductible for the employer. Benefits are not a taxable benefit to the employee for counselling relating to physical or mental health, retirement or financial counselling, and employment placement for terminated employees. Benefits for legal or financial counselling matters are generally a taxable benefit.

⚙️ Group insurer's services

Plan member enrolment: it is the employer's responsibility to enrol employees. A qualification (waiting) period — often three months, coinciding with the probationary period — typically applies before a new employee may join. For non-contributory plans with fixed benefits, enrolment is mandatory and completion of enrolment cards is the sum total of the process. For contributory plans, a minimum percentage of qualifying employees must participate. Optional enrolment windows (30–60 days) give employees time to decide without leaving the option open-ended indefinitely.
Premium billing: for non-contributory plans, the insurer bills the employer one amount monthly. For contributory plans, the employer receives two billing components — the employer's portion and the employees' contributory portion — and must payroll-deduct the employees' share and remit the total to the insurer.
Claims adjudication: the insurer compares the claim form to the contract terms to determine if the event is covered; assesses whether a benefit is payable and how much; makes appropriate payment (lump sum or periodic); and for periodic payments (disability, LTC), monitors the ongoing claim to ensure the claimant continues to qualify for benefits.

📊 Coverage and plan design comparison

Reasons to shop at renewal: price; desire for additional or more flexible benefits; dissatisfaction with service including claims turnaround times and percentage of claims denied.
Evaluating existing coverage: requires an in-depth analysis of plan design (covered conditions, benefit limits, definitions, deductibles, co-insurance), an evaluation of the group's claims experience, and an assessment of overall satisfaction with the current provider.
Claims experience as indicator: high claims may not simply reflect sicker members — the plan design itself (very broad coverage, liberal definitions, high maximums, no deductibles or waiting periods) may encourage a high level of claims. Both factors must be evaluated. An unusually high past claims experience might be attributed as much to plan design as to plan members, particularly if there is no EAP in place.
Comparing plans: switching carriers should not be decided on price alone — a lower price may indicate deficiencies. Key comparison points include: disability benefit definitions, waiting and benefit periods, percentage of income covered, rehabilitation services; drug benefits (deductibles, co-insurance, drugs covered, brand vs. generic, pay-direct or reimbursement basis); vision care; dental (deductibles, co-insurance, covered procedures, annual/lifetime maximums); LTC and CI coverage; and EAP inclusion.

💰 Funding formulas

Non-refund accounting (fully insured/pooled): the insurer bears full financial responsibility for paying all claims. If actual claims exceed anticipated costs, the plan sponsor has no liability for the excess. If claims are less than anticipated, the insurer keeps the windfall — there is no premium refund to the plan sponsor. However, excess claims will be reflected in higher premiums at renewal.
Refund accounting (retention accounting): a form of participating insurance for the plan sponsor. If the group's claims experience is better than premiums were priced for, the employer receives a partial refund of premiums paid. If actual experience is worse than anticipated, the insurer can recoup losses at contract renewal.
Administrative Services Only (ASO): the insurer provides only administrative services (record-keeping, pricing, adjudicating and paying claims) — there is no insurance element. The plan sponsor assumes all risk of paying claims. Most appropriate for large organizations with thousands of employees and substantial assets and cash flow able to absorb claims costs.

💳 Premium payment structure

Non-contributory: all premiums are paid by the plan sponsor (usually the employer). 100% of qualifying employees must join the plan.
Contributory: some or all premiums are paid by group members. Participation is usually optional, but a minimum percentage of qualifying employees must participate to ensure a broad cross-section of risk. Members often have more freedom in selecting benefits, though certain coverages may be mandatory. Group association plans are typically fully contributory since there is no incentive for the plan sponsor to pay premiums on behalf of members.

📈 Premium rates

Manual rating (pooled): used when a prospective group has no previous coverage or unreliable claims history. Sets premium standards for groups of similar size and makeup within the same industry sector, based on their past claims experience.
Experience rating: for groups renewing coverage. Premiums are based on an estimate of future claims derived from the group's past claims experience. Valid only when the group is sufficiently large for past statistics to be meaningful and the group makeup has not changed significantly.
Blended rating: most common approach. A blend of manual and experience rating. The degree to which past claims experience weighs on pricing is determined by credibility.
Credibility: determines how reliable experience rating is in a given case. A large, highly stable group with long-term coverage and low turnover has high credibility — past experience reliably predicts future claims. Smaller groups with high turnover and short histories have low credibility. Groups with no previous coverage have null credibility — premiums based exclusively on manual rating. Master Mobile example: 37 employees, 18% turnover, 4 years of coverage — credibility rated at 40%, meaning 40% experience rating and 60% manual rating.

🧾 Taxation and group plan registration

LTD premium tax treatment: employer-paid group premiums for disability, extended health, and other group A&S insurance are always tax-deductible for the employer. If the employer pays 100% of the LTD premium, disability benefits are taxable income to the recipient. If the employee pays 100% of the LTD premium, benefits are tax-free (but premiums are not deductible for the employee). If premiums are shared, the employee receives a tax-free portion of benefits equal to the aggregate premiums they paid, with the balance being taxable. Most contributory plans structure employee contributions to pay the LTD premium entirely, making the full LTD benefit tax-free to the employee.
Shawna example: disability benefits of $1,200/month; employer and employee share premiums 50/50; each has paid $3,600 in premiums. Shawna receives $9,600 in benefits over 10 months. Her first $3,600 (equal to her premiums paid) is tax-free; the remaining $6,000 is taxable income.
ESDC registration of STD plans: if an employer has STD group coverage equal to or greater than EI sickness benefits (26 weeks, 55% of weekly insurable earnings), the employer can apply for a partial reduction in EI premiums payable. This is because EI is a second payer to group insurance coverage. The employer must register the group plan with ESDC.
Provincial premium tax: premiums paid for all forms of group A&S and extended health coverage are subject to provincial premium tax, generally between 2% and 3.48% of the net premium. The tax applies whether paid by the insured plan member or an employer and is included in the overall premium quoted — not broken out as a separate charge. Jorge example: $150.81/month = $147.06 base premium + $3.75 premium tax (2.55%).

🔧 Cost drivers and claims administration

Reducing claim frequency: deductibles and co-insurance factors require the plan member to pay part of any claim, discouraging unnecessary claims. Extending the waiting period for disability eliminates short-term claims. For drug benefits, eliminating brand name and non-prescription drug coverage reduces exposure. A contributory plan — particularly for STD — may paradoxically increase claims since members who have paid for coverage may feel entitled to use it.
Claims administration process: (1) receive notification on insurer claim form with proof of claim; (2) confirm the group plan is in force and the claimant is a member; (3) determine whether the products or services are covered; (4) adjudicate the claim; (5) make appropriate payment (lump sum or periodic). For small groups (25 or less), the agent may be called upon to communicate insurer requirements and processes directly to members.

🔄 Co-ordination of benefits (COB)

COB rules: where a claimant is covered by two group plans (their own plan and a spouse's plan), COB rules determine which plan pays first and how much each pays. The claimant's own plan (as primary insured) is always the first payer. The spouse's plan is the second payer. The entire expense is filed with the second payer, which pays the lesser of: what it would have paid as first payer; or the difference between the total expense and what the first payer paid.
Children's claims: the plan covering the parent with the birth date earliest in the calendar year — regardless of age — is the first payer.
Jasmine and Abdul example: dental claim of $2,500. Jasmine's plan (first payer): $100 deductible; pays $1,500 (annual maximum). Uncovered balance: $1,000. Abdul's plan (second payer): would have paid $2,000 as first payer (after $50 deductible). Pays the lesser of $2,000 or $1,000 = $1,000. Combined total recovered: $1,500 + $1,000 = $2,500 (fully covered).

🤝 Agent's service role

Minimum role: if not actively involved in group business, the agent's role may be simply identifying a group prospect and introducing them to a group representative or account executive. If the plan is successfully placed, the agent receives a commission.
Active role: if directly involved in sales and service, the agent has a two-pronged responsibility — remaining up-to-date on the client's needs and situation, and documenting all services provided. The agent may also be involved in education and promotion programs to encourage employee participation in new or replacement plans.
Ongoing awareness: the agent should contact the client 90 days prior to the plan's yearly renewal date to assess whether plan modifications are required or whether the client wishes to shop for an alternate provider. The agent acts as a conduit between the client and the insurance company to anticipate and head off dissatisfaction issues.
Documentation: the agent must document every step of the service process — dates of contact, what was discussed, what action was taken, and how matters were resolved. Copies of all written communications (quotes, letters, emails) must be retained. Details of phone conversations should be recorded in writing. All records could be vital in the event of a claim or legal dispute.
