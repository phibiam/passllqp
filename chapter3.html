Chapter 3 - Annuities

This lesson covers everything in depth - payout vs accumulation annuities, every guarantee type, funding rules, taxation, FINTRAC, and the new ALDA/VPLA products. Take your time and work through it like a textbook chapter.

💰 What is an annuity?

An annuity converts a lump sum into regular income payments. You give the insurance company your capital and they pay you back over time partly as return of capital and partly as interest.

Think of it as the opposite of life insurance: life insurance protects against dying too early; a life annuity protects against living too long.

Two core types: Payout annuities (pay regular income) and Accumulation annuities (save and grow, also called insurance GICs - 100% of capital is returned to the investor).

👥 Who's who in an annuity contract

The annuitant (measuring life) is the person whose lifespan determines how long payments are made. The payee receives the payments. The investor (policy owner) deposits the capital. These can all be the same person, or different people, depending on the contract.

Registered funds rule: If an annuity is funded with RRSP or RRIF savings, the policy owner MUST also be the annuitant and payee - he cannot name someone else to receive the income.

Immediate vs deferred: Immediate annuity starts payments right away (must be funded with a lump sum). Deferred annuity starts payments at a future date with an accumulation phase first - and can typically offer a higher payment guarantee since the insurer holds the deposit longer before paying out.

🏥 Impaired life annuities

An impaired (also called enhanced, age-rated, or accelerated) life annuity is issued when the annuitant has a shortened life expectancy due to serious illness. The annuitant must provide a medical certificate proving substandard health.

Once impairment is proven, the annuitant typically receives a HIGHER payment than someone of the same age and gender in standard health, since the insurer expects to pay out over a shorter period. This applies to both single life and joint life impaired annuitants.

🛡️ Guarantees: protecting capital

A guarantee period (e.g. 5, 10, or 20 years) ensures that if the annuitant dies before the period ends, the beneficiary receives a payment for the REMAINING guaranteed payments - either as a lump sum (commuted) or in instalments. If the annuitant outlives the guarantee period, no further payment is owed.

A capital protection guarantee goes further - it returns the FULL difference between the original capital and the payments already received, regardless of any specific time period. If paid as a lump sum, it's called a cash refund annuity; if paid in instalments, an instalment refund annuity.

No guarantee = real risk: A life annuity without any guarantee pays at a HIGHER rate, but if the annuitant dies shortly after payments begin, any remaining capital stays with the insurer - nothing goes to the estate or any beneficiary.

💵 Term annuity payout options on death

If the annuitant of a term certain annuity dies during the term, the policy owner can typically choose between three payout methods for the beneficiary:

MethodHow it works
Instalment refundBeneficiary receives the remaining monthly payments, spread out over what's left of the term
Cash refund (commuted value)Beneficiary receives a lump sum equal to the present value of remaining payments
Return of premiumBeneficiary receives what's left of the original deposit (deposit minus payments already made)

🏦 Funding an annuity

An immediate annuity must be funded with a lump sum, since payments must begin within the first year. A deferred annuity can be funded with a lump sum or a series of regular deposits - but no payments can begin until after the last premium has been paid.

Locked-in funds rule: Money from a LIRA, LIF, or locked-in pension plan can ONLY fund a life annuity (never a term certain annuity). If the policy owner has a spouse, this generally must be a JOINT life annuity to protect the spouse's income - unless the spouse waives that right in writing.

🔍 FINTRAC and large cash transactions

Agents must follow FINTRAC client identification requirements when an individual purchases an annuity or segregated fund costing $10,000+ over the policy's duration, paid in cash (not by transfer).

Reporting thresholds: A single cash transaction of $10,000+, or multiple cash transactions totalling $10,000+ within a 24-hour period, requires a Large Cash Transaction report. Suspicious behaviour requires a Suspicious Transaction Report REGARDLESS of dollar amount.

👶 T-90 and T-18 annuities

A T-90 is a term certain annuity to age 90 - an option for someone whose RRSP/RRIF is maturing and wants to continue tax deferral via an income product rather than a life annuity.

A T-18 is for a financially dependent child under 18 who inherits RRSP/RRIF proceeds from a deceased parent or grandparent. The lump sum transfers in tax-free, and the child receives payments (taxed at their own marginal rate) until just before turning 19.

💸 Taxation of annuities

Registered annuity (funded from RRSP or RRIF) - Every payment is 100% taxable as ordinary income. No return-of-capital component.

Non-registered prescribed annuity - Each payment blends capital (non-taxable) and interest (taxable). The SAME taxable amount every year. Predictable tax burden.

Non-registered accrual annuity - Interest is front-loaded in early years. More tax early, less later. Less tax-efficient than prescribed in terms of timing.

Prescribed requirements: Must not be indexed. Annuitant, payee and owner must be the same person (unless joint life with spouse/sibling as co-annuitant). Payments must begin no later than Dec 31 of the year following purchase.
Important: The TOTAL tax paid over the life of a prescribed vs. accrual annuity is generally the SAME - the difference is only the TIMING (accrual front-loads tax; prescribed spreads it evenly).

📉 Capital loss on annuity shortfall

If an annuitant dies and the total payments received under a non-registered prescribed annuity are LESS than the original premium paid, the shortfall is recognized as a capital loss.

This capital loss can offset capital gains on the final tax return, and any unused portion can be carried back 3 years or forward indefinitely - similar to capital loss treatment for other investments.

💰 Tax-saving features: income splitting and the pension credit

Income splitting: up to 50% of taxable payout annuity income can be transferred to a spouse, producing tax savings when the spouse is in a lower marginal tax bracket (subject to attribution rules).

Pension income tax credit: a federal credit of up to $2,000 annually, available to annuitants 65+ (or younger if receiving payments from a deceased spouse's registered annuity).

🏛️ The insured annuity strategy

An insured annuity combines a permanent life insurance policy (e.g. T-100 or whole life) with a single life annuity. The annuity provides maximum income during life; the life insurance restores the capital to beneficiaries, tax-free, at death.

Critical order of operations: The life insurance policy MUST be purchased FIRST. If the proposed policy owner cannot qualify for life insurance due to health, the entire strategy cannot be implemented. Once insurance is secured, the annuity is funded with enough capital that its payment covers at least the insurance premium.

⚠️ Key risks and limitations

Interest rate risk - Rates locked in at purchase forever. Cannot renegotiate. Managed by laddering annuity purchases over time.

Inflation risk - Fixed payment buys less every year. Managed with indexed annuities (which pay less initially to fund future increases).

Liquidity/lack of flexibility - Once a payout annuity starts, you generally cannot surrender it, withdraw from it, or change its terms.

Loss of capital - Life annuity without guarantee: if you die early, remaining capital stays with the insurer.

Market value adjustment (MVA) - A penalty that may apply on early withdrawal from an accumulation annuity, based on time to maturity, interest rate changes, and insurer expenses.

🔐 Assuris protection and creditor protection

Assuris for payout annuities: monthly payments up to $5,000 fully protected; above $5,000, protected at the greater of $5,000 or 90% of the promised benefit.
Assuris for accumulation annuities: 100% up to $100,000, or 90% of contract value, whichever is higher.

Creditor protection may apply when a preferred beneficiary (spouse, child, parent, grandchild) or an irrevocable beneficiary is named - not the estate. The contract must have been purchased well before any insolvency to qualify.

🆕 New annuity types: ALDA and VPLA

ALDA (Advanced Life Deferred Annuity) - Purchased with registered funds (RRSP, RRIF, DPSP, PRPP, DCPP). Payments deferred until the end of the year the annuitant turns 85. Max 25% of qualifying plan value, up to $180,000 (2025), indexed to inflation. Cannot be surrendered or provide guarantees. Funds transferred are excluded from RRIF/PRPP/DCPP minimum withdrawal calculations after the year of purchase - addressing longevity risk while preserving flexibility with the remaining funds.

VPLA (Variable Payment Life Annuity) - For PRPP or DCPP members. Payments vary based on investment performance of the pooled fund and the mortality experience of the group. Minimum 10 retired members required to establish a VPLA arrangement. No guarantees - payments can rise or fall.
