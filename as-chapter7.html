Chapter 7 — Insurance Recommendation, Contract and Service Needs

Once the client profile is complete, the insurance process moves through six key stages: recommendation, application, underwriting, contract delivery, claims, and ongoing client service. This chapter covers how agents build and present recommendations, complete applications accurately, understand how insurers assess risk, deliver and explain contracts, guide clients through the claims process, and provide ongoing service throughout the relationship.

📋 Insurance recommendation: basis, premium management, and documentation

Basis for the recommendation: starts with the client profile — goals, needs, wants, and available resources. Compare identified needs against current resources (income, assets, existing insurance) to find shortfalls and gaps. First consider whether existing policies can be modified (coverage changes or riders added). Then recommend new products to complement existing coverage and complete the portfolio.
Managing premiums — extending waiting period: a longer waiting period (e.g., extending 60 days to 90 days) substantially reduces premiums if the client has enough resources to self-fund during the longer wait.
Managing premiums — shortening benefit period: a shorter benefit period reduces the insurer's maximum payout and therefore reduces premiums. Care must be taken to ensure the shorter period still meets the client's needs.
Managing premiums — reducing benefits: the amount of benefit can be reduced, which is the most radical solution since the recommended benefit amount should be what the client actually needs. This is a last resort. Jason's example: $50,000/year income → 60% = $2,500/month ideal, but premium too high → budgeting reveals $1,900/month covers actual needs at a lower premium.
Managing premiums — nature of the contract: if a non-cancellable contract is too expensive, switching to a guaranteed renewable contract provides more affordable premiums while still providing solid coverage. Always recommend the best affordable contract that meets the client's needs.
Head-office modifications for non-standard risks: insurers classify applicants as standard, non-standard, or uninsurable (decline). For non-standard risks, the insurer may: impose exclusions (activities or conditions not covered), impose limitations (restrictions on benefit amount or period), rate the premium (higher than standard), or impose a deductible on claims. The agent may anticipate modifications based on disclosed health information and prepare the client in advance.
Providing quotes: two schools of thought: (1) present only the best single solution — less complex for the client; or (2) present two suitable options — client is more engaged. If presenting options, use a clear comparison chart showing similarities and key differences. Too many choices lead to analysis paralysis. Focus on material differences (e.g., two CI policies with different stroke definitions — level of neurological impairment required and days before a declaration can be made).
Documenting the recommendation: rigorously document all recommendations in writing and verbally. Include a clear rationale explaining how the client's needs are addressed. Record client expectations. The Horst example: when a competitor challenged his recommendation, Horst's documentation of five products researched and a comparison grid successfully defended his due diligence.
Client expectations: document the type and amount of insurance the client feels they need, their expectation of qualifying as standard, their cost expectations, etc. Address any divergences at the delivery interview — if serious divergences exist, prepare a waiver for the client to sign acknowledging the differences. Record how the recommendation meets or does not meet each expressed expectation and why.
Presenting complementary policies: sometimes one policy cannot meet all needs and a combination is required. Elijah and Malik example: business needs $10,000/month for up to 2 years (key person DI, 180-day wait, payable to company); Malik's family needs $3,000/month for 5 years (personal DI, 60-day wait, payable to Malik). Two policies are recommended even if the client cannot purchase both immediately — disclose remaining gaps.
Revising the recommendation: the recommendation cannot be take-it-or-leave-it. If the product is too expensive or lacks desired features, the agent has both a moral and legal obligation to restructure the recommendation to be acceptable while still meeting the client's coverage needs as closely as possible.

📝 Application for insurance: process and accuracy

The application is the cornerstone of the issue process: subsequent underwriting is based on the information provided. The agent must gather information that is as complete and accurate as possible. The applicant is a lay person entitled to rely on the agent's expertise to interpret questions and explain expected answers. Inaccurate or incomplete applications cause delays at best; incorrect coverage or lawsuits from denied claims based on misrepresentation at worst.
Application components requiring care: product type and coverage details; owner, life insured, and beneficiary (in business cases these may differ — key person: business is owner and beneficiary; Blanche's case: if she is beneficiary, she reports premiums as taxable benefit; if business is beneficiary, it pays benefits to her and the entire benefit becomes taxable, not just premiums); premium quoted (may differ from final if rated); riders applied for (separate underwriting and pricing).
Naming beneficiaries in business cases: in most personal policies, the policyholder, life insured, and beneficiary are the same individual. For business policies, the correct beneficiary matters for tax reasons. Key person: business is both owner and beneficiary. Grouped disability plan where benefits replace salary: if the employee is beneficiary, premiums are a taxable benefit to the employee; if the business is beneficiary, it pays the employee as salary continuation — the entire benefit to the employee is taxable (not just premiums). Either structure is acceptable but both parties must understand the tax consequences.
Agent's contribution to the application: the agent is not just a recorder — they can proactively solicit information the underwriting department will need even if not explicitly on the form. Agent's comments section: document additional context the underwriter needs (e.g., planned job change and expected income reduction; pending raise to $100,000 in 6 months; cigarette smell on a self-declared non-smoker). The company is deemed to be in possession of all information the agent knows — whether speculative or not.
Medical questions: the agent interprets questions for the applicant and records answers fully. Simply recording "last July, check-up" for a doctor visit leaves many questions unanswered for underwriters — was it scheduled or triggered by a problem? What symptoms? What tests? What were the results? Any treatment prescribed? The agent must prompt the applicant to expand upon answers where necessary.
Inspection report: ordered by head office underwriting (not the agent) — a third-party investigation of lifestyle, spending habits, hobbies, dangerous sports, drinking habits, etc. Authorized by the applicant's signature on the application.
Hazardous sports/occupation questionnaires: if the applicant engages in hazardous activities (private flying, skydiving) or risky occupations (firefighter), a special questionnaire is required detailing the activity and its frequency. A sufficiently risky activity may result in an exclusion rider denying coverage for any claim arising from that activity.
Necessary documents: medical exam (required based on age, coverage amount, or disclosed medical history); income confirmation (T4, T5, T1 return + Notice of Assessment — often two years; for business applications: financial statements for at least 3 years); replacement coverage forms (if replacing existing coverage, a prescribed comparison form must be completed covering premium, conditions covered, exclusions, riders, expiration, waiting/benefit periods, and definitions). New policy must be issued BEFORE existing policy is cancelled — never leave the client uninsured.

🔍 Underwriting by insurer: medical, financial, and premium factors

Two types of underwriting: financial (verify income level to determine maximum coverage entitled and ability to pay premiums long-term); medical (determine whether the applicant is an acceptable claims risk from a health perspective).
Factors affecting premiums: administrative costs (higher costs = higher premiums); investment returns (higher returns = lower premiums); lapse rates (higher lapse = potentially fewer claims = lower premiums for others); morbidity rates (higher rates of illness/injury = higher premiums); ratings and exclusions (ratings are higher premiums for non-standard risks; exclusions limit benefits for specific conditions — both lead to lower premiums for standard risks).
Morbidity rates: morbidity = instance of disability (injury or illness) in the population. Morbidity tables predict how many persons of a given age and gender will become disabled — they work best with large populations and are less reliable for predicting claims within a single insurer's smaller pool. Tables show number of past claims, causes, and duration — not dollar exposure per claim. The degree to which actual claims exceed or fall short of pricing assumptions impacts profits and future premium pricing.
Financial underwriting: DI coverage is restricted to a percentage of pre-disability income coordinated with all other sources (group, government) — typically 60-70%, up to 85% — to prevent the insured from being better off financially while disabled than while working (which could encourage anti-selection: filing false claims or malingering valid claims). Proof of income (T4, T5, T1, Notice of Assessment, business financial statements) is required. Insurers want assurance that the applicant can maintain premiums long-term; an applicant applying for coverage they obviously cannot afford raises red flags — possibly wanting coverage anticipating an imminent claim.
Attending Physician's Statement (APS): the insurer may seek input from the applicant's personal physician or other attending physicians. The APS is requested directly between head office underwriting and the physician — cost is borne by the insurer. May be triggered automatically by age, coverage amount, or disclosed medical history. May require additional tests (paramed exam, blood/urine tests, electrocardiogram). Jorge's example: disclosed "minor" heart attack 4 years ago → insurer required APS + medical with stress test.
Medical Information Bureau (MIB): an organization that compiles historical data on insurance applicants. Insurers report coded information to the MIB and also query the MIB when assessing applications. The coded information provides general health "markers" (not specific enough to underwrite alone) that trigger the insurer to seek additional information. The MIB also tracks current coverage in force, current applications, and prior declines — preventing applicants from seeking coverage from a second insurer without disclosing a prior decline or the reason for it. Applicants authorize MIB data sharing when signing the application.

📄 Insurance contract: delivery, disclosure, and policy features

Contract formation: the application is the "offer to treat." If the insurer issues the contract (as applied for or with modifications) and delivers it to the applicant, this is the insurer's offer. The applicant's acceptance + payment of the first premium completes the contract. Once in force (including the application), the policy is the basis of all future rights and obligations.
Prompt delivery is critical: provincial insurance acts require that the contract is delivered; the initial premium is paid; and neither the financial situation nor the health of the life insured has changed since application. If income has declined since application, the coverage may be excessive and the insured may not qualify to claim the full amount; if income has increased, no reporting to insurer is required. If health has declined, the offer must be re-assessed — do not deliver the contract; return it to the insurer with notes for re-underwriting. Lucille's example: disclosed angina onset after application → agent returned contract to insurer for reassessment instead of delivering it.
10-day "free look" period: convention allows the new policyholder 10 days from the date of physical delivery to return the contract for cancellation and a full premium refund. The period does not start until the policy is physically delivered — so delay in delivery extends the free look window and prolongs the period during which the applicant might decline the coverage.
Delivering rated cases: a policy issued with increased premiums, exclusions, or reduced benefits may upset the applicant. Steps: (1) warn the applicant during application if health issues might lead to a rating; (2) if the rating may be temporary, explain this; (3) gently reinforce that the need for coverage has not changed — and the additional risk factors make coverage even more important. An applicant who rejects a rated policy may be left entirely without needed protection.
Contract disclosure at delivery: explain: benefits and coverage limits; riders; key definitions of covered conditions (particularly for CI); exclusions. Definitions and exclusions deserve special attention — the client may assume all manifestations of a condition are covered and not realize the specific severity thresholds that must be met. Exclusions are typically added by head office after the application was taken — the client may not be aware the coverage they expected has been removed. The client (and family for CI and LTC) should clearly understand what IS and is NOT covered.
Statutory provisions limiting coverage: where coverage from different contracts overlaps, the insured cannot collect more than once for the same loss (benefits supplement but cannot duplicate). Jorge's example: group DI pays $2,500/month; individual DI "tops up" to $3,000/month total — but he cannot collect more than $3,000/month combined. For DI: all-source maximum of 85% of pre-disability income from all combined sources (groups, government, private). For CI: no specific limits on benefit amounts (not tied to income or expenses). For LTC: cannot be reimbursed more than actual expenses incurred regardless of overlapping coverage.
Tax treatment of premiums and benefits: common questions at delivery ("Can I deduct premiums?" and "Will benefits be taxable?") should be addressed carefully. The agent should focus on items discussed during the recommendation process. The agent is not a tax expert and should avoid definitive statements on complex tax situations — always refer the client to their accountant or tax lawyer for specific tax advice.
Policy feature opportunities (FPO, conversion, rating review): FPO rider — allows the policyholder to acquire additional coverage at pre-established dates with little or no medical underwriting (subject to financial underwriting). Consuela's example: originally purchased $2,500/month on $90,000 income; FPO allows $500 additional without medical underwriting at the option date; she now needs $4,000/month — exercises the $500 FPO option and applies for an additional $1,000 with full underwriting. Conversion options — group disability plans often allow conversion to individual policy within 31 days of leaving the group plan without medical evidence. Rating/exclusion review — if a medical condition has resolved or the insured no longer engages in the hazardous activity that caused the rating, they may apply to have the rating amended or removed — reducing premiums and potentially freeing funds for additional coverage.

⚕️ Policy claims: requirements, process, and factors reducing benefits

Claims process overview: (1) Insured notifies the insurer (or agent) of the event; (2) Insurer provides claim forms; (3) Insured completes forms and submits with supporting information; (4) Insurer may seek further information (physician statements, additional tests, physical exam); (5) Insurer adjudicates and pays, partially pays, or denies the claim. Most insurers require notification within 30 days but never more than 6 months. Advise clients to notify as soon as possible after any event that may trigger a claim.
Agent's role in claims: a potential conflict of interest exists since the agent represents both the insurer and the insured. Some insurers restrict agents to delivering blank claim forms and returning completed forms — not assisting with completion. The agent should always follow the carrier's guidelines.
Supporting documentation required: receipts (for extended health, dental, chiropractic, prescription drugs — originals required). Medical proof of diagnosis: DI — inability to work due to injury or illness; CI — diagnosed critical illness meeting the policy's definition (heart attack, stroke, etc.); LTC — inability to perform at least 2 ADLs; BOE — inability to work and contribute to the business; extended health — assessment of proposed treatment. The insurer always requires a physician's report on the nature, degree, and prognosis of the condition. For DI, ongoing proof of treatment (physiotherapist, chiropractor) may be required periodically.
Factors that may reduce or eliminate benefits: standard exclusions (suicide, alcohol/drug abuse, criminal activity, normal pregnancy); material misrepresentation at time of application (if discovered, policy may be voided or claims denied); change in financial situation (if income at time of claim is less than reported at application, DI benefits may be reduced); all-source maximum provisions (if total benefits from all sources would exceed the contractual maximum, benefits are scaled back).

🤝 Client service and pandemic considerations

Agent's service role: provide claim forms and other relevant documents; be prompt and accurate (delay could result in uncovered events, health changes before policy delivery, or unprocessed beneficiary changes during which a claim might occur); develop a strategy for ongoing awareness of client situation and needs; stay in touch annually or semi-annually; schedule regular review appointments. Seng's example: stresses to clients they must contact him immediately if there is any significant change in employment status — income change, employer change, duties change, or unemployment.
Documenting services: record the date and content of all client contacts (who initiated, what was discussed, what action was taken, when resolved). Retain copies of all written communications including applications, forms, waivers, and letters. This documentation protects both the agent and the client and is vital evidence in the event of a claim or legal dispute.
Pandemic and COVID-19 considerations for DI: quarantine or curfew compliance alone (with or without symptoms) is NOT sufficient for a DI claim — it is not an illness. COVID-19 with documented medical complications and physician confirmation of inability to work = may be covered like any other disability. COVID-19 without medical complications = not covered by DI; government programs may apply. BOE insurance: requires physician statement confirming inability to work for medical reasons — ceasing work as a preventive measure or due to lockdown alone is not sufficient.
Pandemic considerations for CI and LTC: CI claims from COVID-19 complications must satisfy existing contractual definitions of covered illnesses (no definition called "COVID-19"). Pre-existing conditions: no changes to grandfathering of disclosed pre-existing conditions; however, insurers consider COVID-19 claims experience in underwriting new contracts. LTC: loss of ADLs must stem from physical inability included in the contract clauses — if disclosed following COVID-19 complications, may be covered. Travel insurance during a pandemic: medical emergencies generally covered whether or not from COVID-19; quarantine costs and additional hotel stays may or may not be covered depending on the specific contract — always check with the insurer before travelling. Vaccines: if a covered condition (e.g., stroke) results from a vaccine, the claim is based on the resulting disability or illness, not on the vaccine itself.
