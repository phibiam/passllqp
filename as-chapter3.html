Chapter 3 — Insurance to Protect Assets

Neither life insurance nor disability insurance protect an insured's assets from erosion due to catastrophic illness or the ongoing costs of long-term care. Critical illness (CI) insurance and long-term care (LTC) insurance fill this gap — providing living benefits to help protect assets when the insured survives a major health event or can no longer care for themselves independently.

🏥 Critical illness (CI) insurance: overview and coverage provided

CI insurance provides a tax-free lump-sum benefit to an insured who survives a covered condition. There are no restrictions on how the benefit is used: paying off debt; alternative treatments; home renovations (ramps, widened doorways for wheelchair); reducing work hours; medical expenses not covered by provincial plans; vacations to reduce stress; changing to a less demanding job; etc.

Issue ages: adults typically aged 18–65 (risk too high beyond 65 for most insurers). Children's plans are also available with lower benefits and conditions specific to minors.
Face amounts: minimum $10,000, maximum $3,000,000; typical range $25,000–$500,000. Actual coverage depends on age, financial status, health, medical history, and family medical history.
Qualification period (30-day exclusionary clause): conditions first diagnosed or manifesting within 30 days of policy issue are NOT covered. Some insurers extend this to 90 days for specific conditions like cancer, to guard against anti-selection (applicants seeking coverage because they suspect they may already have a covered condition).
Survival (waiting) period: the insured must survive the covered condition for at least 30 days after diagnosis before benefits become payable. Some conditions may have a 90-day waiting period. Benefits are still payable to the insured's estate if the insured survives the waiting period but dies before payment is approved.
Duration of coverage: term policies (10 or 20 years, or to age 65 or 75) — guaranteed renewable with increasing premiums at renewal to partly offset adverse selection; convertible to permanent coverage up to age 65. Permanent policies run to age 100 (similar to Term-100 life insurance). Most policies expire after one successful claim — however, some offer a second event rider permitting a lesser benefit for a subsequent unrelated condition.

📋 CI policy types and conditions covered

Basic (3 or 4 conditions): the "Big 4" — heart attack, stroke, cancer, and (for 4-condition contracts) coronary bypass surgery. Most CI policies cover at minimum these conditions.
Extended (10 conditions): includes the Big 4 plus blindness, deafness, kidney failure, multiple sclerosis, organ transplant, and paralysis.
Comprehensive (26+ conditions): adds Alzheimer's, aortic surgery, aplastic anemia, bacterial meningitis, benign brain tumor, coma, heart valve replacement, loss of independent existence, loss of limbs, loss of speech, major organ failure (on waiting list), motor neuron disease, occupational HIV infection, Parkinson's disease, severe burns, and others. More conditions = higher premium.
Children's plans: typically cover most adult conditions plus conditions specific to minors — muscular dystrophy, Type 1 diabetes, cerebral palsy, cystic fibrosis, congenital heart disease. Often convertible to adult plans at age 18 or 25.
Special CI products: mortgage-linked CI (minimal underwriting, benefit pays off outstanding mortgage balance, underwriting done at time of claim); guaranteed issue contracts (brief declaration of good health only, typically limited to under $100,000, higher premiums); CI riders on life insurance policies (basic 3–4 illness coverage at relatively low additional premium).

🔧 CI riders: return of premium and waiver of premium

Return of premium (ROP) — three triggers: (1) On death — premiums refunded to estate/named beneficiary if insured dies from a non-covered cause, or from a covered condition but before completing the qualification and waiting periods; (2) On surrender — premiums refunded (in whole or part) if policyholder cancels after a prescribed number of years (e.g., 15) with no claim filed; (3) On maturity — premiums refunded when the policy expires (e.g., at age 75) with no claim filed.
Waiver of premium: added at policy issue (requires medical qualification and additional premium). Waives all CI policy premiums if the insured becomes totally disabled. Subject to a 4–6 month waiting period from disability onset. Available only if insured is age 55 or younger at application. Premiums paid during the waiting period may be refunded. Waiver ends at the earlier of: recovery from disability; a successful CI claim; or policy expiry. Note: the disability triggering the waiver is different from conditions that trigger CI benefits (e.g., a minor stroke may trigger the waiver but not be severe enough to trigger the CI benefit).

⚡ CI payment of benefits, tax treatment, and limitations

Payment conditions: condition must be diagnosed more than 30 days after policy issue; insured must survive for 30+ days post-diagnosis; claim must be filed within 30 days of diagnosis; medical proof must be provided within 90 days of filing. Benefits are normally a one-time lump-sum payment; policy is cancelled after a successful claim. Some policies permit instalment payments or offer a second event rider.
Standard exclusions: war; terrorism; attempted suicide; self-inflicted injuries; normal pregnancy; criminal activities; abuse of non-prescription drugs; non-occupational AIDS/HIV.
Tax treatment: premiums are NOT tax-deductible and do NOT qualify for the Medical Expense Tax Credit. CI benefits received (lump sum) are NOT taxable. Return of premium benefits are also NOT taxable.
Key limitations: contract wording is crucial — only conditions meeting the precise contractual definition qualify. Definitions evolve and may differ between contracts issued at different times. Over 60% of CI insurer claim denials relate to limitations/exclusions, pre-existing conditions, not meeting definitions, and survival/waiting periods (AMF data). The insurer's medical advisors have final say on qualification — the insured must resort to courts if they disagree.

👴 Long-term care (LTC): demographics and care options

Long-term care is required by individuals of any age who can no longer independently manage daily physical needs — young people with muscular dystrophy or MS, those with brain/spinal injuries, or seniors with Alzheimer's or dementia. However, need is primarily concentrated in those over 65.

Canadian demographics: on July 1, 2023, for the first time in Canadian history, there were more people aged 65+ (7,568,308) than people under 18 (7,497,048). The proportion aged 65+ reached 18.9% in 2023. Baby boomers are the primary driver — the youngest cohorts will continue reaching age 65 until 2030. By 2024, over 7.8 million Canadians (19%) were over age 65. Increasing longevity combined with a relatively smaller working-age tax base creates a long-term care funding challenge that individual LTC insurance is positioned to help address.
Home care: first tier. Preferred by most seniors. Includes nursing care, companionship, home help (Meals-on-Wheels, housekeeping), personal care (ADLs), overnight stays. Charged by the hour; may not be required daily.
Respite care: provides relief for the primary family caregiver (spouse, child) who provides 24/7 care (e.g., for an Alzheimer's sufferer). Relief may come from a short nursing home stay, adult day care, or community services — helping prevent caregiver burnout while the care recipient remains at home primarily.
Assisted living: transitional facilities for those who can no longer function independently but don't require 24-hour supervision. Offers part-time nursing, assistance with shopping/cleaning/cooking/ADLs. Also called "seniors' residences."
Nursing home (facility) care: 24-hour supervision, 24-hour nursing, on-site medical doctor, full ADL assistance, meals, and group/individual recreation. For those who have lost the ability to live independently. Cost: $900 to $5,000+/month depending on province and type of accommodation (provincial governments subsidize partially based on means testing).

🛡️ Individual LTC insurance: purpose, coverage, and ADLs

Who needs LTC insurance: best prospects are aged 45–55 (younger = very low claim risk; 65+ = risk too high for most insurers to offer). Coverage needed to supplement insufficient government funding, provide choice of care level, and preserve estate assets from being consumed by care costs.
Advantages: minimizes or eliminates financial burden of care costs (protecting the insured AND family estate); allows the insured to access the level of care needed rather than what they can afford; provides dignity and self-control in old age.
Coverage structure: LTC policies provide a maximum daily benefit for qualifying care expenses. Daily maximum × number of care days = overall maximum (e.g., $200/day × 365 days = $73,000 overall). If some days cost less than the daily maximum, benefits extend beyond the stated number of days until the overall maximum is exhausted. Subject to an elimination period (0–90 days); shorter elimination period = higher premium.
Activities of daily living (ADLs) — 6 standard: Dressing (dressing/undressing without assistance); Bathing (washing in tub or shower independently); Toileting (getting to/from toilet independently); Transferring (moving from bed to chair/wheelchair without support device or assistance); Eating (feeding oneself, not just food preparation); Maintaining continence (controlling one's bladder). Inability to independently perform 2 or more ADLs typically qualifies the insured for LTC benefits.
Cognitive impairment test: separately qualifies an insured for LTC benefits. Severe cognitive impairment (inability to understand/communicate written or spoken word or non-verbal cues) due to stroke, heart attack, Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, or dementia — qualifies for benefits regardless of ADL performance. Certification provided by attending physician; may be verified by insurer's physician.
Standard exclusions: war; terrorism; attempted suicide; self-inflicted injuries; normal pregnancy; criminal activities; abuse of non-prescription drugs; AIDS/HIV.

💡 LTC riders, policy benefits, premiums, and tax treatment

COLA rider: automatically increases the maximum daily benefit (typically 2–3% per year) to help keep pace with rising care costs. LTC care is by nature long-term — inflation compounds over years to significantly erode a fixed daily benefit.
Return of premium (ROP) rider: on death of the insured, if the policy has been claims-free, a portion of premiums is refunded (e.g., 50% after 10 years, 100% after 20 years). Cost: adds 25–50% to the basic LTC premium. All ROP benefits are tax-free.
Benefit payment models: Indemnity — benefits paid directly to the care provider (typically for nursing home care). Reimbursement — insured pays for care first, then submits receipts to claim reimbursement (typical for home care). Reimbursement is for the actual amount spent, not the daily maximum if less was spent.
Premiums: generally payable for the life of the contract, with waiver during claim periods. Some insurers offer fixed-term premium payment (e.g., 20 years, allowing policy to be paid up before retirement). LTC policies are generally guaranteed renewable — the insurer can increase premiums but only for a class of policies, not individual policyholders. Grace period: 30 days after premium due date.
Tax treatment: LTC premiums are NOT tax-deductible but MAY qualify for the federal Medical Expense Tax Credit. All LTC benefits received (whether paid as indemnity to a care facility or as reimbursement to the insured) are TAX-FREE.

🔗 Coordinating LTC with DI and CI insurance

LTC vs. DI insurance: no overlap risk. DI replaces lost income (does not cover care expenses). LTC covers care expenses (does not replace income). They serve complementary but distinct needs. Some insurers permit conversion of a DI policy to LTC coverage up to a prescribed age.
LTC vs. CI insurance: potential overlap exists but is difficult to avoid. CI provides a lump sum for any use following a qualifying condition (stroke, heart attack). The consequences of the CI event may or may not impair the insured's ADLs. If they do, both CI and LTC benefits may be payable simultaneously. Since CI proceeds are not restricted in use, no practical offset mechanism exists between the two. Holding both CI and LTC is often appropriate because: CI provides immediate cash flexibility; LTC provides ongoing specific care cost coverage — and neither benefit can be predicted in advance to be redundant.
