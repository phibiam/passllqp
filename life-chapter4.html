Chapter 4 - Universal Life Insurance

This lesson covers everything in depth - transparency through unbundling, the flow of money through a UL policy, premium flexibility, adjusting the face amount, insuring multiple lives, pricing the insurance component (NAAR, YRT, LCOI), death benefit options, investment components and account choices, the investment account's non-forfeiture benefits (surrender, withdrawals, premium offsets, policy loans, partial assignment, leveraging), and when UL insurance is the right fit. Take your time and work through it like a textbook chapter.

🧬 What universal life insurance is

Universal life (UL) insurance is the third type of permanent insurance, evolving out of repeated modifications to whole life insurance - sometimes called "adjustable whole life insurance." UL combines permanent insurance with tax-advantaged investing, within limits, and is considered the MOST FLEXIBLE type of life insurance, since the policyholder can modify the policy in various ways throughout its duration.

2017 reform note: a major reform effective January 1, 2017 changed how tax-advantage parameters are calculated for UL policies issued from that date forward. Policies issued BEFORE that date keep their previously "vested" or "grandfathered" rights - caution is needed with older policies, since certain changes could cause vested rights to be lost under the new rules.

🔓 Transparency through unbundling

With term and guaranteed whole life insurance, the three pricing factors (mortality costs, investments, expenses) are BUNDLED together as a single "cost of insurance" (COI) - the policyholder typically doesn't know what portion of his premium funds which factor. With UL, these three factors are SEPARATED (though still interrelated) - giving the policyholder visibility into exactly how mortality costs and expenses are deducted, and what investment returns are added, to his account.

Why this matters: this transparency encourages the policyholder to pay attention to his policy's activity - ignoring a rising COI without compensating via higher premiums or better investment returns can lead to disastrous policy lapsation.

💧 How money flows through a UL policy

Premiums are deposited to the investment account; investment returns are reinvested in that account; the investment account funds mortality costs and expenses; the death benefit arises from the policy contract; and the policyholder can make withdrawals, partial surrenders, or take loans against the accumulated value.

Cash value (CV) vs. CSV: the cash value (CV) of a UL policy is the value of the investment account itself. It may or may not equal the cash surrender value (CSV), depending on whether surrender charges apply.

⚰️ Mortality costs in a UL policy

Mortality costs (also called the mortality charge) reflect the insurer's cost of covering the risk of death - applicable to all life insurance types, but in a UL policy this cost is deducted DIRECTLY and VISIBLY from the investment account, giving the policyholder some choice in how these costs are calculated (discussed further under pricing the insurance component).

💼 Expenses in a UL policy

The insurer also deducts its own expenses from the UL investment account - selling, underwriting, issuing costs, the insurer's income taxes, and claims investigation/payment. These may be charged as a percentage of the annual premium or as a flat monthly fee, and like mortality costs, the policyholder knows the actual amount.

Trade-off: UL's complexity and flexibility contribute to HIGHER administrative expenses than other forms of permanent insurance.

📈 The investment component

Premiums paid into the investment account support mortality cost and expense deductions, but are designed to leave a residual (cash) value contributing to growth. The policyholder has considerable flexibility over how this value is invested, both initially and over time - investment income accumulates TAX-FREE within the policy as long as limits set by the Income Tax Act aren't exceeded (the "exemption test," covered later).

🏛️ Premium tax on UL premiums

Whenever a premium (sometimes called a "deposit" in sales literature) is paid into a UL policy, the insurer remits a percentage to the provincial/territorial government as premium tax - ranging from 2% to 4% depending on jurisdiction, charged on the ENTIRE premium (not just the portion covering insurance cost). The remainder flows into the investment account.

🎛️ Six dimensions of UL flexibility

UL can be precisely tailored to each client's objectives, both at issue and throughout the policy's life, across: the timing and amount of premiums, adjustment of the face amount, the life/lives insured, the cost of insurance (COI), investment options, and additional riders/options.

💵 Timing and amount of premiums

Within limits, the policyholder decides how much and when to pay. The MINIMUM premium covers premium tax, mortality costs, and expenses - enough to keep the policy in force to age 100, but NOT enough to build cash value. A policy paying only the minimum is "minimally funded" (a significant share of UL policies sold today are minimally funded).

Maximum funding: to take advantage of tax-sheltered growth, the policyholder can pay more than the minimum - at issue, via future annual/monthly payments, or as a lump sum. Most policies cap the maximum premium to preserve tax-exempt status (the exemption test). A policy always paying the maximum is "maximum-funded." The policyholder can also reduce or stop premiums temporarily, as long as the account's cash value can support ongoing mortality/expense deductions.
Worked example - Elaine: minimum premium is $325/month, she currently pays $500/month, with a $2,900 cash value. If she stops premiums entirely during maternity leave, the policy would likely lapse in about 8-9 months ($2,900 ÷ $325), once the account is exhausted by ongoing deductions.

⚠️ Why insufficient account value matters

A UL policy stays in force only as long as the investment account's cash value can support mortality cost and expense deductions. Low cash value (from minimal funding, withdrawals, reduced/stopped premiums, or low/negative investment returns) increases lapse risk.

📅 Modal factors for UL policies

For term and whole life, the modal factor reflects an interest charge for spreading premiums across more frequent payments. For MOST UL policies, there is NO modal factor charge - the premium is simply the annual amount divided by the number of payments per year.

Sample UL modal factors: monthly = 0.0833 (1÷12); semi-annual = 0.50 (1÷2) - meaning the annual and annualized premium are THE SAME for UL, unlike term/whole life where non-annual payment frequencies cost more in total.

📏 Adjusting the face amount

The policyholder selects a face amount at issue (subject to insurer minimums/maximums). Additional coverage can later be ADDED to the existing policy on an attained-age basis - no new policy needed - though any INCREASE requires evidence of insurability (unless a guaranteed insurability rider applies). Coverage can also be DECREASED. Insurers typically restrict increases/decreases to a minimum amount (e.g. $25,000 increase, $10,000 decrease) to limit administrative expense.

Worked example - Richard and Maria: Richard has a $500,000 UL policy; after learning Maria is pregnant with twins, he increases coverage by $500,000 within his existing policy (after proof of insurability) rather than buying a new policy.
Impact on cash value growth: COI is directly linked to the face amount. A face amount INCREASE means higher COI deductions (funding the larger death benefit), leaving less for investment and potentially eroding cash value - the remedy is increasing premiums to compensate. A DECREASE means lower COI deductions, leaving more for investment (potentially boosting cash value) if premiums stay the same.

👪 Insuring multiple lives under one UL policy

A single UL policy can insure multiple lives - joint-life, single-life, or (depending on the insurer) sometimes both within the same contract.

Worked example - Joe and Sherry: Joe holds $500,000 of joint-last-to-die coverage with his wife Sherry (to offset estate tax liability), PLUS $1,000,000 of individual coverage on his own life (to replace lost income for Sherry if he dies first) - all under one policy. UL policies may also allow adding or substituting a new life insured (e.g. Joe later adds his sister Deborah as a new single life insured after she became essential to caring for the children, with Deborah needing to provide her own proof of insurability first).

🎯 Net amount at risk (NAAR)

For a TERM policy, the insurer's net amount at risk is simply the death benefit. For PERMANENT policies (including UL), NAAR = death benefit − value of the investment account. Since the investment account tends to grow over time, it correspondingly REDUCES the insurer's NAAR.

Worked examples - Pratik: Example 1: $500,000 death benefit, $36 investment account value → NAAR = $500,000 − $36 = $499,964. Example 2: $500,000 death benefit, $128,000 account value → NAAR = $500,000 − $128,000 = $372,000. Note: "death benefit" in this formula refers to the FULL amount payable at death, which could exceed the original face amount depending on the death benefit option chosen.

🔁 Yearly renewable term (YRT) costing

YRT is one-year term insurance that renews every policy year - the annual premium for a given year is based on the life insured's risk of death THAT year. COI is usually expressed as a dollar amount per $1,000 of NAAR. Since mortality risk generally increases with age, the YRT cost per $1,000 generally INCREASES each year (just like standalone term insurance premiums).

Worked example - Pratik: with a COI of $18.57 per $1,000 of his $372,000 NAAR: deduction = $18.57 × $372,000 ÷ $1,000 = $6,908 for that policy year.

⚖️ Level cost of insurance (LCOI) costing

LCOI mirrors the premium concept from basic T-100 insurance - the cost per $1,000 generally remains CONSTANT over the duration of the policy, unlike YRT's increasing cost.

🆚 Choosing between YRT and LCOI

Comparing a sample UL policy for a 40-year-old healthy woman: YRT starts much lower than LCOI (e.g. $1.13 vs $7.44 per $1,000 in year 1) and stays lower for roughly the first 25 years, but eventually crosses over and becomes dramatically more expensive (e.g. $109.04 vs $7.44 per $1,000 by year 50) - while LCOI remains constant throughout.

Client goalBetter costing choice
Greater short-term policy fund growthYRT, at least initially - lower early deductions leave more for investment
Longer-term policy valuesLCOI - locks in a level rate for life, avoiding the steep later-year YRT increases
One-way switch: some policies allow switching from YRT to LCOI after issue (with the LCOI rate based on attained age AT the time of the switch), but switching FROM LCOI back TO YRT is generally not permitted.

🔒 Guaranteed vs. adjustable COI

Whether YRT or LCOI costing is GUARANTEED for life, or subject to future adjustment by the insurer (to reflect its actual mortality, investment, and expense experience), depends on the specific policy. Policyholders generally prefer certainty (especially for YRT, which already increases dramatically with age); insurers prefer flexibility to adjust. Agents must understand and clearly disclose which applies.

Open-ended vs. restricted increases: where COI adjustments ARE permitted, open-ended increases create significant policyholder risk. Most adjustable policies instead restrict increases to a cap (e.g. 25%, 50%, or 100% of the original schedule, or a specific dollar limit).

💰 The four death benefit options

A UL policyholder chooses from several death benefit structures, each affecting the NAAR (and therefore COI) differently.

➖ Level death benefit

The simplest, least expensive option - the beneficiary receives either the original face amount, OR the account value if it exceeds the face amount (whichever is greater). For most of the policy's life, only the face amount is paid; the account value only matters much later if it grows large enough to exceed it.

Best suited for: clients who can plan on receiving the death benefit as a minimum guarantee, with potential upside if account value eventually surpasses the face amount.

➕ Level death benefit plus account value

The beneficiary receives the ORIGINAL face amount PLUS the FULL investment account value - the insurer commits to this combined payout from day one. NAAR stays level over time (since the death benefit obligation is fixed at face amount), but COI is HIGHER than a comparable level death benefit policy, drawn from the investment account, which can slow account growth unless deposits increase.

Best suited for: those whose insurance need will increase over time, or who are confident in their own investing skill within the policy, and who deposit larger premiums.

💯 Level death benefit plus cumulative premiums

Generally the MOST EXPENSIVE option - the beneficiary receives the original face amount PLUS the gross amount of every premium ever paid (before mortality/expense deductions) - effectively a full premium refund at death. NAAR = original face amount + cumulative premiums − account value.

Best suited for: policyholders maximizing premiums who want assurance those premiums aren't "lost" to the insurer if death occurs early. Reduces COI and lets the account grow faster - but if account value eventually EXCEEDS face amount plus cumulative premiums, the insurer keeps that excess at death.

📈 Indexed death benefit

The death benefit equals the original face amount, indexed for inflation - either tied to the Consumer Price Index (CPI) or a rate the policyholder chooses at issue (typically 1%-8% annually). NAAR is NOT level under this option - it can increase over time depending on the index rate and the investment account's growth rate, which also affects COI.

Best suited for: covering an end-of-life risk expected to GROW over time, such as a tax liability on appreciating assets.

💸 Net premiums and the exemption test

Whenever a premium is deposited, the NET premium (gross premium minus premium tax, mortality cost, and expense deductions) is what's actually invested. As COI decreases, more becomes available for investment (assuming gross premiums stay constant).

The exemption test: insurers limit net premiums to keep the policy within tax-exempt status (covered in detail in a later chapter). If a policyholder deposits MORE than the maximum allowed net premium, the excess goes to a non-exempt side fund, where investment income IS taxable annually.

⏳ Tax deferral on investment growth

Investment income earned within the investment account is NOT taxable when earned, allowing the full amount to be reinvested and compound (growth builds on growth). Tax on this growth is deferred until the policy is surrendered - and if the death benefit option includes the account value, that growth may NEVER be subject to tax at all.

🎲 Investment choices within a UL policy

Unlike other permanent insurance (where the insurer decides how funds are invested), UL gives the policyholder active control over a wide range of fixed-income and equity choices - he can split premiums across multiple options (subject to minimums) to diversify, and can adjust the mix over time (subject to insurer restrictions) - though this requires genuine investment knowledge to use well.

OptionKey feature
Daily interest accounts (DIAs)Minimum rate tied to a benchmark (e.g. 30-day T-bill yield); absolute floor of 0% - principal guaranteed
Guaranteed investment accounts (GIAs)Like GICs - fixed rate for 1/3/5/10/20-year terms, principal guaranteed, early cash-out usually penalized; proceeds roll over at maturity

⚙️ How investment returns affect policy viability

Though UL unbundles premiums, mortality costs, expenses, and investment, these factors remain INTERRELATED and must stay in balance. If COI increases, more is drawn from the investment account, slowing or even reversing its growth - the remedy is additional premiums or improved investment returns. If returns are lower than expected (or negative, a real risk with index/mutual fund options) and premiums aren't increased to compensate, the policy faces real lapse risk.

Policy illustrations: UL documentation includes projected values for premiums, COI, account value, CSV, and death benefit over future years - NOT guarantees, just illustrations of how the policy works. Because UL outcomes are highly sensitive to even small investment performance changes, most illustrations now show at least two scenarios (current/requested rate, and a slightly lower rate), accompanied by strong disclaimers - policyholders are typically required to read and SIGN the illustration, acknowledging its limitations.

🏦 The investment account's non-forfeiture benefits

The investment account (also called the "accumulating fund") can provide several benefits: surrendering the policy, partial withdrawals, premium offsets, policy loans, partial assignment for third-party loans, leveraging, and distribution upon death.

🚪 Surrendering a UL policy

Upon surrender, the policyholder receives the investment account's value (after converting to cash), less applicable charges. Surrender charges generally apply during the first 10 years, calculated as a multiple/percentage of the annual LCOI insurance charge, gradually decreasing to zero over time - but the charge can never exceed the account's actual value, so a policy with no cash value carries no surrender charge.

Worked example - Fiona: with an LCOI-based annual charge of $500, surrender multiples decline from 2x (year 1) to 0x (year 11+). In year 1, with $200 account value and a 2x multiple ($1,000 charge), her CSV is $0 (since the charge exceeds the account value, capped at zero, not negative). By year 9, with $14,200 account value and a 2x multiple ($1,000 charge), her CSV is $13,200. By year 11, with no surrender multiple remaining, her full $24,400 account value becomes her CSV.
Tax note: surrendering a UL policy will, in most cases, result in a taxable policy gain (covered further in a later chapter).

💳 Policy withdrawals (partial surrender)

A UL policyholder can withdraw funds directly from the investment account - this impedes account growth and may threaten the policy's long-term viability if the remaining balance can't cover ongoing mortality/expense deductions. A withdrawal is treated as a partial surrender and may create taxable income. Insurers may set a minimum withdrawal amount; the maximum is the CSV. Charges may apply depending on contract terms and timing.

Worked example - Andrew: $200,000 level death benefit, $21,000 cash value, $3,800 annual COI. Withdrawing $20,000 reduces his death benefit to $180,000 and cash value to just $1,000 - putting the policy at real risk of lapsing, since there won't be enough to cover the next $3,800 COI deduction unless he deposits additional premiums first.

🔄 Premium offsets in a UL policy

While participating whole life uses DIVIDENDS to offset premiums, a UL policy's investment account naturally offsets premiums through its own investment income and accumulated deposits. If the account grows large enough, it can fund future COI and expense deductions INDEFINITELY - meaning the policyholder can stop paying premiums entirely while keeping the policy in force.

Worked example - Walter: $500,000 UL policy, $10,500 annual LCOI deductions, always maximum-funded with strong investment choices, now has a $460,000 cash value at 65. He could stop paying premiums entirely and the policy would likely remain in force for life - though he should shift his investment mix toward principal-guaranteed options first if pursuing this strategy.

🏧 Policy loans on a UL policy

Similar to whole life, a UL policyholder can typically borrow against the CSV - usually limited to 50%-90% of cash value, with the interest rate set at the time of the loan. No repayment is required, but the outstanding loan plus accrued interest reduces the eventual death benefit. A policy loan may also create taxable income to the policyholder.

Worked example - Joshua: $250,000 level death benefit, $35,600 CSV, takes a $30,000 loan at 4% to help fund his daughter Rebecca's education (also reporting $1,800 in taxable income from the loan, per his insurer). One year later he dies with no payments made: Rebecca receives $250,000 − $30,000 − ($30,000 × 4%) = $218,800.
Loan vs. withdrawal trade-off: a policy loan lets funds continue earning tax-sheltered income within the account (an advantage over a straight withdrawal) - but the loan's interest rate may exceed the actual return earned on those funds.

🤝 Partial assignment for third-party loans

If the policyholder wants access to capital WITHOUT the taxable disposition a withdrawal or policy loan can trigger, he may use the CSV as COLLATERAL for a loan from a third party (typically a bank), which may require additional proof of assets/income. Principal/interest repayment terms vary by arrangement - but critically, the FULL cash value stays inside the policy, continuing to grow tax-sheltered.

Continuing Joshua's example (alternate path): instead of an insurer policy loan, Joshua takes a 5-year bank loan at 4% using his policy as collateral. After one year, he's repaid $6,738 ($5,538 principal, $1,200 interest) before dying. The bank receives $30,000 − $5,538 = $24,462 from the death benefit; Rebecca receives the remaining $250,000 − $24,462 = $225,538 - notably MORE than under the direct policy loan path, since principal had already been partly repaid.

📊 Leveraging

Leveraging means borrowing money to invest - a UL policyholder may obtain a loan (or series of loans) using the CSV or death benefit as collateral. For leveraging to succeed, investment returns must exceed the cost of borrowing; if returns are poor, the policyholder may be unable to repay, diminishing the CSV or death benefit. If principal/interest go unpaid during life, the accrued debt is repaid from the death benefit at death.

Tax treatment: these loans are NOT taxable. If loan proceeds are invested to produce property income (dividends, interest, rents), the accruing loan interest is tax-DEDUCTIBLE to the policyholder.
Worked example - Wendy: $500,000 death benefit, $260,000 CSV, borrows $200,000 at 6.5% using her CSV as collateral, expecting 8% returns from tech start-up investing. Within 10 months, her $200,000 has lost $58,000 combined ($36,000 then $22,000 more), now worth $142,000, plus $13,000 in accrued loan interest - meaning she'd need to deposit $213,000 just to restore her original position, against $71,000 in actual losses. Withdrawing from her policy to pay off the loan would also trigger taxable income, compounding her losses further.
The real danger: if loan value plus accrued interest exceeds the CSV, the lender may demand repayment - if the policyholder has no other funds, he could be forced to surrender the policy entirely, leaving him uninsured AND facing a potential tax liability on the surrender itself.

🧭 When universal life insurance is the right fit

UL is most appropriate for an INVESTMENT-SAVVY policyholder with long-term insurance needs who is also seeking tax-advantaged investment opportunities.

Use caseExample
Maxed-out RRSP/TFSA roomAlistair, 45, has limited RRSP room (defined benefit pension) and has already maxed his TFSA - a maximum-funded UL policy gives him tax-free investment growth plus a $500,000 tax-free death benefit for a scholarship fund, with the option of tax-free loans to support retirement
Tax-free retirement incomeJoan expects to be in the highest marginal tax bracket in retirement and wants income that avoids further taxation - she maximum-funds a UL policy, then takes a series of loans against the CSV during retirement (loan proceeds are tax-free, and since she isn't withdrawing funds, the investment account keeps growing tax-free too)
