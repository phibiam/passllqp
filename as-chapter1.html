Chapter 1 — Financial Protection Provided by A&S Insurance

Accident and sickness (A&S) insurance relieves insured individuals of the burdens of medical expenses or income loss arising from illness, injury, or medical treatment not covered by provincial health insurance plans. This chapter establishes the risks A&S insurance guards against — for individuals, families, and business owners.

📋 What A&S insurance covers

Provincial Insurance Companies Acts define A&S insurance to include: benefits for loss resulting from accidental bodily injury (including death); benefits for loss resulting from sickness or disability; refund of expenses due to sickness or accidental bodily injury; and refund of expenses due to medical treatment of the life insured.

🎯 Financial goals of individuals and families

Most families share three main financial goals that require regular cash flow and a long-term surplus:

Wealth accumulation: after meeting immediate needs (food, shelter, clothing, transportation), surplus income funds an emergency reserve of 3–6 months of expenses, then longer-term wealth goals — investing in a home, building a securities portfolio (registered and non-registered), or creating a legacy for the next generation.
Retirement income: nearly everyone eventually retires. Retirement income comes from CPP/QPP, OAS, employer pension plans, RRSPs, non-registered investments, inheritances, part-time work, or asset liquidation. Almost all of these require years of prior work and saving — both of which can be interrupted by disability. This is where the A&S agent plays a critical protective role.
Family needs: prior to retirement, families must fund ongoing needs — children's tuition, orthodontics, vacations, and support for special-needs children, aging parents, or "boomerang" adult children returning home.

⚠️ Financial risks threatening individuals and families

Unexpected expenses: even the best budget cannot anticipate every cost. A disability can trigger large unplanned expenses (home adaptation, medical equipment, caregiving) that derail the family budget entirely. Example: Emily, a single mother who suffered a stroke, spent $75,000 adapting her home, depleted her savings and RRSP, and was ultimately forced to sell her house.
Loss of income: during working years, income is the foundation of all financial success. Injury or illness causing prolonged disability eliminates the income needed to accumulate savings — even for those with secure employment.
Loss of savings: large unplanned expenses or lost income, or both, force families to draw on savings to meet basic needs. Rebuilding depleted savings can take years and permanently sets back long-term goals.
Lower standard of living: not a risk in itself, but a consequence of other losses — income loss, savings loss, asset loss — creating a "domino effect." Families may need to sell vehicles, downsize housing, or eliminate vacations. Reduced standard of living today can translate into substantially reduced income, assets, and lifestyle in the future — with psychological impacts on ambition and productivity as well.
Inflation: a basket of goods costing $100 in 2005 cost $153.27 by 2025 (53.27% increase). Disability income replacement benefits must be indexed to inflation to retain their buying power, particularly during prolonged disability or high-inflation periods. Canada's inflation was 12.12% in 1982 — a reminder that low inflation is not guaranteed.
Longevity: life expectancy at 65 has increased by approximately 6 years over the past 50 years. In 2020–2022, 65-year-old men were expected to live to 84.34; women to 88. This means retirements lasting 20+ years, requiring substantially more retirement assets than in the past. Nursing home costs can run $5,000/month or higher, with private retirement homes often much more — raising the very real risk of outliving one's money.
Debt: average Canadian household debt reached $1.76 for every $1.00 of disposable income in 2024; total residential mortgage debt reached $3,033.4 billion. Consumer credit cards carry 18–24% interest on unpaid balances. Debt servicing comes from current income — income that may be interrupted by disability or critical illness, potentially leading to lawsuits, foreclosure, and bankruptcy.

📊 Personal risks: disability prevalence and long-term care

Likelihood of disability: in 2022, 1 in 4 Canadians (8 million people) aged 15 and older had at least one activity-limiting disability (Canadian Survey on Disability, 2022). Disability prevalence increases with age: 27% of youth aged 15–24; 24% (4.6 million) of working-age adults 25–64; and 40% (2.5 million) of seniors 65+. Women (30%) have higher prevalence than men (24%) across all age groups. 59% of those with disabilities had mild or moderate disability; 41% had severe or very severe disability.
Loss of independence and long-term care costs: people are living longer with physical conditions (stroke, Parkinson's) and cognitive conditions (Alzheimer's) that leave them unable to care for themselves independently. Long-term care facility costs range from approximately $900 to over $5,000 per month depending on the province and type of accommodation.

🛡️ Needs met by A&S insurance for individuals

Protection for income — disability insurance: replaces a percentage of employment or self-employment income when the insured cannot work due to illness or injury. After a waiting period (during which no benefits are paid), the insurer pays a monthly benefit for a set benefit period or until the insured returns to work. Example: Raoul, a plumber earning $80,000/year, has a policy with a 90-day waiting period, $4,000/month tax-free benefit, and 5-year benefit period.
Protection for savings — extended health insurance: covers unexpected medical expenses (dental, hospital, prescriptions, vision) that would otherwise force the family to liquidate savings. Example: the Johnson family faced $21,250 in unbudgeted expenses — Mrs. Johnson's 8-month disability and her children's medical costs — all covered by disability and extended health insurance, preserving their savings entirely.
Protection for assets — long-term care and critical illness insurance: prevents retirement and estate assets from being consumed by the cost of ongoing care in senior years. Group health benefits typically terminate at retirement, leaving seniors exposed. Example: Eudora, 69, with $250,000 in savings, faced $33,000/year in nursing home costs against $15,000/year in government benefits — a $18,000/year shortfall that would have depleted $290,000 from her estate over 16 years. Her long-term care policy covers $1,000/month ($12,000/year), reducing estate erosion by $96,000 over her lifetime.

🏢 Goals of business owners

Business owners face all individual risks plus additional business-specific risks. Their three primary goals translate directly into personal protection needs:

Business profitability (income protection): the immediate goal is survival, then profitability and growth. Business continuity is a key factor in profitability — a disabled owner-operator can eliminate the revenue the business depends on to pay bills and employees.
Business succession (capital protection): many owners see the business as their legacy, hoping to pass it to children or other successors. Most family-owned businesses do not successfully transition to the next generation, primarily due to lack of planning. A&S insurance ensures the business remains viable and transferable during an owner's disability.
Sale at fair market value (estate protection): when succession within the family is not possible, the owner needs to sell at fair market value — which requires advance planning. An unplanned sale is often a "fire sale" at a fraction of fair value. A buy/sell agreement funded by A&S insurance ensures a disabled owner can obtain a fair price.

⚙️ Business risk and risk management options

Business risk (unsystematic risk): factors specific to a business — including the health of the owner and key employees — that could impact profitability or survival. If the business relies on the owner to generate revenue, a disability of even a few days can threaten the business; prolonged disability can be fatal to it.
Self-funding: three options — pay costs from current cash flow, borrow funds, or erode savings/sell assets. None is truly advantageous: current cash flow is interrupted by disability; lenders may refuse loans when income is at risk; and savings earmarked for other needs are not a reliable source. Self-funding is generally suitable only for businesses with substantial liquid reserves.
Insurance: for a regular premium, funds are guaranteed to be available when needed to replace income or pay expenses caused by disability. Premiums may be "lost" if no claim is ever made, but the premium cost is typically far less than the loss that would occur without coverage. "Better to have it and not need it than to need it and not have it."

💼 Needs met by A&S insurance for businesses

Business overhead expense (BOE) insurance: pays the business's fixed ongoing expenses (rent, utilities, staff salaries, loan payments) while the owner is disabled and cannot work. Example: Ingrid, sole owner of an import/export business, was off work for 7 months after a car accident. Her BOE policy paid up to $15,000/month to cover rent and staff salaries, keeping the business operating until she returned.
Disability buy-sell insurance: funds the purchase of a disabled owner's business interest by the remaining partners or shareholders at a pre-agreed fair price, using legal documentation (a buy/sell agreement) negotiated in advance.
Key person disability insurance: compensates the business for the financial loss caused by the disability of an owner or essential employee, providing funds to hire and train a replacement or offset lost revenue during the transition.
Group employee benefits: A&S insurance is a critical component of employee compensation packages. Employees want income replacement during illness or injury, and health benefits (dental, vision, extended hospital care) to cover medical expenses — needs that are even more important for employees with families. Businesses that offer inadequate benefits risk losing employees to competitors.
