Chapter 5 - Segregated Fund Contract and Annuity Recommendation

This lesson covers everything in depth - Fair Treatment of Customers, fund analysis, the complete recommendation checklists for both seg funds and annuities, MER math, Fund Facts details, and modern practices like virtual meetings and e-signatures. Take your time and work through it like a textbook chapter.

✅ The recommendation process

Every recommendation must follow this sequence: Complete KYC, analyze needs, research suitable products, make and document the recommendation, deliver required disclosures (Fund Facts), then monitor ongoing suitability.

Core principle - Suitability: The recommended product must genuinely fit THIS client's specific situation, goals and risk tolerance. Not just any client. THIS client, right now. Each NEW investment requires a fresh needs assessment - never assume past needs still apply.

⚖️ Fair Treatment of Customers (FTC)

Regulators require agents to treat customers fairly throughout the relationship. FTC means: putting client interests ahead of agent/insurer/intermediary interests; providing accurate, clear product information before, during, and after the sale; avoiding or managing conflicts of interest; minimizing unsuitable sales; ensuring advice is relevant; and handling complaints fairly and promptly.

The reason-why letter: required documentation disclosing the pros and cons of every recommendation, discussed with the client and recorded in client notes - this protects both the client's understanding and the agent's record if the recommendation is ever questioned later.

🔍 Fund analysis and "like-to-like" comparison

Agents must stay current via industry journals, insurer materials, and wholesalers - relying only on old standbys is not adequate. The recommendation must NEVER be based on commission or trailing fees - suitability is the only standard.

Like-to-like rule: compare performance, management, and fees between funds of the SAME type (e.g., balanced income vs. balanced income). If genuinely different fund types are recommended together, the rationale for each type must be explained separately. Third-party sources (e.g., internet research) must be verified - consider compliance officer approval before sharing. Be clear with clients about what is fact vs. agent opinion.

💵 Deposits, funding, and the seg fund recommendation checklist

A complete seg fund recommendation covers: fund identification & characteristics; deposit/funding; value of guarantees; contract maturity date; principal risks; reset; contract riders; fund taxation; sales charges; MER; fund details; and fund penalties.

Deposits are allocated at NAV per unit on the valuation day - the same dollar amount buys different unit counts in different funds based on their NAV, which is NOT an indicator of quality. Lump-sum deposits, transfers (registered→registered, locked-in→locked-in), and periodic deposits each have rules. Periodic deposits can create a SERIES of maturity dates/guarantees, one per deposit or group.

Example: Chris deposits $250/month with a 75%/75% guarantee - each of his 12 monthly deposits is guaranteed at $187.50 ($250×75%) on its own maturity date.

🛡️ Value of guarantees and maturity date disclosure

Maturity guarantee: 75% or 100% of deposits, automatic in all seg fund contracts. If market value exceeds the guarantee, investor gets market value (guarantee is a floor, not a cap). Lost if surrendered early; adjusted (same % but lower base) after a withdrawal.

Death benefit guarantee: also automatic, also 75% or 100% - valuable peace of mind for those in poor health or advanced age.

Maturity date disclosure: always state an ACTUAL date (e.g. "May 16, 2035"), not a vague duration. 100% guarantees often require longer terms (e.g. 15 years vs. the 10-year minimum for 75%).

⚠️ Principal risks and the risk scale

Every contract must display the bold warning: amounts allocated to a seg fund are invested at the contractholder's risk and may rise or fall. Risk arises from how the fund invests; individual investment risks are diversified into an overall fund risk rating.

Risk scale: insurers commonly use a 5-band scale (low, low-to-moderate, moderate, moderate-to-high, high) similar to mutual funds. Bond funds carry high interest rate risk but low market risk; equity funds carry high market risk but low interest rate risk. Even "low" risk funds fluctuate with interest rates and economic conditions.

🔄 Reset feature

Reset lets the investor lock in market value increases at specified intervals (e.g. annually). If value has declined at a reset date, reset simply isn't triggered. Not all contracts offer reset, and the cost may show up as a higher MER.

Key trade-off: utilizing reset extends the maturity date AND increases the guarantees (now based on the new higher value). For automatic-reset contracts, this compounding extension must be discussed - it could push the maturity date well past a client's original planning horizon (e.g. delaying CPP/OAS deferral plans).

📎 Contract riders, taxation, and sales charges

GMWB / GLWB riders: income riders providing guaranteed withdrawal benefits - not widely available, so cross-provider comparison can be difficult.

Taxation: pre-determined by tax law, not a point of recommendation. Registered deposits = contributions (RRSP deductible); non-registered allocations increase ACB; withdrawal taxation differs sharply between registered and non-registered.

Sales charges: front-end load, no load, advisor chargeback, deferred sales charge (DSC) - reviewed in depth in Chapter 2.

💰 MER mechanics and the "real return" calculation

MER = % of expenses relative to average fund assets, charged regardless of performance. Performance is always reported NET of MER (what the investor actually receives).

Real return formula: Real (pre-MER) return = Reported return + MER. Example: Fund A has $10,000 invested, 3.5% MER, 5.0% reported return → real return is 8.5% (5.0%+3.5%); the manager keeps 3.5%, investor gets 5.0%. A higher MER isn't automatically bad - if net (reported) performance is still better than a lower-MER alternative, the investor may rationally prefer it.

📄 Fund Facts and the information folder

The information folder + Fund Facts must be delivered BEFORE the application is signed (in person or electronically); client must acknowledge receipt. Financial statements are NOT bundled with the folder - must be requested separately (annual audited + semi-annual unaudited, for up to 5 years of fund history).

🚫 Fund penalties

Common segregated fund penalties: account closing charge, frequent switching charge, registered account/contract setup fees, short-term trading fee, unscheduled withdrawal/switch fee.

Important: penalties should be disclosed and explained, but should NOT be used as a basis for comparison between recommendations - doing so could imply the agent approves of early withdrawal or surrender, which should always be discouraged given the long-term nature of these products.

🎯 Annuity analysis questions

The Chapter 3 product-feature questions become client-needs questions for the recommendation: Does the client need income or savings? Should coverage extend to a spouse? Is the income need temporary or lifelong? When should income begin? Level or variable income? How will the contract be funded? What is the tax impact?

Honesty when trade-offs exist: it may not be possible to satisfy every client preference. The agent should present the best available option honestly, explaining advantages and shortfalls rather than overselling an imperfect fit.

📝 Annuity recommendation checklist

A complete annuity recommendation covers: type & characteristics; timing of payments; annuity rate; value of guarantees; principal risks; and annuity penalties.

Type: payout (term or life - personal or joint & last survivor) vs. accumulation. A prescribed annuity (equal interest/capital blend each payment) is generally tax-preferable to accrual taxation.

Income bridging: a term annuity aligned to the gap between when someone stops working and when their pension begins - shorter terms mean higher payments. RRSP-funded annuities must begin payments the year following conversion (cannot be deferred) - this rule is specific to RRSP conversions and should not be assumed for other funding sources like a RRIF.

📈 Annuity rates, timing, and guarantees

Fixed-income annuity: equal payments, suited to those without risk tolerance for variability. Variable income annuity: payments rise/fall with markets - age matters since older clients have less time to recover from a downturn. Indexed annuity: payments rise with cost of living, at a significant price premium - always disclose the non-indexed comparison cost.

Guarantee period trade-off: longer guarantee period = lower payment to annuitant but more assurance for a beneficiary if death occurs early. Shorter guarantee period = higher payment but beneficiary risk. Age matters: a 20-year guarantee at age 80 may be impractical. A return of premium guarantee rider pays the full premium to a beneficiary if the annuitant dies before the first payment - useful for poor health, low risk tolerance, or estate-protection goals.

⚠️ Annuity risks and penalties

Principal risk - rising rates: once locked in, an annuitant can't benefit if rates later rise. Laddering (buying a series of annuities over several years) can help manage this risk, though there's no guarantee rates will actually rise.

Inflation risk: managed via indexed annuities, at a cost. Loss of flexibility: the investor gives up control of capital in exchange for income security.

Penalties: payout annuities generally CANNOT be surrendered. Accumulation annuities are generally redeemable, though penalties may apply. As with seg funds, surrender/withdrawal flexibility should never be used as a selling point in a recommendation.

💻 Virtual meetings and e-signatures

Virtual (e.g. Zoom) meetings require reliable technology and connection; a hybrid approach (documents by courier + phone discussion) may suit clients lacking digital comfort, though phone-only loses visual cues. Etiquette norms for virtual meetings should be researched beforehand.

Payment: electronic transfer is preferred; if it exceeds the client's daily limit, arrange courier pickup of a cheque rather than forcing a post-office visit.

E-signature risk: scanned signature, typed name, stylus/touchscreen signature, or click-to-confirm are all valid methods - but relying on e-signatures means the agent can't observe undue influence, duress, fraud, identity theft, or capacity issues directly. Stay alert to red flags and never proceed without being satisfied the client understands. Keep hard copies of meeting notes and documents regardless of how digital the relationship is.
