Chapter 12 - Ongoing Service

This chapter covers the life agent's ongoing role after a policy is issued. Topics include monitoring changing client needs (new dependants, marriage, divorce, employment changes, new mortgage, acquiring a business, leaving Canada); amending a policy (changes requiring vs. not requiring underwriting); renewing a policy (adverse selection and renewal rates); replacing a contract (churning, twisting, and the LIRD disclosure requirement); cancelling and surrendering a policy; policy assignments (absolute and partial/collateral); the claims process (agent's role, proof requirements, probate, APS, beneficiary confirmation); factors that could vary the payment upon death (participating WL dividends, UL options, misstatement of age/gender, collateral assignments, policy loans, unpaid premiums); settlement options; time requirements; and tax treatment of death benefits.

🔄 Monitoring changing client needs

An individual's insurance needs are rarely static. The agent should schedule periodic reviews with existing clients to determine if a change in coverage is warranted. Key life events that may impact insurance needs include: new dependants; marriage or civil union; divorce or dissolution of civil union; employment changes; new mortgage; acquiring a business; leaving Canada; and changes that require an updated needs analysis.

New dependants: when a new dependant (such as a baby) enters the family, the need for life insurance usually increases. The policyholder will want to ensure his estate can provide for that dependant. Typically this means increasing coverage. He may also want to acquire coverage on the life of the new dependant, either as a new policy or by adding a family rider to an existing policy. If a family rider already exists, it usually automatically covers a baby 14 or 15 days following birth.
Marriage or civil union: may prompt: changing the beneficiary designation in an existing policy to the new spouse; and acquiring additional coverage if the new spouse is financially dependent on the policyholder.
Divorce or dissolution of civil union: in MOST Canadian provinces, divorce does NOT automatically revoke the beneficiary designation on a life insurance policy - the policyholder must actively update it. EXCEPTION: in Québec, the Civil Code states that a beneficiary designation made during the marriage or civil union is NULLIFIED upon divorce or dissolution. Regardless, the policyholder should review beneficiary designations upon divorce. If the policyholder is required to pay spousal or child support, the settlement agreement may specify that he must obtain or maintain life insurance for this purpose, and may also specify that the policy cannot lapse and the beneficiary designation must be irrevocable (cannot be changed without consent of the recipient).
Employment changes: key considerations include: the new employer may offer LESS group life insurance than the previous employer (may need additional coverage); there may be an opportunity to convert group life coverage from the previous employer to individual coverage (usually within 30 days after leaving the group plan); any income increase from the job change should be reflected in the coverage amount (if using a human capital approach); and a change in occupation may result in a better lifestyle rating, potentially allowing lower premium rates.
New mortgage: the lender may require life insurance assigned as collateral to cover the debt. Even without this requirement, many families opt to buy enough life insurance to pay off the mortgage upon death, to lessen the financial burden on the surviving family.
Acquiring a business: clients who become business owners may want to buy life insurance to: fund a buy-sell agreement; cover a key employee; protect wealth from business creditors; or secure a business loan.
Leaving Canada: if a policyholder plans to move to another country and cease to be a Canadian resident, it is important to review the policy terms and conditions. Some policies are only valid if the life insured is resident in Canada, or they include an exclusion if death occurs in certain countries.
Updated needs analysis: when meeting with an existing client with changing needs, the agent should review the previous needs analysis and update it to reflect new circumstances. Recommendations may include: modifying the amount of coverage; converting term to permanent insurance; changing beneficiary designations; or adding a life insured or family coverage rider.

✏️ Amending a policy: changes requiring vs. not requiring underwriting

There are two general categories of policy amendments: those that do not require additional underwriting (typically administrative), and those that do.

Changes NOT requiring underwriting (administrative): changing the legal name of the policyholder, life insured, or beneficiaries without changing the parties (e.g., name change due to marriage or divorce); mailing address; beneficiary designations; premium amount (for UL policies); payment frequency; and fund choices. The policyholder can typically request these changes in writing or by filling out a prescribed form. The agent can direct the policyholder to the insurer's website for the necessary forms, or use this as an opportunity to meet the client.
Changes REQUIRING underwriting: adding a life insured; adding a rider or supplementary benefit; increasing coverage; changing smoking status; changing health status or lifestyle category; changing the dividend option on a participating policy to paid-up additions; and changing the type of death benefit (e.g., from level death benefit to level death benefit plus account value).

🔁 Renewing a policy

Renewable policies renew automatically at the end of their term without requiring new evidence of insurability. The term upon renewal is the same as the original term. New premiums upon renewal reflect the life insured's age on the renewal date. Most policies provide a schedule of guaranteed renewal rates when first issued.

Adverse selection at renewal: the guaranteed renewal rate may be significantly HIGHER than rates available on new policies with the same term. This is because the insurer had no way of knowing the life insured's health at renewal time. Based on adverse selection, those whose health has DECLINED are MORE likely to renew (since they may not qualify for new coverage), while those in good health may shop around. As a result, the guaranteed renewal rate reflects the health of the AVERAGE life insured at renewal, not the original health-screened population - making it less favorable than new-issue rates.
Shopping for replacement coverage before renewal: policyholders who are still in good health will often shop around for replacement coverage before renewal, to determine if better rates are available. The key caution: a new contract will include new incontestability (2-year contestability) and suicide exclusion clauses that restart from the date of the new policy. Worked example - Kevin: bought $500,000 T-10 at 35 for $450/year; at renewal (age 45), guaranteed rate is $2,250/year. Kevin (still in good health) applies for a new $500,000 T-10 and gets $770/year - substantially cheaper. He then cancels his existing policy once the new contract is in place.

🔀 Replacing a contract: churning, twisting, and LIRD

Because the life agent earns a commission whenever he places a new contract, there is a potential CONFLICT OF INTEREST whenever a new contract replaces an existing one. The agent's fiduciary duty requires that he only recommend replacing an existing contract when doing so is CLEARLY in the best interests of the client.

Churning: when a life agent deliberately uses misrepresentations, incomplete information, or false statements to convince the policyholder to replace an existing policy with a new one (without a valid reason), or to withdraw cash from an existing policy and use it to buy a new policy - and the existing and NEW policies are with the SAME insurance company. This is a breach of fiduciary duty.
Twisting: the same deliberately deceitful practice as churning, but where the existing policy and the new policy are with DIFFERENT insurance companies.
Life Insurance Replacement Declaration (LIRD): to protect consumers from churning and twisting, most jurisdictions require the policyholder to receive and sign a LIRD. The LIRD must be accompanied by a written explanation specifying: why the policy is being replaced; how the new policy benefits the policyholder; and any situations where benefits under the new policy may not be paid. In Québec, the equivalent document is called the Notice of Replacement of Insurance of Persons Contract.
Key LIRD questions the policyholder must consider: Is the new policy better, and how? Should they just buy more insurance or change the existing policy instead? When should they cancel the existing policy (consider timing of dividends, cancellation charges)? Will cancelling create a tax liability? Does the new policy have the same benefits, riders, and guarantees as the existing one? Are there new contestability periods or suicide exclusion clauses in the new policy? Do premiums go up, and when?
Critical rule: it is absolutely imperative that the policyholder keep the EXISTING policy in force until the new policy has been issued, DELIVERED, and ACCEPTED. Cancelling the existing policy before the new policy is in force creates the risk of being left without any life insurance coverage if the new policy is not issued.

❌ Cancelling and surrendering a policy

A policyholder may cancel a policy because: he no longer needs the protection; he needs a different type of policy; he could obtain a better rate with a new policy; or he can no longer afford the premiums.

Cancelling a term policy: theoretically, a policyholder can cancel a term policy by simply stopping premium payments and letting the policy lapse. However, it is better to cancel in WRITING (either on a form supplied by the insurer or in a letter) specifying: the policy number; the policyholder's name; the life insured's name (if different); and the date coverage should stop. If cancelled between policy anniversaries, the insurance company will generally refund a pro-rated portion of the premiums paid.
Cancelling a whole life or UL policy (surrendering): stopping premiums on a permanent policy may NOT achieve cancellation if the policy has a cash value or investment account from which the insurer can withdraw premiums. The insurer will not withdraw premiums if instructed to cancel. When a policyholder of a whole life or UL policy cancels the contract, it is more commonly called FULLY SURRENDERING the policy - he gives up ALL rights under the contract and no death benefit will be paid after surrender.
Partial surrender: the policyholder can PARTIALLY surrender the policy by making a withdrawal from the policy's cash value. In this case, the contractual rights under the policy continue - a death benefit will still be paid if the life insured dies after the withdrawal (though the death benefit may be reduced). NOTE: fully or partially surrendering a policy can result in taxable income for the policyholder (as discussed in Chapter 7).

📋 Policy assignments: absolute and partial (collateral)

A policyholder can choose, during his lifetime, to assign some or all of his rights under an insurance policy to another party.

Absolute assignment: the policyholder transfers the LEGAL TITLE of the contract (all rights and obligations) to an assignee. Once completed, the original policyholder has NO further control or financial interest in the policy. Common example: a parent who purchased life insurance on a child when young assigns the policy to the child when he becomes an adult. Requirements are typically contained within the policy itself (e.g., the prescribed assignment form must be signed and filed at head office; the assignment is subject to any outstanding debt on the policy). IMPORTANT: an absolute assignment is a DEEMED DISPOSITION for tax purposes and could result in taxable income for the transferor (as discussed in Chapter 7). QUÉBEC EXCEPTION: in Québec, unlike other provinces, the assignment is null and void if the assignee has no interest in the life insured OR the life insured does not consent to the assignment in writing.
Partial assignment (collateral assignment/movable hypothec): in all parts of Canada, using a life insurance policy as security for a loan requires the policyholder to execute a COLLATERAL ASSIGNMENT (called a "movable hypothec" in Québec). This is a partial assignment where the lender does NOT become the owner of the policy - the policyholder still owns it. However, the assignment allows the lender to PREVENT the policyholder from taking any action that would diminish the lender's security (e.g., making a policy withdrawal, failing to remit premiums). If the loan is outstanding when the life insured dies, the lender has FIRST RIGHTS on the death benefit (up to the outstanding loan balance, interest, and accessories) before any amount is paid to the beneficiary. If the policyholder defaults while the life insured is alive, the lender can FORCE a surrender to recover the amounts owed. The policyholder must give a "Notice of hypothecation of a right resulting from the insurance contract" to the insurer for the notice to be enforceable.

📝 The claims process: agent's role and documentation

Once the life insured dies, the beneficiary (or the estate if no other beneficiary) becomes the claimant. The life agent's role is to facilitate the claims process in a timely and sensitive manner - from providing and completing forms to discussing settlement options. The agent may become aware of a death through personal connections, business network, or a newspaper death notice, and can proactively contact the claimant. The claimant may also contact the agent or insurance company directly. The agent should NOT give the impression that he is the one who decides whether, when, or in what amount the claim will be paid - and should not imply payment is guaranteed.

Completed claim form: the agent supplies all required forms to the claimant, helps complete them if necessary, and submits the forms along with any other required documentation to the claims examiner at the insurance company.
Policy status: one of the first things the claims examiner does is confirm the policy was IN FORCE at the time of death. The policy may NOT be in force if: the policyholder allowed the policy to lapse (failed to pay premiums); the policyholder surrendered the policy prior to death; or the policy expired because the term was limited.
Proof of death: an official certification of death is required - usually a provincial death certificate, funeral director's certificate, or coroner's report. The claims examiner uses this to confirm the person who died is the life insured under the policy.
Attending Physician's Statement (APS): the insurance company may request an APS to certify the cause of death or verify medical information provided at application. If the APS reports the life insured died by suicide AND the policy is within the two-year suicide exclusion period (from issue or reinstatement), the claim is DENIED. If the APS reports death from an excluded activity or cause, the claim is also DENIED.
Proof of age and gender: the claims examiner verifies the age and gender of the life insured (usually via birth certificate). If the actual age or gender differs from what was recorded at policy issue, the death benefit may be ADJUSTED (see Factors that could affect payment upon death).
Confirmation of beneficiary: the claims examiner confirms the claimant is the beneficiary entitled to the benefit - consulting the insurer's records, the life insured's will (if applicable), and any other beneficiary designation. If the primary beneficiary predeceased the life insured, proof of the beneficiary's death is required before considering the contingent beneficiary. If there is no designated beneficiary, the estate receives the death benefit. If the policyholder named a CLASS of people (e.g., "all my children"), payment may be delayed while the insurer identifies all qualifying members - which is why specific naming is recommended over class designations.

⚖️ Probate and the claims process

A KEY BENEFIT of life insurance: when a beneficiary other than the estate is named, the death benefit is paid DIRECTLY to the beneficiary WITHOUT going through probate - increasing the value of the death benefit (no probate fees) and speeding up payment.

When probate DOES apply to life insurance: when no beneficiary is named; when no beneficiaries are living to accept the proceeds; when a minor is the heir; or when the estate is named as the beneficiary. In these cases, the claims examiner may request a copy of the probated will. The court issues "letters probate" (or a grant of probate), which verifies the executor's authority to act on behalf of the estate, including signing off on the receipt of insurance proceeds. In the absence of a will, court involvement in administration of estate assets (including insurance proceeds) is required - a lengthy and expensive process.
Québec probate rules: a will executed before a notary is an authentic act and does not need to be probated - the claims adjuster requests a copy of the notarial will and will search certificates. If the will is handwritten or made in the presence of witnesses, it must be probated by a notary or by the Superior Court of Québec. If the life insured died intestate (without a will), the legal heirs are the liquidators of the succession unless they appoint one by majority vote - the claims adjuster requests a declaration of heredity and (where applicable) an act of appointment of a liquidator, obtained from the court or a notary at the succession's expense.

👥 Group life insurance claims

Group life insurance claims are processed similarly to individual claims, except the PLAN SPONSOR (rather than a life agent) facilitates the claims process. The claims examiner confirms that the deceased was covered under the plan - he may NOT be covered if he hadn't yet satisfied the plan's waiting period, or if he had terminated employment prior to death. As noted in Chapter 9, group creditor insurance is usually subject to post-claim underwriting.

💰 Factors affecting payment upon death: participating WL and UL policies

The amount paid upon death could be MORE or LESS than the policy's face amount, depending on several factors.

Participating whole life policies: the dividend payment option chosen affects the death benefit: paid-up additions (PUAs) PERMANENTLY increase the death benefit with each dividend; the accumulation option MAY increase the death benefit if funds aren't withdrawn; the term insurance option TEMPORARILY increases the death benefit for the duration of the one-year term only. The death benefit would be DECREASED by: use of the automatic premium loan (APL) or CSV; or an outstanding collateral assignment.
Adjustable whole life policies: the insurance company usually guarantees the death benefit and premiums for a certain period (e.g., five years). At the end of that period, the insurer may increase, decrease, or maintain the death benefit depending on its experience.
Universal life policies: UL has several death benefit options: level death benefit; death benefit plus account value; death benefit plus cumulative premiums; indexed death benefit. Depending on the option chosen, deposits made, and investment returns earned, the amount paid upon death can EXCEED the original face amount.

📐 Misstatement of age: adjustment formula and fraud

If the insurance company determines at the time of the claim that the life insured misstated his age on the application, the company can VOID the policy and deny the claim ONLY if it can prove the misstatement was FRAUDULENT (even if the two-year contestability period has expired). A FRAUDULENT misstatement of age can be acted on at any time.

If the misstatement was NOT fraudulent: the insurance company will pay the death benefit, but adjusts the amount to reflect the coverage that the premiums paid would have purchased had the correct age been known. Formula: adjusted death benefit = (premium actually paid ÷ premium that should have been paid) × face amount. A misstatement of age does NOT make the policy void; the amount is simply adjusted.
Worked example - Paul: Paul (actually age 40) applied for a $100,000 T-10 and listed his age as 45. Annual premium at 45 = $324; correct premium at 40 = $230. Paul died 4 years later. Adjusted death benefit = ($324 ÷ $230) × $100,000 = $140,870. Since Paul had stated an older age (and therefore paid MORE than he should have), the insurer would actually pay MORE than the face amount. This illustrates why the agent should double-check the life insured's date of birth prior to completing delivery.
Important note from footnote: the insurer could seek to nullify the policy if the stated age placed the life insured outside the insurer's age boundaries at issue. This nullification must occur within a certain time period following policy issue (varies by provincial legislation), during the lifetime of the insured, and within 60 days of discovery of the error.

👤 Misstatement of gender: adjustment and fraud

If the insurance company discovers a false statement about the insured person's gender: it can void the policy and deny the claim if the contract has been in force for LESS THAN two years; or if the contract has been in force for TWO YEARS OR MORE, it can still void the policy if it can prove the misstatement was fraudulent. Usually when gender is misstated, the insurer adjusts the premium accordingly. When the discovery is made at the time of death, it withholds the appropriate premium difference from the benefit payable.

Worked example - Leslie: Leslie (male, age 35) bought a $100,000 T-10. His gender was entered as female, so he paid $62/year (female rate) instead of $80/year (correct male rate). He died three years later. Adjusted death benefit = ($62 ÷ $80) × $100,000 = $77,500. Since Leslie paid LESS than he should have (female rates are lower than male rates), the insurer pays LESS than the face amount. The agent should double-check the life insured's gender on the policy prior to delivery.

🔗 Policy loan, collateral assignment, and unpaid premium effects on death benefit

Three additional factors can reduce the death benefit paid upon death.

Policy assigned as collateral: if the policy was assigned as collateral to secure a loan, the insurer pays the outstanding loan balance to the creditor FIRST, and only pays the remaining portion to the designated beneficiary or estate. Worked example - Zachary: $500,000 policy assigned for a $300,000 business loan. Outstanding balance at death = $230,000. Insurer pays $230,000 to bank and $270,000 to Deborah (wife and beneficiary).
Outstanding policy loan: if the policyholder took out a policy loan against CSV and a balance is outstanding at death, the loan balance PLUS ACCRUED INTEREST is deducted from the death benefit. Worked example - Marva: $500,000 whole life policy; took $100,000 policy loan 3 years ago at 4.5% compounded annually, no payments made. Outstanding balance = $100,000 × (1.045)³ = $114,117. Death benefit paid to beneficiary = $500,000 – $114,117 = $385,883.
Unpaid premiums (within grace period): if the policyholder dies after missing a premium payment but during the grace period, the death benefit IS paid, but REDUCED by the amount of the unpaid premium. Worked example - Joshua: $250,000 term policy, annual premium $3,300. Missed premium, died within grace period. Death benefit paid = $250,000 – $3,300 = $246,700.

💡 Settlement options, time requirements, and tax treatment

The claimant or estate's executor may not be aware that alternatives to a lump sum death benefit exist. The life agent can provide assistance by discussing settlement options and providing quotes. Options include: a term certain annuity (monthly or annual payments for a fixed period); or a life annuity (payments for life). These are discussed in more detail in the Segregated Funds and Annuities module.

Time requirements for filing a claim: there is NO actual time limit for filing a life insurance claim - policies may be discovered years after the death of the life insured. As long as the policy was valid at the time of death, the death benefit will be paid (including interest). Processing time varies: once filed, a straightforward claim could be processed within days; complex cases (e.g., suspected suicide within the exclusion period) may take several months. Once all documents are received and the investigation is complete, the insurance company MUST pay the insured amount within 30 DAYS.
Tax treatment of death benefits: the death benefit from a personally-held life insurance policy is NOT taxable income for the designated beneficiary - regardless of how long the policy was in place or how much the policyholder paid in premiums. The full amount paid out is tax-free (including, for UL policies offering death benefit plus account value, the original face amount plus the investment account value). Group life insurance death benefits are also paid out TAX-FREE, regardless of whether the life insured or his employer paid the premiums. For corporate-owned life insurance, the death benefit received by the corporation is tax-free; if the corporation is a Canadian private corporation, some or all of the death benefit is credited to the Capital Dividend Account (CDA), and the Board of Directors can direct payment of a tax-free special dividend from the CDA to shareholders - preserving the tax-free nature of the death benefit.
